Weather is not random. It is the Sun heating an uneven planet, air and water carrying that heat around, and moisture condensing along the way. The 18 chapters form one machine in three layers. How the ideas connect: follow the air

Read the map as one chain from the Sun to a storm. The Sun pours energy onto the planet (Chapter 2); that energy moves as heat by radiation, conduction, and convection (Chapter 3) and sets the temperature of the air near the ground (Chapter 4). Uneven heating makes uneven pressure (Chapter 5), and air flowing from high pressure to low, bent by the spin of the Earth, is wind (Chapter 6). That is the whole engine in one line: sun to heat to temperature to pressure to wind.

Now add water. Warm air holds more moisture (Chapter 7). When air rises it cools without swapping heat with its surroundings, which is an adiabatic process (Chapter 8), and whether a rising parcel keeps going or sinks back is its stability (Chapter 9). Rising, cooling, moist air condenses into clouds (Chapter 10), and when the droplets grow heavy enough they fall as precipitation (Chapter 11). That is the second chain: humidity to adiabatic to stability to clouds to precipitation.

Zoom out and the same pieces assemble into big systems. Large bodies of similar air are air masses, and where they meet are fronts (Chapter 12). The global pattern of heating drives the circulation cells and jet streams that steer everything (Chapter 13). Traveling lows become mid-latitude cyclones (Chapter 14), and when the ingredients are extreme you get severe weather: thunderstorms and tornadoes (Chapter 15), then hurricanes, floods, and winter storms (Chapter 16). Third chain: air masses to fronts to cyclones to severe weather.

Finally, climate (Chapter 17) is not a fourth kind of weather. It is the long-run statistics of everything above: what you expect over decades rather than what you get tomorrow. And tools and forecasting (Chapter 18) close the loop, teaching you to read the pressure, fronts, and systems off a real map and predict what comes next.

Where this connects outward

Meteorology is one room in a larger building, and the purple ovals on the map are the doors. Heat, work, and the behavior of rising air run on physics and thermodynamics, the ideal-gas law and energy conservation underneath Chapters 3 and 8. The ocean is the planet's giant heat reservoir, so oceanography sits right behind climate and hurricanes (Chapters 16 and 17): currents move heat, and warm water fuels tropical storms. And turning today's map into tomorrow's forecast is the job of climate science and numerical modeling, where computers solve the same fluid equations across the whole atmosphere (Chapters 17 and 18). You do not need those fields to do well here, but knowing the doors are there is where the subject keeps going.

The foundation and core-weather chapters (Layers 1 and 2) are the everyday-weather core, so weight your study there this year. The whole map stays useful, because the focus rotates and the same basics underlie all three.

1. The Atmosphere

The air is a thin blanket of gas held to the planet by gravity. Almost everything we call "weather" happens in its bottom layer.

🌦️ Meet our running character, Parcel P. Picture one small blob of air, about the size of a hot-air balloon, floating over Kansas City (near 39 degrees north, in the middle of the Great Plains). We will call it Parcel P, and we will follow it all the way up this map: the ground warms it, it gets pushed by pressure, it fills with water vapor, it rises, cools, and one day grows into a thunderstorm. Right now, in Chapter 1, all you need to know about P is where it lives: down in the lowest layer of the air, the part where weather happens.
Prerequisites: none. This is the entry point of the guide.
This chapter: what the atmosphere is made of, and its four layers.
Unlocks: Chapter 2: Energy from the Sun · Chapter 5: Air Pressure

Start here: a balloon ride up from Kansas City

Before any definitions, watch what actually happens when you send a weather balloon straight up from Kansas City and read its two most basic instruments at each height: a thermometer (temperature) and a barometer (air pressure, the weight of the air pressing in from all sides).

Observation 1: the first 12 kilometers. The balloon leaves the ground on a mild day at about +15 °C, with the air pressing on it at roughly 1013 hPa (that is "one atmosphere" of pressure; hPa means hectopascal, the standard pressure unit). As the balloon climbs, both readings drop together:

  • At 1.5 km: about +5 °C, and about 845 hPa.
  • At 5.5 km: about minus 20 °C, and about 505 hPa. Notice the pressure is already about half of its surface value.
  • At 10 km (where jet airliners cruise): about minus 50 °C, and about 265 hPa.
  • At 12 km: about minus 57 °C, and about 195 hPa. This is roughly the top of the weather-making layer.

So through the whole climb, both temperature and pressure fall. That matches everyday life: mountaintops are cold, and it is hard to breathe high up because the air is thin.

Observation 2: the surprising turn. Suppose the balloon could keep rising past 12 km. The pressure keeps falling smoothly, just as before. But the temperature does something unexpected: it stops falling and starts rising. Climb higher still, past about 50 km, and it turns around and falls again. Past about 85 km, it rises once more. Temperature does not simply keep getting colder forever.

Name the pattern

Two very different things happen as you go up, and telling them apart is the key to this whole chapter:

  • Pressure always falls with height, smoothly, and it never reverses. (Observation 1.)
  • Temperature zig-zags: it falls, then rises, then falls, then rises. (Observations 1 and 2.)

Each place where the temperature switches direction marks the start of a new region of the air. Scientists use exactly these turns to divide the atmosphere into four layers. The rest of the chapter explains why pressure only ever falls (gravity), why temperature turns where it does (each layer is heated in a different way), and what the air is actually made of.

The core idea: a thin skin of air, held by gravity

Earth is wrapped in a layer of gas we call the atmosphere. Gravity holds it down, the same way gravity holds you in your chair, so the air does not drift off into space. Because gravity pulls the gas toward the ground, the air is densest near the surface and gets thinner and thinner the higher you go, until it fades into space with no sharp edge. This is exactly why the balloon's barometer kept falling: near the ground you sit under almost the whole weight of the air above you, and the higher you go, the less air is still overhead to press down.

It is thinner than it looks. About 99 percent of all the air sits within roughly 30 km of the ground. Earth is about 6,371 km in radius. So compared with the whole planet, the atmosphere is a very thin skin, thinner in proportion than the skin on an apple. Everything in this whole guide, every cloud and storm and front, happens inside that thin skin.

Four layers, sorted by how temperature changes with height

We just watched the temperature zig-zag on the way up: it falls, then rises, then falls, then rises. Each time the temperature switches direction, you have crossed into a new layer. That is the whole rule for naming the four layers. From the ground up:

  • Troposphere (0 to about 12 km): temperature falls with height. All weather happens here. This is Parcel P's home, and it is the layer the balloon climbed through above.
  • Stratosphere (about 12 to 50 km): temperature rises with height. It holds the ozone layer, which soaks up harmful rays from the Sun.
  • Mesosphere (about 50 to 85 km): temperature falls again. This is the coldest part of the atmosphere. Most meteors burn up here.
  • Thermosphere (about 85 km and up): temperature rises again, and can get very high. The air here is so thin, though, that it would still feel freezing to you.

The turns are not random. A layer's temperature trend is set by where its heat comes from: the troposphere is heated from below (by the sun-warmed ground) so it cools as you climb away from that heat, while the stratosphere is heated from within (its ozone absorbs the Sun's ultraviolet rays) so it warms as you climb. Worked Example 2 walks through this.

Aha: the layers are not defined by "how high" for its own sake. They are defined by which way the temperature is going. Down low it falls, so hot-air balloons and mountaintops are cold. Higher up it flips and rises, because a different layer is being heated in a different way.

What the air is made of

Dry air (air with the water vapor taken out) is almost the same everywhere on Earth, because winds keep it well mixed. By count of molecules it is:

  • Nitrogen (N₂): about 78%
  • Oxygen (O₂): about 21%
  • Argon (Ar): about 1% (0.93%)
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂): about 0.04%, plus tiny traces of other gases

So two gases, nitrogen and oxygen, make up about 99 percent of dry air. Notice how small carbon dioxide is: only about 4 molecules in every 10,000. It is a tiny part of the air, yet it matters a lot for climate, which is a story for Chapter 17: Climate.

Water vapor is the special one. Water vapor is not in the list above, because we listed dry air. Real air also carries water vapor, and that amount is variable: it swings from almost 0 in a cold, dry place up to about 4% by volume in warm, muggy air. Water vapor is the single most important ingredient for weather, because it is what turns into clouds and rain. Our Parcel P over Kansas City is humid, so it carries a good dose of it.

Worked examples

Example 1. A weather balloon carries Parcel P from the ground up to a cruising airliner's height, about 11 km. Is P still in the troposphere? Is it getting warmer or colder on the way up?

The troposphere runs from 0 to about 12 km. Since 11 km is below 12 km, P is still in the troposphere the whole trip. Inside the troposphere, temperature falls with height. So P gets steadily colder as it rises. Near the surface it might be about 15 degrees C, and near the top of the troposphere the air is roughly minus 57 degrees C.

Aha: "higher equals colder" is only the troposphere rule. It is true for the layer we live in, which is why mountaintops have snow, but it stops being true once you climb into the stratosphere.

Example 2. Why does the temperature stop falling and start rising in the stratosphere? What is different about that layer?

The troposphere is heated from below. Sunlight passes through the air and warms the ground, and the warm ground heats the air touching it. So the air is warmest at the bottom and cools as you go up, away from the heat source.

The stratosphere is heated from inside. It holds the ozone layer, and ozone soaks up energetic rays from the Sun (ultraviolet light) directly. That absorbed energy warms the stratosphere, and the warming is strongest in the upper part where the sunlight hits first. So in the stratosphere the air gets warmer as you go up.

Aha: a layer's temperature trend is a fingerprint of how it gets its heat. Heated from below means it cools going up. Heated from within (by ozone) means it warms going up.

Example 3. Out of every 10,000 molecules in a sample of dry air near Kansas City, about how many are nitrogen, and about how many are carbon dioxide?

Nitrogen is about 78% of dry air. Of 10,000 molecules, that is \[ 0.78 \times 10{,}000 = 7{,}800 \text{ nitrogen molecules.} \] Carbon dioxide is about 0.04%. That is \[ 0.0004 \times 10{,}000 = 4 \text{ carbon dioxide molecules.} \]

Aha: a percent is just "how many out of 100." Scaling to 10,000 makes the tiny amount of CO₂ easy to picture: 4 molecules out of 10,000. Small in number, big in effect.
Common pitfalls:
  • Thinking weather happens all through the sky. Nearly all weather (clouds, rain, storms, fronts) is stuck in the troposphere, the bottom layer. The layers above are calm and clear.
  • Thinking it keeps getting colder forever as you go up. That is only true in the troposphere. In the stratosphere it warms with height, in the mesosphere it cools again, in the thermosphere it warms again.
  • Mixing up the ozone layer with the "greenhouse" story. The ozone layer (up in the stratosphere) blocks harmful ultraviolet rays coming in. The greenhouse effect (down here) is about carbon dioxide trapping heat trying to leave. The "ozone hole" is a different problem from carbon-dioxide warming. Chapter 17 keeps them straight.
  • Listing water vapor as a fixed percent. The 78 / 21 / 1 split is for dry air. Water vapor is on top of that and changes constantly.

Practice

P1. A tornado, a rain shower, and a snowstorm are all forms of weather. Which layer of the atmosphere do they all happen in?

AnswerThe troposphere, the bottom layer (0 to about 12 km). Almost all weather is confined there.

P2. As you go up through the troposphere the air gets colder, but as you go up through the stratosphere the air gets warmer. Explain why the trend flips between these two layers.

AnswerThe two layers get their heat in different ways, so their temperature trends point in opposite directions.

Why it works: The troposphere is warmed from below. Sunlight heats the ground, and the ground heats the air touching it, so the air is warmest at the bottom and cools as you climb away from that heat source. The stratosphere is warmed from within: its ozone layer soaks up ultraviolet rays from the Sun directly, and that absorption is strongest higher up where the sunlight arrives first, so the air there gets warmer with height. Two different heat sources, two opposite trends.

Pattern (read the temperature trend as a heat clue): whenever a layer cools with height, suspect it is heated from below (like the troposphere). Whenever it warms with height, suspect something inside it is absorbing energy (like ozone in the stratosphere, or, higher still, the very thin thermosphere soaking up the Sun's most energetic rays). The zig-zag in the figure is really a map of where the heat goes in.

P3. A student says, "Air is mostly oxygen, since that is the part we breathe." Correct them using the composition of dry air.

AnswerOxygen is only about 21% of dry air. The biggest part is nitrogen, at about 78%. Together they are about 99% of dry air. (Argon is about 1% and carbon dioxide only about 0.04%.) So air is mostly nitrogen, even though oxygen is the part our bodies use.

P4. Rank these from lowest to highest above the ground: mesosphere, troposphere, stratosphere, thermosphere. Then say which one Parcel P lives in.

AnswerLowest to highest: troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere. Parcel P lives in the troposphere, the bottom layer, where all the weather is.

Teaching notes

Opener (examples-first): start with the balloon ride, not the definitions. Put the five altitude readings on the board (surface, 1.5, 5.5, 10, 12 km) and let the class notice that BOTH temperature and pressure fall. Then drop the twist: past 12 km the temperature turns around and rises. Only after they have seen the zig-zag do you name the layers. This "observe first, define second" order is what makes the layers stick.

The apple-skin hook: ask the class to guess how thick the atmosphere is compared with the Earth. Most guess way too thick. Then reveal the apple-skin comparison (99 percent of the air within about 30 km of a 6,371 km planet). That "it is thinner than you think" moment makes the whole subject feel fragile and worth understanding.

Board move: draw the zig-zag temperature profile big, and build the four layers by asking "which way is the line going now?" at each turn. Do not lead with heights. Lead with the falls/rises pattern, then hang the approximate heights on it. This is the one visual that makes the layers make sense.

What to name: the four layers, the 78 / 21 / 1 / 0.04 split of dry air, and "water vapor is variable." Introduce Parcel P here so students meet the character before it starts doing physics in later chapters. Name the ozone layer as a landmark, but do not teach the chemistry.

Check for understanding: "In which layer does weather happen?" (troposphere). "Do mountaintops being cold agree with the troposphere rule?" (yes, temperature falls with height there). "Does the pressure ever turn around like the temperature does?" (no, pressure only ever falls). "Is the ozone hole the same thing as global warming?" (no, keep them separate). If they conflate ozone and greenhouse warming, slow down and separate the two on the board: ultraviolet coming IN versus heat trying to get OUT.

Pacing: this is a foundation chapter and should move briskly. Spend most of the time on the three figures (the balloon ascent, the composition bar, and the layer profile) and the idea that a layer's temperature trend tells you how it is heated. Save real thermodynamics for Chapters 2 through 4.

2. Energy from the Sun

Almost every bit of weather starts as sunlight. Where the Sun heats the ground hardest, the air stirs the most.

Start with what you can see

The Sun is the engine behind all weather. Sunlight warms the ground, the ground warms the air, and warm air rising while cool air sinks is what we feel as wind, clouds, and storms. Turn the Sun off and the weather stops. But before naming a single law, look at three plain facts about heat on Earth. Each one is a clue, and together they point straight at the physics.

Observation 1: the equator bakes, the poles stay frozen

Stand on the equator at noon and the Sun is almost straight overhead. Stand near a pole and the Sun never climbs far above the horizon, even in summer. The tropics are hot all year and the poles are frozen all year. It is the same Sun for both. The only thing that differs is the angle at which its light strikes the ground.

Picture a flashlight beam. Point it straight down at the floor and you get a small, bright circle. Tilt the flashlight so it hits at a slant and the same light smears into a big, dim oval. The beam still carries the same amount of energy. It is just spread over more floor, so each square meter gets less. Sunlight works the same way: near the equator it lands concentrated on a small patch of ground (strong heating), while near the poles the same beam spreads over a larger, slanting patch (weak heating).

Observation 2: Kansas City is hot in July and freezing in January

Same city, same spot on the map, wildly different weather six months apart. Kansas City sits at about 39 degrees north. At noon on the longest day of the year (late June) the Sun climbs to about 74 degrees above the horizon, nearly overhead. At noon on the shortest day (late December) it reaches only about 27 degrees, low and slanting. The high summer Sun piles its light onto a small patch of ground; the low winter Sun smears the same light thin. That is the very angle rule from Observation 1, now playing out at a single place across the year.

It is tempting to blame the distance to the Sun, but that is backwards. Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January, in the dead of Northern winter, and farthest in early July. Something other than distance must be at work. The figure below shows what: Earth's tilted axis keeps pointing the same way in space all year, so which hemisphere leans toward the Sun changes with the seasons.

Observation 3: Earth's temperature barely changes from year to year

Sunlight pours onto the planet every single day, yet Earth's average temperature holds roughly steady over the years. If energy only came in and nothing left, the planet would heat up without limit. It does not. So Earth must be shedding energy back to space at almost exactly the rate it takes it in. The energy coming in must balance the energy going out.

The pattern

Two ideas run through all three observations:

  • Angle sets intensity. What heats a surface is not how bright the Sun is but how square-on its light lands. A high Sun concentrates energy onto little ground; a low Sun spreads it thin. This is the equator versus the poles (Observation 1) and summer versus winter (Observation 2).
  • In balances out. Earth's temperature is set by a balance between the energy it absorbs from the Sun and the energy it radiates back to space (Observation 3).

Everything else in this chapter is the machinery behind these two ideas.

The mechanism: shortwave in, longwave out

Insolation and the angle rule

The energy arriving at the top of the atmosphere from the Sun is called insolation (short for "incoming solar radiation"), measured in watts per square meter (W/m²). The angle rule from Observations 1 and 2 is exactly this: the more slanted the Sun, the more area a fixed beam covers, so the fewer watts land on each square meter. Angle, not brightness, decides the heating.

Two kinds of light: solar shortwave in, terrestrial longwave out

The Sun and the Earth both glow, but at very different wavelengths, because the Sun is far hotter. The blazing Sun (about 5,500 degrees C at its surface) radiates mostly as shortwave radiation: visible light, plus some ultraviolet and near-infrared. The much cooler Earth (a global average near 15 degrees C) radiates as longwave radiation: invisible thermal infrared, the same kind of heat glow you feel coming off warm pavement at night. Hotter objects glow at shorter wavelengths, which is why the two flows sit in different parts of the spectrum. So the energy coming IN is shortwave (solar) and the energy going OUT is longwave (terrestrial). Keeping these two straight is the key to the whole energy budget.

Albedo: the fraction reflected

Not all the incoming shortwave gets absorbed. Some bounces straight back to space without heating anything. The fraction a surface reflects is its albedo. Bright, light-colored surfaces have high albedo: fresh snow reflects about 80 to 90 percent of the sunlight hitting it, and thick clouds reflect a lot too. Dark surfaces have low albedo: the open ocean reflects only about 10 percent and green forest about 15 percent, so they absorb most of what lands on them. That is why a snowfield stays cold and a dark parking lot bakes. Averaged over the whole planet, Earth reflects roughly 30 percent of the incoming sunlight and absorbs the other 70 percent.

Energy balance sets the temperature

Now Observation 3 has a mechanism. Earth absorbs the shortwave sunlight it does not reflect, warms up, and radiates longwave infrared back to space. Warmer objects radiate more strongly, so if Earth absorbed more than it emitted it would warm until the outgoing longwave rose to match the absorbed shortwave. The temperature settles at the point where absorbed shortwave equals emitted longwave. This balance, called Earth's energy budget, is what fixes the planet's temperature.

The greenhouse effect (a preview)

Certain gases in the air, mainly water vapor and carbon dioxide, are nearly transparent to incoming shortwave sunlight but absorb outgoing longwave infrared and re-radiate some of it back down. This traps heat near the surface and warms the lower atmosphere: the greenhouse effect. Notice it acts on the OUTGOING longwave, not on the incoming sunlight. The full story is in Chapter 17: Climate.

Seasons come from the tilt, not the distance

Observation 2 hinted that distance is not the cause of the seasons. Here is the real one. Earth spins on an axis that leans over by about 23.5 degrees, and that axis keeps pointing the same way in space all year as Earth orbits the Sun. Around June the North Pole leans toward the Sun, so in the Northern Hemisphere the noon Sun rides high and the days are long: summer. Around December the North Pole leans away, the noon Sun stays low and the days are short: winter. The tilt changes both the Sun's angle and the length of the day, and both push the same way. Because one hemisphere leans in while the other leans out, the two hemispheres always have opposite seasons.

Worked examples

Example 1. A bundle of sunlight carries 1000 watts of power. When the Sun is straight overhead the bundle lands on 1 square meter of ground. When the Sun is low in the sky the same bundle is spread over about 2 square meters (the beam picture in Observation 1). How much energy does each square meter receive in the two cases?

Energy per square meter is just the total power divided by the area it covers.

  • Sun overhead: \(1000 \text{ W} \div 1 \text{ m}^2 = 1000 \text{ W/m}^2\).
  • Sun low: \(1000 \text{ W} \div 2 \text{ m}^2 = 500 \text{ W/m}^2\).

The low Sun delivers only about half the energy to each square meter, even though the beam carries the exact same power. This is Kansas City's summer-to-winter swing in one line: the noon Sun climbs to about 74 degrees above the horizon in June (concentrated) but only about 27 degrees in December (spread thin).

Aha: the Sun does not get "stronger" at the equator or in summer. The same sunlight simply lands more square-on, so it piles onto less ground. Angle, not brightness, is the story.

Example 2. About 340 W/m² of shortwave sunlight arrives at the top of the atmosphere, averaged over the whole globe. Earth reflects about 30% of it straight back to space. How much does the planet absorb, and how much longwave must it radiate back out to stay at a steady temperature? (See the budget arrows in Observation 3.)

  • Reflected (albedo): \(30\% \text{ of } 340 \approx 100 \text{ W/m}^2\) bounced back, absorbing nothing.
  • Absorbed: \(340 - 100 = 240 \text{ W/m}^2\) soaked up by the ground, ocean, and air.
  • Radiated back out as longwave infrared: for a steady temperature, "out" must equal "in," so about \(240 \text{ W/m}^2\) leaves as infrared.
Aha: the incoming sunlight (shortwave) and the outgoing infrared (longwave) are different kinds of light. Their balance is what fixes the planet's temperature, and the greenhouse effect warms us by slowing down the OUTGOING side.

Example 3. It is January in Kansas City and it is freezing. Meanwhile it is summer in Australia. If seasons came from Earth being closer to or farther from the Sun, both places should feel the same season at the same time. Why are they opposite? (The tilt diagram is in Observation 2.)

Because seasons are not about distance at all. They come from the 23.5 degree tilt of Earth's axis, which keeps pointing the same way in space all year as Earth orbits the Sun.

  • Around June: the North Pole leans toward the Sun. In the Northern Hemisphere the noon Sun rides high (about 74 degrees up at Kansas City) and the days are long, so sunlight is concentrated and lasts many hours. That is summer here. At the same moment the South Pole leans away, so Australia has winter.
  • Around December: the North Pole leans away from the Sun. The noon Sun stays low (about 27 degrees up at Kansas City) and the days are short, so heating is weak and brief. That is winter here, and summer in Australia.

The tilt changes two things at once: the angle of the noon Sun (how concentrated the sunlight is, exactly the beam idea from Example 1) and the length of the day (how many hours the Sun shines). Both push the same way, which is why summer and winter feel so different.

Aha: the giveaway is that the two hemispheres have opposite seasons at the same time. A distance change would cool the whole planet at once. Only a tilt can make one half warm while the other half is cold.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: "Summer happens because Earth is closer to the Sun." This is the single most common mistake in this whole topic, and it is backwards. Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January, which is winter in the Northern Hemisphere, and farthest in early July. The whole orbit changes the distance by only about 3%, far too little to matter. Seasons come from the 23.5 degree axial tilt, which changes the Sun's angle and the length of the day.
Pitfall 2: mixing up incoming and outgoing energy. Sunlight arrives mostly as shortwave (visible) radiation. Earth loses energy back to space as longwave (infrared) radiation. They are two different flows, and the greenhouse effect (Chapter 17) acts on the outgoing longwave, not on the incoming sunlight.

Practice

P1. Explain in one or two sentences why the equator is warmer than the North Pole all year round.

AnswerNear the equator the Sun is high overhead, so its energy lands concentrated on a small area (strong heating). Near the pole the Sun is always low, so the same energy is spread over a much larger, slanted area (weak heating). The slanted light also passes through more atmosphere on the way down.

P2. It is January in Kansas City and freezing cold. A classmate says, "Winter is cold because Earth is farther from the Sun right now." Is your classmate right? Explain what is actually going on.

AnswerNo, that is not right, and it is actually backwards. Earth is closest to the Sun in early January (about 147 million km) and farthest in early July (about 152 million km). Across the whole orbit the distance changes by only about 3%, which is far too small to make a season. The real cause is Earth's 23.5 degree axial tilt. In January the North Pole leans away from the Sun, so at Kansas City the noon Sun is low (about 27 degrees up) and the days are short, giving weak heating. In July the North Pole leans toward the Sun, the noon Sun is high (about 74 degrees up), and the days are long.

Why it works: The knockout clue is that the two hemispheres have opposite seasons at the same time. When it is winter in Kansas City it is summer in Australia. A change in distance from the Sun would cool (or warm) the entire planet at once, so it could never make one half cold while the other half is hot. A tilt can, because it decides which hemisphere is leaning into the sunlight. On top of that, the distance actually changes the wrong way for the myth: we are nearest the Sun during Northern winter.

Pattern, "opposite seasons rules out a global cause": when someone offers a reason for a seasonal or regional pattern, ask what the other hemisphere is doing at the same time. If the two hemispheres are opposite, the cause has to be something that flips between them (the tilt and the Sun angle), not something that affects the whole planet equally (the distance to the Sun, or the Sun getting brighter). Use this test whenever a "the whole Earth changed" explanation is offered for something that is really lopsided between the hemispheres.

P3. On a clear summer noon about 1000 W/m² of sunlight reaches the ground. Fresh snow reflects about 80% of it, while dark ocean water reflects only about 10%. How much energy does each surface absorb, and which one warms up more?

AnswerSnow: it reflects 80%, so it absorbs the other 20%: \(20\% \text{ of } 1000 = 200 \text{ W/m}^2\). Ocean: it reflects 10%, so it absorbs 90%: \(90\% \text{ of } 1000 = 900 \text{ W/m}^2\). The dark ocean absorbs about 4.5 times as much and warms up far more. (This is also why melting bright ice, which exposes dark water, lets the surface soak up even more sunlight, a link you will see again in Chapter 17.)

3. Heat on the Move

Four ways energy travels through the air: radiation (waves), conduction (touch), convection (rising air), and latent heat (energy hidden in water vapor).

Prerequisites: Chapter 2: Energy from the Sun (where the energy comes from).
This chapter: Heat on the Move (radiation, conduction, convection, latent heat)
Unlocks: Chapter 4: Temperature and Heating the Air · Chapter 10: Clouds · Chapter 15: Severe Weather I (thunderstorms)

Start with four things you can feel

Before any definitions, step outside on a warm summer afternoon in Kansas City and notice four ordinary things. Each one is energy moving from something warmer to something cooler, but each moves it in a completely different way. Watch what happens first. We will name the four ways right after.

Observation 1. Turn your face toward the Sun on a clear afternoon. It feels warm at once, even if the air around you is cool. That warmth just crossed about 150 million km of empty space to reach your skin.

Observation 2. Walk barefoot across sun-baked pavement or hot sand at noon. Your soles heat up the instant they touch the ground, sometimes enough to hurt. But lift your feet even an inch and the air just above feels far cooler than the surface did.

Observation 3. Watch a hawk or a glider over the Kansas plains on a hot day. It circles higher and higher without ever flapping or running an engine. It is riding an invisible column of rising warm air called a thermal.

Observation 4. After a run, the sweat on your skin dries and you feel a chill, even in still, warm air. And on a calm clear night, dew forms on the grass and the air right above it stops cooling as fast as the air higher up. Two small hints that water quietly takes in and gives back energy when it changes between liquid and vapor.

The pattern: four ways heat moves

Those four observations are the whole chapter. Energy never sits still. It always moves from warmer places toward cooler ones, and the atmosphere has exactly four ways to move it:

  • Radiation warmed your face (waves across empty space).
  • Conduction warmed your feet (direct touch).
  • Convection lifted the glider (moving warm air carries the heat with it).
  • Latent heat cooled your drying sweat and warmed the forming dew (energy hidden in water as it changes phase).

Learn to name each one and why it matters in the sky, and you can explain almost any weather story on this page and in the chapters ahead.

The core idea

Now the four, defined properly, and why each one matters in the atmosphere.

1. Radiation. Energy that travels as electromagnetic waves. Radiation is the only transfer that needs no material to move through, so it can cross the empty vacuum of space. That is how the Sun's energy reaches us at all (Observation 1). The Sun is very hot, so it sends mostly visible light (short waves). Earth is much cooler, so it sends its energy back out as infrared (longer waves you feel as heat but cannot see). Every warm object radiates, including the ground, a cloud, and you.

2. Conduction. Energy passed by direct contact, molecule bumping into molecule. Touch a hot pan and your hand heats up fast, because metal conducts well (the same reason hot pavement burns your feet in Observation 2). Air is a poor conductor, so the hot ground only warms the paper-thin layer of air actually touching it. Conduction alone would take forever to heat the whole sky.

3. Convection. Energy carried by the motion of a fluid (air or water). Warm air is less dense, so it is lighter than the cooler air around it and floats up (the thermal in Observation 3). Cooler, denser air sinks to take its place. This up-and-down churning carries heat from the ground high into the atmosphere. Convection, not conduction, does the real work of warming the air above Kansas City.

4. Latent heat. The hidden energy stored or released when water changes phase. When liquid water evaporates into vapor, it soaks up a large amount of energy and stores it (which is why drying sweat cools you in Observation 4). That energy stays locked in the vapor with no change in temperature. Later, when the vapor condenses back into liquid (dew forming, or droplets forming inside a cloud), that same energy is released and warms the surrounding air. This is the atmosphere's hidden fuel, and it is what powers clouds and storms.

The one rule to never mix up: evaporation stores energy (it takes energy in, which is why sweat drying off cools your skin). Condensation releases energy (it gives energy back, which is why it warms the air inside a growing cloud).
🌦️ Running story, Parcel P over Kansas City: it is a warm summer afternoon. Sunlight crosses space and warms the Kansas City ground (radiation). The hot ground warms the air touching it, including our little Parcel P (conduction). Warm Parcel P is lighter than the air around it, so it floats upward (convection). And because it is a humid day, Parcel P is carrying water vapor, which is packed with hidden energy (latent heat). The worked examples below follow P through all four transfers.

Worked examples

Example 1 (the whole chain in one afternoon). Trace how the Sun's energy gets from space all the way up into the sky above Kansas City. Name the transfer at each step.

Step 1, radiation: sunlight crosses 150 million km of empty space as waves and is absorbed by the ground.

Step 2, conduction: the hot ground touches the air. Molecule-to-molecule contact warms the thin layer of air right at the surface, including Parcel P.

Step 3, convection: warm Parcel P is now less dense than the air above it, so it rises. Cooler air sinks in around it. The heat rides upward with the moving air.

Aha: the Sun does not heat the air directly. It heats the ground, and the ground heats the air from below. That is why the air is usually warmest right near the surface on a sunny day.

Example 2 (why the whole sky warms up, not just the bottom inch). Air is a poor conductor. So if conduction only warms the paper-thin layer touching the ground, how does the warmth reach hundreds of meters up by afternoon?

Conduction gets the job started but cannot finish it. Once that bottom layer of air is warm, it becomes less dense than the air above it, so it lifts off and rises as a bubble (a parcel like P). Cooler air flows in underneath, touches the hot ground, warms, and rises in its turn. This churning is convection, and it moves heat upward thousands of times faster than conduction ever could.

Aha: conduction lights the match, but convection carries the fire. In the open air, the moment a parcel gets warmer than its surroundings, the transfer switches from slow touching to fast lifting.

Example 3 (the hidden fuel). Parcel P is humid: it carries about 10 grams of water vapor for every kilogram of air. Where did the energy to make that vapor come from, and what happens to it when P rises high enough for the vapor to condense into a cloud?

Storing it (evaporation): earlier, sunlight evaporated water from soil, lakes, and plants around Kansas City. Turning liquid into vapor takes a large amount of energy, about 2,500 kilojoules for every kilogram of water (\(2{,}500\ \text{kJ/kg}\)). That energy did not vanish. It is stored, invisible, inside the vapor Parcel P now carries.

Releasing it (condensation): as P rises and cools, its vapor condenses back into tiny liquid droplets. Condensation gives that stored energy straight back to the air: \[ 10\ \text{g} \times 2.5\ \text{kJ/g} = 25\ \text{kJ released per kilogram of air.} \] It takes only about \(1.0\ \text{kJ}\) to warm a kilogram of air by one degree Celsius, so that release could warm the air by roughly \[ \frac{25\ \text{kJ}}{1.0\ \text{kJ per }^\circ\text{C}} \approx 25\,^\circ\text{C}. \]

Aha: humid air is loaded fuel. The water vapor in Parcel P is a battery charged up by the Sun. When it condenses, the battery dumps its energy into the air, making P even warmer and lighter, which shoves it higher still. That feedback is the engine behind clouds and thunderstorms.

Common pitfalls

  • Getting latent heat backwards. Evaporation stores energy (takes it in and cools the surroundings); condensation releases energy (gives it back and warms the surroundings). Sweat drying off feels cool because evaporation is pulling energy out of your skin. Never swap these two.
  • Thinking the Sun heats the air directly. Sunlight mostly passes through clear air and heats the ground. The ground then heats the air from below by conduction and convection. That is why mountaintops, closer to the Sun, are colder, not warmer.
  • Confusing conduction and convection. Conduction is heat passed by touching, with the material staying put. Convection is heat carried by the material itself moving. Air is a poor conductor, so in the open sky convection does almost all the lifting of heat.
  • Forgetting radiation needs no medium. Radiation is the only one of the four that works across empty space. Conduction and convection both need matter to move through, so neither can carry the Sun's energy across the vacuum to Earth.

P3 (the storm fuel). Parcel P holds about 10 grams of water vapor per kilogram of air. (This is an idealized top estimate: in a real cloud the vapor condenses gradually and some warmth spreads out, so the real boost is smaller but still large.)

Why it works. The energy that once evaporated the water did not disappear; it was stored in the vapor. Condensation is just that step run in reverse, so it hands the energy straight back to the air as heat. Warmer air is less dense, so this fresh warmth makes Parcel P even lighter than its surroundings. That extra lightness pushes it upward harder, lifting more vapor to condense, which releases still more heat. The process feeds itself.

Pattern (latent heat as stored fuel): whenever humid air is forced to rise and condense, expect a burst of hidden heating. This is the difference between a small fair-weather cloud and a towering thunderstorm: more water vapor means more stored fuel to release. Reach for this idea any time a problem pairs "humid" with "rising air." Contrast it with plain sensible heating from the ground, which you can feel and measure directly, while latent heat stays hidden until the water changes phase.

4. Temperature and Heating the Air

Sunlight barely warms the air on its way down. It warms the ground, and the ground warms the air from below. That one fact explains why afternoons are the warmest, why dawn is the coldest, and why the coast is milder than Kansas City.

Prerequisites: Chapter 2: Energy from the Sun · Chapter 3: Heat on the Move
This chapter: how the ground heats the air, the daily temperature cycle, land versus water, and the thermometer.
Unlocks: Chapter 5: Air Pressure · Chapter 12: Air Masses and Fronts

Start with one day's temperatures over Kansas City

Before any theory, look at three ordinary facts about temperature in Kansas City (about 39 degrees North, in the middle of the country). Each one is a little surprising, and each one is a clue to where the air gets its heat. See what happens first. We will name the pattern right after.

Observation 1. On a clear summer day the Sun is highest in the sky at solar noon. Yet the air is usually warmest around 3 to 4 in the afternoon, and it is coldest not at midnight but just after sunrise. Neither peak lines up with the Sun.

So the hottest and coldest moments of the day are both shifted later than the Sun. Hold that thought; Worked Example 1 explains why.

Observation 2. On a representative summer day Kansas City swings about 15 C between its cool morning low (about 15 C) and its hot afternoon high (about 30 C). A city right on a coast or a big lake at the same latitude, getting the same sunlight, swings much less, maybe 6 C. Water somehow keeps its neighbors milder.

Same Sun, very different daily swing. The difference is the water. Worked Example 2 lists the four reasons behind it.

Observation 3. A thermometer sitting in the Kansas City air reads 25 on its Celsius scale. The very same thermometer reads 77 on its Fahrenheit scale. One temperature, two numbers, because there are two common scales in use and they do not agree on a value.

Always say which scale, because 30 in Celsius is a hot day while 30 in Fahrenheit is below freezing. Worked Example 3 does the conversions and explains the thermometer.

The pattern: the air is heated from below

Those three facts tell one story. Sunlight does not heat the air much on its way down. It heats the ground, and the ground heats the air from below. So the air's temperature does not follow the Sun moment by moment. It follows the running total of heat piled up in the surface. Read the three observations again with that idea:

  • Observation 1 (the lag): the ground keeps gaining heat all morning and into the afternoon, for as long as the sunlight coming in beats the heat going out. So the warmest time comes after noon, and the coldest comes just after sunrise, because the ground loses heat all night.
  • Observation 2 (water): water takes in and gives back heat slowly, so places next to big water barely swing. Land heats and cools fast, so inland Kansas City swings a lot.
  • Observation 3 (the scales): temperature is just a number for how fast the air molecules are moving, and we read that number on either of two scales.

The pattern in one line: the air is heated from below, and its temperature tracks the running total of heat in the surface, not the instant strength of the Sun. The rest of the chapter is the machinery behind that sentence.

The core idea

The air is heated mostly from below. You might think sunlight simply heats the air it passes through. It mostly does not. Most of the Sun's energy shoots straight through the clear air without warming it much. That energy is absorbed by the ground instead. The warm ground then heats the thin layer of air touching it, and that warmth spreads upward. So the real furnace for the lower atmosphere is the surface of the Earth, not the Sun heating the air directly.

Two of the heat-moving methods from Chapter 3 do the work here:

  • Conduction: the warm ground touches the air right above it and hands off heat, molecule by molecule. This warms only a very thin layer.
  • Convection: that warm bottom air is lighter, so it rises and carries the heat higher, while cooler air sinks to take its place. This is what lifts Parcel P off the ground on a sunny afternoon.

Why the troposphere is warmest at the bottom (a callback to Chapter 1). Because the heat source is the ground at the bottom, the air is warmest near the surface and gets colder as you go up through the troposphere. The heat is coming from below, so the closer you are to the floor, the warmer it is. That is exactly the falling-with-height temperature you met in Chapter 1.

What "temperature" actually measures. Temperature is a measure of how fast the air molecules are jiggling and moving. Faster average motion means a higher temperature. A thermometer is the tool that reads it. Two scales show up in Science Olympiad:

  • Celsius (C): water freezes at 0 C and boils at 100 C.
  • Fahrenheit (F): water freezes at 32 F and boils at 212 F.

To convert between them:

\[ F = \tfrac{9}{5}\,C + 32 \qquad\qquad C = \tfrac{5}{9}\,(F - 32) \]

So 25 C is \(\tfrac{9}{5}(25)+32 = 45+32 = 77\) F, which matches the thermometer in Observation 3. Always write the unit, because "30 degrees" means a warm day in Celsius but a freezing one in Fahrenheit.

🌦️ Running example, Parcel P over Kansas City: keep watching Parcel P, our small blob of air resting just above the ground in Kansas City. In Chapter 3 the Sun warmed the ground. In this chapter the warm ground passes that heat up into Parcel P, first by touching it (conduction), then by lifting it (convection). Worked examples

Example 1 (the diurnal lag). On a clear Kansas City summer day the Sun is highest in the sky at about noon. So why is the air usually warmest around 3 to 4 in the afternoon, not at noon? (See the daily curve in Observation 1.)

Solution. Think of the ground like a bank account for heat. All morning the Sun pays heat in faster than the ground gives it back to space, so the balance keeps climbing. At noon the Sun is highest and the deposits are largest, but the account is not full yet. The ground keeps gaining as long as the incoming sunlight is greater than the heat it loses. Only in mid-afternoon, around 3 to 4 p.m., does the outgoing loss finally catch up to the shrinking incoming sunlight. That is the moment the ground, and the air above it, stops warming and starts to cool. So the peak temperature comes a few hours after the peak sunlight.

The same logic runs backwards overnight. The ground keeps losing heat all night long, so it keeps getting colder right up until the Sun comes back. That is why the coldest moment is not midnight but just after sunrise.

Aha: temperature follows the running total of heat, not the instant strength of the Sun. As long as more heat is coming in than going out, it keeps getting warmer, even after the Sun starts to drop. Peak heat lags peak Sun.

Example 2 (land versus water). Kansas City can swing about 15 C between the low and the high in a single day. A city right on a coast or a big lake at the same latitude has a much smaller swing, maybe 6 C. Why does water keep its neighbors so much milder? (See the two-panel figure in Observation 2.)

Solution. Land and water react to the same sunlight in very different ways, for four reasons that all point the same direction:

  • Land is not see-through, water is. Sunlight only heats a thin top layer of soil or rock, so that thin layer gets very hot. Sunlight reaches down several meters into clear water, spreading the same energy over a much larger amount of stuff.
  • Water mixes, land does not. Waves and currents stir warm surface water down and bring cool water up, sharing the heat through a deep layer. Solid ground cannot stir.
  • Water needs more energy to warm up. It takes about four times as much heat to raise the temperature of water as it does soil or rock by the same amount. Scientists call this water's high heat capacity. Water is a heat sponge.
  • Water cools itself by evaporating. Some of the Sun's energy goes into turning water into vapor instead of raising the temperature.

Put together, water warms slowly by day and cools slowly by night, so it acts like a giant thermostat for the air near it. A place near big water has a maritime climate, mild with small swings. Kansas City sits far from any ocean, so it has a continental climate with no thermostat. Its land heats up fast in the afternoon and cools off fast at night, giving big daily swings and big differences between summer and winter.

Aha: water is slow to heat and slow to cool, so places near big water stay milder (maritime), while a mid-continent city like Kansas City swings hard (continental). This slow-water, fast-land contrast is also the engine behind the sea breezes you will meet later.

Example 3 (converting scales and reading a max and min thermometer). Parcel P reads 25 C on a Celsius thermometer. What is that in Fahrenheit? And a backyard maximum and minimum thermometer shows a high mark at 30 C and a low mark at 15 C for the day. What were the high and low in Fahrenheit, and what was the day's range?

Solution. Use the conversion formula \(F = \tfrac{9}{5}C + 32\).

  • Parcel P: \(F = \tfrac{9}{5}(25)+32 = 45+32 = 77\) F.
  • Day's high: \(F = \tfrac{9}{5}(30)+32 = 54+32 = 86\) F.
  • Day's low: \(F = \tfrac{9}{5}(15)+32 = 27+32 = 59\) F.
  • Daily range in Celsius: \(30 - 15 = 15\) C. (In Fahrenheit the range is \(86 - 59 = 27\) F. The range is not the same number in the two scales because a Celsius degree is bigger than a Fahrenheit degree.)

How a max and min thermometer works. A normal thermometer only tells you the temperature right now. A maximum and minimum thermometer remembers the hottest and coldest points since you last reset it. The classic design is a U-shaped tube with two tiny sliding markers, one in each arm. As the liquid rises and falls through the day, it pushes each marker along and leaves it stuck at the farthest point it reached. One marker gets left at the day's high, the other at the day's low. You read both numbers, then drag the markers back down to the current level (often with a small magnet) to start a fresh day. This is how weather records keep a daily high and low even though nobody is watching at 4 a.m.

Aha: a thermometer measures how fast molecules are moving, and a max and min thermometer adds a memory so it can report the day's high and low without anyone standing by. Watch the units: 30 in Celsius (86 F) is a hot day, but 30 in Fahrenheit would be below freezing.

Common pitfalls

Thinking sunlight heats the air directly. Most sunlight passes through the clear air and heats the ground. The ground then heats the air from below. If you forget this, the whole daily cycle and the warm-at-the-bottom troposphere stop making sense.
Assuming the warmest time is noon and the coldest is midnight. Temperature follows the running total of heat, not the instant strength of the Sun. The warmest time lags to mid-afternoon, and the coldest time is just after sunrise, because the ground keeps losing heat all night.
Mixing up why water is mild. It is not that water "attracts" cool weather. Water simply heats and cools slowly, so it smooths out the swings for the air near it. That is a different idea from land being warmer or cooler on average.
Dropping the units on temperature. "It is 30 degrees" means a hot day in Celsius and a freezing one in Fahrenheit. Always say which scale.

Practice

P1. On a clear day the Sun is highest at noon, yet the thermometer keeps climbing until about 3 or 4 in the afternoon. Explain why the hottest part of the day comes after the Sun is highest.

Answer The hottest time is mid-afternoon, a few hours after solar noon.

Why it works: the ground keeps warming for as long as it gains more heat from sunlight than it loses back to the sky. At noon the Sun is strongest, but the ground is still gaining, so its temperature is still rising. It keeps rising through the early afternoon. Only when the weakening afternoon sunlight finally drops to equal the heat the ground is losing does the temperature stop climbing and start to fall. Since the air is heated from below by that ground, the air peaks at the same time. The same idea explains the morning: the ground loses heat all night and reaches its coldest just after sunrise, not at midnight.

Pattern, watch the running total, not the instant input (the lag rule): whenever a store of heat (or water, or anything that fills and drains) is fed by an input and drained by an output, its level keeps rising as long as input beats output, and it peaks when the two are equal, not when the input is largest. So the peak of the store always lags the peak of the input. Reach for this whenever a quantity seems to peak "too late": ask when the gains stop beating the losses. Here the store is the heat in the ground and lower air, the input is sunlight, and the output is heat radiated back to space.

P2. Kansas City and a city on an ocean coast sit at the same latitude and get about the same sunlight. Why does the coastal city usually have milder days and nights, with a smaller gap between its summer and winter?

Answer Water heats and cools slowly, so the ocean acts like a thermostat for the coastal air, keeping it mild all year (a maritime climate). Kansas City sits far from big water, so its land heats and cools fast and swings much more, both day to night and season to season (a continental climate).

P3. A max and min thermometer in a Kansas City backyard shows the low marker at 15 C and the high marker at 30 C for the day. Give the high and low in Fahrenheit, and the day's range in each scale.

Answer Low: \(\tfrac{9}{5}(15)+32 = 59\) F. High: \(\tfrac{9}{5}(30)+32 = 86\) F. Range: \(30 - 15 = 15\) C, which is \(86 - 59 = 27\) F. (The two range numbers differ because a Celsius degree is larger than a Fahrenheit degree; only actual temperatures match up point for point, like 0 C = 32 F.)

5. Air Pressure

Air has weight, so it pushes on everything in every direction. The pressure at any spot is the weight of the whole column of air above it.

Prerequisites: Chapter 1: The Atmosphere · Chapter 4: Temperature and Heating the Air
This chapter: Air Pressure
Unlocks: Chapter 6: Wind and the Coriolis Effect · Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones

Start from what the barometer shows

A barometer is the instrument that measures air pressure. Before any definition, watch what one actually does in two everyday situations. The pattern behind both is the whole idea of this chapter.

Observation 1: the reading swings as weather moves through. Follow the three-day cold-front episode over Kansas City that runs through this course. On Day 1, warm and humid under a departing high, the barometer reads about 1013 hPa. On Day 2 a cold front arrives, the reading falls to about 1002 hPa, and thunderstorms fire along the front. On Day 3, behind the front under a new high, the reading climbs to about 1020 hPa and the sky clears.

Observation 2: the reading drops as you climb. Carry the same barometer up a mountain and it falls the whole way. At sea level it reads about 1013 hPa. About 5.5 km up it has dropped to roughly 500 hPa, about half. Near 8.8 km, the height of Mount Everest, it reads only about 300 hPa, roughly one third of the sea-level value. That thin air is why climbers up there gasp for breath and carry oxygen.

Name the pattern

Both observations point to the same simple idea. Air pressure at any spot is the weight of all the air stacked above that spot. Once you have that, the two behaviors follow at once:

  • Pressure falls as you go up (Observation 2): the higher you are, the less air is left above you, so less weight presses down.
  • A falling barometer warns of a storm; a rising one means clearing (Observation 1): when the air overhead thins toward a low, unsettled weather is on the way; when a high builds and the air overhead grows heavier, the weather fairs up. Read the trend, not the single number.

The mechanism: why pressure is the weight of the air above

Air has weight. It does not feel heavy because it is spread out, but a tall stack of it is not nothing. Add up all the air from the ground to the top of the sky and it presses down hard. Because air is a gas that fills the space and bumps into every surface, that push acts in all directions at once: down, up, and sideways.

So here is the definition. Air pressure at a point is the weight of all the air in the column above that point, shared over the area. At sea level that comes to about 1 kilogram pressing on every square centimeter (about 14.7 pounds on every square inch). Put another way, roughly 10,000 kilograms of air, about 10 metric tons, sits over every square meter of ground.

Aha: the reason all that weight does not crush you is that the pressure inside your body pushes back out with the same force. The two balance, so you never feel it, until it changes.

Why pressure falls as you go up

The mechanism is simple once you have the definition: pressure is the weight of the air above you, and the higher you go, the less air is left above. Less air above means less weight, which means lower pressure. Air is also squeezed densest near the ground, so most of the air (and most of the weight) is packed in the lowest few kilometers, which is why the reading drops fast low down and slower up high. A rough rule of thumb is that pressure roughly halves about every 5.5 kilometers, which is exactly the fall you saw on the climb above (1013 hPa at sea level, ~500 hPa near 5.5 km, ~300 hPa near 8.8 km).

Highs, lows, and units

Highs and lows are relative. A "high" is just a region where the pressure is higher than the air around it, and a "low" is a region where it is lower than its surroundings. It is always a comparison to the neighbors, never a fixed cutoff. A reading of 1013 hPa is not automatically a "high."

The units. The same pressure can be written four common ways. The standard sea-level value is:

\[ 1013.25\ \text{hPa} = 1013.25\ \text{mb} = 29.92\ \text{inHg} = 1\ \text{atm} \]

A few things to keep straight. The hectopascal (hPa) and the millibar (mb) are the same number; forecasters just use both names. The inch of mercury (inHg) comes from how a mercury barometer works. To go from inHg to hPa, multiply by 33.86 (since 1 inHg = 33.86 hPa). To go the other way, from hPa to inHg, multiply by 0.02953. In science units, 1 atm = 101325 pascals (Pa).

Worked examples

Example 1. Why does all that air not crush you? Estimate how hard sea-level air pushes.

Set up the picture. The air above one square centimeter of ground weighs about 1 kilogram. Spread that over a whole square meter and you get roughly 10,000 kilograms, about 10 metric tons, pressing down. In the units engineers use, standard sea-level pressure is about 14.7 pounds on every square inch.

  • On 1 square centimeter: about 1 kg of force (1013 hPa).
  • On 1 square meter: about 10,000 kg, roughly 10 metric tons.
  • Per square inch: about 14.7 pounds.
Aha: the air pushes in every direction at once, and the pressure inside your body pushes back out with the same force. The two balance, so you never feel the 10 tons. You only notice pressure when it changes, like the ear pop in an elevator or a plane.

Example 2. How much does the pressure drop as you climb? Use the "halves about every 5.5 km" rule.

Start at the ground with the standard sea-level value, then halve it for each 5.5 km of height:

  • Sea level: ~1013 hPa (29.92 inHg).
  • About 5.5 km up: roughly half, so ~500 hPa.
  • About 8.8 km up (near the height of Mount Everest): down to about ~300 hPa, roughly one third of sea level.

The air-column picture near the top of the chapter shows why: most of the molecules, and so most of the weight, are packed in the bottom few kilometers. High up there is little air left above you.

Aha: fewer molecules above means less weight, which means lower pressure. This is the whole reason airplanes are sealed and pressurized and why mountaineers gasp for breath.

Example 3. On Day 1 of our Kansas City cold-front episode, the barometer reads ~1013 hPa. Write that in the other units, and say what instrument measured it.

Convert. A reading of ~1013 hPa is essentially the standard sea-level value, so:

  • ~1013 hPa = ~1013 mb (same number, different name).
  • ~1013 hPa is right at the standard sea-level value 1013.25 hPa, which is 29.92 inHg.
  • That is about 1 atm.

The instrument. A barometer measures air pressure. In a mercury barometer, air pushes down on a dish of mercury and holds a column of mercury up a glass tube. At sea level the column stands about 760 mm high, which is the same as 29.92 inHg. In an aneroid barometer there is no liquid at all: a small sealed metal cell squeezes or expands as the pressure changes, and a spring turns that motion into a moving needle on a dial. Most home barometers and aircraft altimeters are aneroid.

Aha: it is one pressure written four ways. The barometer is simply how a forecaster feels the weight of the air, and watching it move matters more than any single reading.
Common pitfalls:
  • Pressure is not only downward. Air pushes equally in every direction, up and sideways too. That is why a suction cup sticks and why your body is not squashed.
  • "High" and "low" are relative. They compare a spot to the air around it. A single number like 1013 hPa is not by itself a high or a low.
  • Do not mix up the units. hPa and mb are the same number. When you see a station reading in inches, remember 1 inHg = 33.86 hPa, so 29.92 inHg works out to 1013 hPa, not something near 30.

Answer

The calculation. To inHg, multiply by 0.02953: \(1020 \times 0.02953 \approx 30.12\) inHg. To atm, divide by the standard value 1013.25: \(1020 / 1013.25 \approx 1.01\) atm. And in millibars it is simply 1020 mb, the same number as hPa.

Inches of mercury use a fixed factor because a taller mercury column always means the same amount more pressure, so multiplying by the constant 0.02953 (or dividing by 33.86) turns one into the other every time.

Pattern, use the fixed conversion factor, do not re-derive it: whenever a value comes in one pressure unit and you need another, reach for the constant (1 inHg = 33.86 hPa, hPa = mb, 1 atm = 1013.25 hPa) instead of reasoning it out fresh. 6. Wind and the Coriolis Effect

Wind is just air moving from high pressure toward low pressure. Three forces set how fast it goes and which way it points.

Prerequisites: Chapter 5: Air Pressure
This chapter: Wind and the Coriolis Effect
Unlocks: Chapter 13: Global Circulation and Jet Streams · Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones

Start with a real weather map

In Chapter 5: Air Pressure we learned that air presses harder in some places than others, and that isobars are the lines on a weather map joining places of equal pressure. Air does not sit still next to a pressure difference. It flows, and that moving air is what we call wind. Before naming any rules, look at what the wind over Kansas City actually does.

Observation 1: the wind will not run straight downhill. On today's map the isobars over Kansas City run east to west. Pressure is high to the south (1024 hPa) and low to the north (1004 hPa). Common sense says the air should slide straight "downhill," due north, from the high into the low. It does not. The measured wind blows toward the east, along the isobars, as if the straight-north path had been bent to the right.

Observation 2: around a low, the wind goes in circles. Now zoom out to a whole low-pressure center. Air does not pour straight in from every side and pile up in the middle. Instead the wind wraps around the low, circling it counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere, and leaking only slowly inward near the ground. This is why storms spin instead of collapsing straight inward.

The pattern

The same three steps repeat on every Northern Hemisphere weather map:

  • A pressure difference starts the wind moving, from high pressure toward low.
  • The moving air is always bent to its RIGHT.
  • Once it has bent far enough, it stops heading into the low and coasts along the isobars. Around a low that gives counterclockwise flow; around a high, clockwise.

That steady wind, running parallel to the isobars, has a name: the geostrophic wind. The rest of the chapter explains the three forces behind these steps and why they balance the way they do.

The mechanism: the three forces on the wind

Why does the wind refuse to run straight downhill, and why does it circle a low instead of collapsing into it? Three forces act on our moving parcel of air, "Parcel P," floating over Kansas City near 39 degrees north. Add the three together and the pattern above falls out. Take them one at a time.

Force 1: the pressure-gradient force (the push)

On a weather map, lines called isobars connect places with the same pressure (you met them in Chapter 5). The pressure-gradient force is the push that points straight from high pressure toward low pressure, at a right angle to the isobars. It is the engine that starts the wind.

How strong is the push? It depends on how tightly the isobars are packed. Closer isobars mean a stronger wind. Think of it like a hill: a steep slope (isobars packed close together) rolls a ball faster than a gentle slope (isobars spread far apart). When you see isobars crowded together on a map, expect gusty conditions there.

Force 2: the Coriolis effect (the turn)

The Earth spins. Because the ground under Parcel P is turning as the parcel travels, its path looks bent to us standing on the surface. This apparent bending is the Coriolis effect. The key fact to memorize:

In the Northern Hemisphere, moving air is deflected to its RIGHT. In the Southern Hemisphere, to its LEFT. At the equator the deflection is zero.

Kansas City sits near 39 degrees north, so the air there always gets nudged to the right of wherever it is heading.

Myth check: the Coriolis effect does NOT decide which way your sink or toilet drains. That is far too small and too quick for Earth's slow spin to matter. Coriolis only shows up over large distances and long travel times, like a wind crossing hundreds of kilometers. Drains swirl from the shape of the basin and how the water enters.

Force 3: friction (the drag, only near the ground)

Near the surface, wind rubs against trees, hills, buildings, and the ground itself. This friction does two things: it slows the wind down, and it turns the wind so that it blows slightly ACROSS the isobars, angled toward the low pressure. Higher up, above roughly one kilometer, the air no longer touches the ground, friction fades away, and this drag disappears.

Putting them together: the geostrophic wind aloft

Follow Parcel P up above the friction layer, where only the push and the turn are left. The pressure-gradient force starts P moving toward the low. As soon as P moves, Coriolis bends it to the right. P keeps turning until the two forces exactly oppose each other and cancel out. When that balance is reached, the parcel is no longer heading toward the low at all. It is coasting parallel to the isobars. This balanced wind is called the geostrophic wind.

This is the fact students most often get wrong, so say it out loud: wind aloft blows parallel to the isobars, not straight down into the low. A handy rule for the Northern Hemisphere: if you stand with your back to the wind, the low pressure is on your left.

Measuring the wind

Two simple instruments record what the wind is doing. An anemometer measures wind SPEED, usually with three or four little cups that spin faster in stronger wind. A wind vane measures wind DIRECTION, swinging so its arrow points into the wind. One naming habit trips people up: a wind is named for the direction it comes FROM. A "west wind" blows from the west toward the east. So the westerly wind aloft in the figure above comes from the west.

Worked examples

Example 1. On the map above, the isobars run east to west, with lower pressure to the north and higher pressure to the south. Above the friction layer, which way does the wind over Kansas City blow?

Step 1, the push. The pressure-gradient force points from high toward low, at a right angle to the isobars. High is south, low is north, so the push points straight north.

Step 2, the turn. Parcel P starts drifting north. Coriolis deflects it to the right (Northern Hemisphere), which bends it toward the east.

Step 3, the balance. P keeps turning until the push and the Coriolis turn cancel. Now P coasts parallel to the isobars, heading east. The low pressure ends up on its left.

Answer: the geostrophic wind blows toward the east. Because a wind is named for where it comes from, this is a west wind (a westerly).

Aha: the wind does not charge straight north into the low. The push and the turn settle into a truce, and the leftover wind runs sideways along the isobars.

Example 2. Same pressure map. How is the wind DIFFERENT right at the surface, where Parcel P feels the ground?

Near the ground, friction drags on the wind. It does two things. First, it slows the wind down, so surface wind is weaker than the wind aloft. Second, a slower wind gets a weaker Coriolis turn, so the push wins a little and pulls the wind partly toward the low. The result is that the surface wind blows slightly ACROSS the isobars, angled toward the low pressure, instead of straight along them.

Answer: aloft the wind is faster and parallel to the isobars; at the surface it is slower and tilted across the isobars toward the low.

Aha: friction is the reason surface air actually leaks into a low. That slow drift toward the low is how air piles up and rises in a storm, which you will use in Chapter 14.

Example 3. Two spots on the map both have isobars drawn 4 hPa apart. At spot A the isobars are 200 km apart. At spot B they squeeze to only 100 km apart. Where is the wind stronger, and by roughly how much?

The strength of the push is the pressure change divided by the distance it happens over.

  • Spot A: \(\dfrac{4\ \text{hPa}}{200\ \text{km}} = 0.02\ \text{hPa per km}\).
  • Spot B: \(\dfrac{4\ \text{hPa}}{100\ \text{km}} = 0.04\ \text{hPa per km}\).

Answer: spot B has twice the push (0.04 versus 0.02 hPa per km), so the wind there is about twice as strong.

Aha: you can read wind speed straight off a map. Packed isobars mean a steep pressure hill and strong wind. Spread-out isobars mean a gentle slope and light wind.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: thinking wind aloft dives straight into the low. It does not. Above the friction layer the push and the Coriolis turn balance, and the wind ends up parallel to the isobars. Only at the surface, where friction weakens the Coriolis turn, does the wind cross the isobars toward the low.
Pitfall 2: getting the Coriolis direction backward. In the Northern Hemisphere the deflection is to the RIGHT of the motion, in the Southern Hemisphere to the LEFT, and it is zero at the equator. Kansas City is in the Northern Hemisphere, so its air always turns right.
Pitfall 3: believing Coriolis swirls your sink. The effect is far too weak over the size of a basin and the few seconds a drain takes. It matters only over big distances and long times, like weather systems.

Answer: the wind blows toward the north (a south wind, or southerly).

Answer: because of the Coriolis effect. The moment the air starts moving toward the low, its path gets bent to the right (in the Northern Hemisphere). It cannot travel in a straight line to the low.

Why it works: the push and the Coriolis turn reach a balance called the geostrophic balance. In that balance the two forces are equal and opposite, so there is nothing left to pull the air toward the low. The wind ends up coasting parallel to the isobars, circling around the low rather than diving in. Water on a hill has no Coriolis turn to fight, so water really does run straight downhill, but air over a spinning planet does not.

Pattern, geostrophic balance: whenever two steady forces on the air point opposite ways and cancel, the wind stops accelerating and flows straight along the isobars. Reach for this idea for any wind ABOVE the friction layer. Near the surface the balance breaks because friction weakens the wind and its Coriolis turn, so the push wins a little and the wind angles across the isobars toward the low. That leak is exactly how air converges into a storm.

AnswerIn the Southern Hemisphere the deflection is to the LEFT of the motion. Right on the equator the Coriolis deflection is zero, so there is no turning at all.

Checks for understanding: (1) Given isobars, point where you would put an anemometer to record the strongest wind (packed isobars). (2) On a map, is the wind aloft parallel to or across the isobars? (3) Which way does air turn in the Northern Hemisphere? (4) Why does surface wind leak toward the low?

7. Humidity

The air is full of invisible water vapor. Warm air can hold much more of it than cold air, so simply cooling humid air is what makes water condense out.

Prerequisites: Chapter 3: Heat on the Move (latent heat) · Chapter 4: Temperature and Heating the Air
This chapter: Humidity (absolute humidity, relative humidity, dew point, saturation)
Unlocks: Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates · Chapter 10: Clouds

Start with what you can see

Before any definitions, look at three ordinary things that happen with the air over Kansas City. Each one is a puzzle you can already picture. The names and formulas come afterward, once the pattern behind all three is obvious.

1. Dew appears on the grass from nowhere

One clear, calm night the evening air is 30 °C. No rain falls and nobody waters the lawn. Yet by dawn the grass is soaked with dew. The only thing that changed overnight was the temperature: the air cooled down. Track it and you find the dew starts forming the moment the air reaches about 20 °C, not before. Something about cooling to that particular temperature pulled liquid water out of thin air.

2. Two 30-degree days that feel nothing alike

On Day 1 of the cold-front episode the afternoon reads 30 °C and it feels muggy, sticky, and heavy. On a different day the thermometer again reads 30 °C, but the air feels dry and comfortable. Same temperature, opposite feel. The thermometer clearly is not the whole story. What differs is how much invisible water the air is carrying: about 17.3 grams in every cubic meter on the muggy day, only about 6.8 grams on the dry one.

3. Warm air has far more room for water

Both puzzles have the same cause. There is a ceiling on how much water vapor the air can hold, and that ceiling climbs steeply as the air warms. Air at 0 °C can hold only about 4.8 g/m3, while air at 30 °C can hold about 30.4 g/m3, more than six times as much room. That is why cooling the night air eventually forces water out (the ceiling drops below what the air is carrying) and why the same 30 °C can be near its ceiling (muggy) or far below it (dry).

The pattern

Two simple rules run through all three observations, and the rest of the chapter is just naming them precisely.

  • Humidity as a percent is water held divided by the capacity at that temperature. The same amount of water is a small percent of a warm air's big capacity but a large percent of a cold air's small capacity. That is why two 30 °C days differ (same capacity, different water), and why a percent alone never tells you the actual amount. This ratio is relative humidity.
  • Cooling toward the dew point causes condensation. When air cools, the water it carries stays put but its capacity shrinks. Once the capacity drops to the amount of water present, the air is full, and any more cooling squeezes the extra out as liquid: dew, fog, or a cloud. Nothing was added. The air was only cooled. The temperature where the air becomes full is its dew point.

Now the concepts behind those two rules, with units and why each one matters.

The core idea

Water vapor is a gas. There is always some water in the air as an invisible gas called water vapor. You cannot see it. (The white you see rising from a kettle or a cloud in the sky is not vapor. It is tiny liquid droplets that have already condensed.) "Humidity" is just how much of this invisible water vapor the air is carrying. There are three different ways to measure it, and mixing them up is the number one mistake in this whole topic.

1. Absolute humidity: the actual amount

Absolute humidity is simply how much water vapor is in the air, measured as a weight in each cubic meter of air, in grams per cubic meter (g/m3). If a parcel of air holds 17 grams of water vapor in every cubic meter, its absolute humidity is 17 g/m3. This is a plain amount. It does not care about temperature. A close relative you may see on exams is specific humidity, the same idea measured per unit mass of air instead of per unit volume (grams of water vapor per kilogram of air). Both are direct amounts of moisture; this chapter uses absolute humidity in g/m3 throughout so the numbers line up with the saturation table below.

2. Saturation: the most the air can hold

Here is the key fact for the whole chapter. Warm air can hold much more water vapor than cold air. There is a maximum amount of vapor the air can hold at a given temperature. When the air is holding that maximum, we say it is saturated. The table below shows how fast that maximum climbs with temperature. Notice that the maximum at 30 °C is more than six times the maximum at 0 °C.

Air temperatureMost water vapor the air can hold (g/m3)
0 °Cabout 4.8
5 °Cabout 6.8
10 °Cabout 9.4
15 °Cabout 12.8
20 °Cabout 17.3
25 °Cabout 23.0
30 °Cabout 30.4

These are typical textbook values. Real numbers shift a little with air pressure, but the shape is always the same: the warmer the air, the far more vapor it can hold.

3. Relative humidity: the percent of the maximum

Relative humidity (RH) is what the weather report means when it says "humidity is 57 percent." It is the water vapor the air is actually holding, written as a percent of the maximum it could hold at its current temperature:

\[ \text{RH} = \frac{\text{water vapor now}}{\text{maximum at that temperature}} \times 100\% \]

Because the bottom of that fraction (the maximum) grows as the air warms, RH is a moving target. The same amount of water can be a high RH in cold air and a low RH in warm air. Keep this in your head: RH is a percentage, not an amount.

4. Dew point: the temperature of saturation

The dew point is the temperature the air would have to cool down to before it becomes saturated (RH reaches 100 percent), with no water added or removed. It sounds indirect, but it is actually the most honest single number for moisture, because it is a real amount in disguise. A high dew point always means a lot of water vapor. A low dew point always means a little. Unlike RH, the dew point does not change when you just warm or cool the air, so it is the moisture measure forecasters trust.

One handy shortcut ties them together. The spread is the air temperature minus the dew point.

  • A small spread (temperature close to dew point) means high RH: the air is near saturation.
  • A large spread means low RH: the air is far from saturation and feels dry.
  • When the spread is zero (temperature equals dew point), the air is saturated: RH is 100 percent, and water starts to condense.

The instrument: the hygrometer

A hygrometer is any instrument that measures humidity. The classic one is a sling psychrometer: two thermometers side by side, one with a wet cloth wick over its bulb. You swing them through the air. Water evaporates off the wet wick and cools that thermometer down (evaporation stores latent heat, from Chapter 3). Dry air makes water evaporate fast, so the wet thermometer reads much lower. Humid air makes it evaporate slowly, so the two thermometers read almost the same. The gap between the dry-bulb and wet-bulb readings is looked up in a table to give the relative humidity and the dew point.

Aha: absolute humidity is the amount of water. Dew point is the temperature at which that amount would saturate the air, so it is a direct moisture measure. Relative humidity is only a percent of a maximum that itself changes with temperature. Three different things.

Worked examples

All three examples follow Parcel P, our single parcel of air over Kansas City, on Day 1 of the cold-front episode: a warm, humid afternoon in the warm sector.

Example 1. On Day 1, Parcel P has an air temperature of 30 °C and a dew point of 20 °C. What is its relative humidity?

Step 1, find the actual water vapor. The dew point tells us this directly. The dew point is 20 °C, which means the air would saturate if cooled to 20 °C. So the amount of water vapor it is carrying equals the maximum that 20 °C air can hold: about 17.3 g/m3 from the table.

Step 2, find the maximum at the real temperature. The air is actually at 30 °C, and 30 °C air can hold about 30.4 g/m3.

Step 3, divide.

\[ \text{RH} = \frac{17.3\ \text{g/m}^3}{30.4\ \text{g/m}^3} \times 100\% \approx 57\% \]

So Parcel P is at about 57 percent relative humidity. The dew point of 20 °C is genuinely a lot of moisture (a muggy summer day), yet the RH is only in the fifties, because the warm 30 °C air could hold so much more.

Aha: to turn a temperature and dew point into RH, read the water amount at the dew point, read the maximum at the real temperature, and divide.

Example 2. That evening the ground cools and chills Parcel P. No water is added or removed, so its water vapor stays at 17.3 g/m3 (its dew point stays 20 °C). What happens to its relative humidity as it cools from 30 °C down toward 20 °C?

The water vapor amount on top of the fraction is frozen at 17.3 g/m3. Only the maximum on the bottom shrinks as the air cools. So the fraction climbs:

Temperature of Parcel PMaximum it can holdRelative humidity
30 °Cabout 30.4 g/m317.3 / 30.4 ≈ 57%
25 °Cabout 23.0 g/m317.3 / 23.0 ≈ 75%
20 °Cabout 17.3 g/m317.3 / 17.3 = 100%

When Parcel P reaches 20 °C, its own dew point, the RH hits 100 percent. The air is now saturated. Cool it even one degree more and it cannot hold all its water, so the extra vapor condenses into liquid: dew on the grass, or fog in the air, or, higher up, a cloud. Nothing was added to the air. We only cooled it.

Aha: cooling raises relative humidity. The air holds less at each colder step while the water stays the same, until it saturates at the dew point and condenses. This is why dew, fog, and clouds all form by cooling, not by "adding air."

Example 3. Compare two situations over Kansas City. (A) A cold winter dawn: temperature 0 °C, dew point 0 °C, so the air is foggy at 100 percent RH. (B) The Day 1 warm afternoon from Example 1: 30 °C at 57 percent RH. Which air actually holds more water?

Situation A is saturated (RH 100 percent), which sounds like "maximum water." But 0 °C air can only hold about 4.8 g/m3 in the first place, so being full means holding just 4.8 g/m3.

Situation B is "only" 57 percent RH, which sounds drier. But it is holding 17.3 g/m3, from Example 1.

\[ \frac{17.3\ \text{g/m}^3}{4.8\ \text{g/m}^3} \approx 3.6 \]

The warm 57 percent air is holding about 3.6 times as much water as the cold, foggy, 100 percent air. The high percentage fooled us. This is exactly why a freezing but "100 percent humid" winter morning is not muggy, while a summer afternoon at 57 percent is sticky and heavy.

Aha: 100 percent RH does not mean "a lot of water." In cold air it can mean very little. When you want to know how much moisture is really there, look at the dew point (or the absolute humidity), never the RH alone.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: thinking high relative humidity means lots of water. RH is only a percentage of the maximum, and that maximum collapses in cold air. Freezing air at 100 percent RH holds far less water than warm air at 50 percent (Example 3). Use the dew point when you want the real amount.
Pitfall 2: thinking you need to "add air" or "add moisture" to make it rain. The usual trigger for condensation is cooling. Cool humid air toward its dew point and it saturates on its own, no new water required. That is the whole mechanism behind dew, fog, and clouds.
Pitfall 3: confusing the three measures. Absolute humidity is an amount (g/m3). Dew point is a temperature (°C) and is a direct moisture measure. Relative humidity is a percent. They answer different questions. Do not report one when you mean another.

P1. Two parcels of air. Parcel A is at 25 °C with a dew point of 15 °C. Parcel B is at 15 °C with a dew point of 10 °C. (a) Which parcel is holding more water vapor? (b) Which parcel has the higher relative humidity? Use the table in the core idea.

Answer

(a) More water: compare the dew points. A dew point of 15 °C means the air holds about 12.8 g/m3 (Parcel A). A dew point of 10 °C means about 9.4 g/m3 (Parcel B). Parcel A holds more water.

(b) Higher RH: divide each parcel's water by its own maximum.

  • Parcel A: \(\frac{12.8}{23.0} \approx 56\%\) (25 °C air can hold about 23.0 g/m3).
  • Parcel B: \(\frac{9.4}{12.8} \approx 73\%\) (15 °C air can hold about 12.8 g/m3).

So Parcel B has the higher RH (73 percent versus 56 percent), even though it holds less water.

Why it works: the two questions measure different things. "More water" is answered by absolute amount, and the dew point reads that amount off directly, so the higher dew point wins. "Higher RH" is answered by how close each parcel is to its own saturation, which the spread tells you. Parcel B has a spread of only 5 °C (15 minus 10), so it is close to saturation and feels humid. Parcel A has a spread of 10 °C, so it is further from saturation even though it is carrying more total water. The colder parcel needs less water to feel "full."

Pattern, dew point for amount, spread for RH: when a problem gives you a temperature and a dew point, use the dew point (or the water amount it stands for) to compare how much moisture there is, and use the spread (temperature minus dew point) to compare relative humidity. A higher dew point can still go with a lower RH, so never rank moisture by the RH percentage. This is the same trap as Example 3, now with two parcels instead of one.

P2. On Day 1, Parcel P has a dew point of 20 °C. Overnight the ground cools it steadily. Explain what happens as it cools to 20 °C, and then what happens if it keeps cooling to 18 °C. Why do dew and fog form this way?

Answer

Down to 20 °C: the water vapor stays put (nothing is added or removed), so only the maximum shrinks as the air cools. The relative humidity climbs. At 20 °C, its dew point, the air is exactly saturated: RH is 100 percent.

Past 20 °C, down to 18 °C: now the air is colder than its dew point, so it can no longer hold all the vapor it carries. The maximum at 18 °C is smaller than 17.3 g/m3, so the leftover vapor must condense into liquid water. It shows up as dew on cold grass and surfaces, or as fog if it condenses on tiny particles in the air itself. Because water has left the air, the parcel's new dew point drops to 18 °C.

Why it works: condensation is not caused by adding water to the air. It is caused by taking away the air's ability to hold water, and cooling does exactly that, because warm air holds much more than cold air. Once the temperature falls to the dew point, the air is full, and any further cooling squeezes out the excess as liquid. Dew, fog, and clouds are all the same event at different heights: humid air cooled to its dew point.

Pattern, cool to the dew point to condense: whenever you want to know if or when water will condense, compare the temperature to the dew point, not to some fixed number. Condensation begins the moment the temperature reaches the dew point (spread equals zero, RH equals 100 percent). This is why clear calm nights, when the ground radiates heat away and cools the air fastest, are the ones that leave the heaviest dew and morning fog.

8. Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates

When a parcel of air rises, the lower pressure lets it puff out and cool, with no heat added or taken away. That cooling has a fixed speed, and the speed changes once clouds start to form.

Prerequisites: Chapter 5: Air Pressure · Chapter 7: Humidity
This chapter: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates
Unlocks: Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability · Chapter 10: Clouds

Start here: lift Parcel P over Kansas City

It is a warm afternoon over Kansas City. Parcel P, a small blob of air, sits at the surface at a temperature of 30 C with a dew point of 10 C. (The dew point is the temperature at which its water vapor would start to condense into droplets; see Chapter 7: Humidity.) A breeze pushes P up a hillside and it keeps climbing. What happens to its temperature as it goes up? Let us track it kilometer by kilometer, and then look for the pattern.

Careful measurements of real rising air give us two numbers to work with. While a parcel has not yet started making cloud, it cools by about 10 C for every kilometer it climbs. Once it starts making cloud, it cools more slowly, by about 6 C per kilometer. We will see why in a moment. First, just use the numbers and watch what happens.

Step 1: cool at 10 C per km until cloud starts. P begins with its temperature (30 C) well above its dew point (10 C), so it is not making cloud yet. It cools at about 10 C per km:

  • At 1 km: \(30 - 10 \times 1 = 20\) C.
  • At 2 km: \(30 - 10 \times 2 = 10\) C.

Step 2: find where cloud starts. Cloud forms the instant the parcel's temperature falls to its dew point, because that is when the vapor begins to condense. The dew point here is 10 C, and P reaches 10 C at 2 km. So the cloud base forms at 2 km. That is the flat bottom of a fair-weather cumulus cloud. (To keep the arithmetic clean we treat the dew point as roughly steady as the parcel rises. In real air the dew point drops a little too, so the true cloud base is usually a bit higher. The method is exactly the same.)

Step 3: above the cloud base, cool at 6 C per km. From 2 km up, P is making cloud, so it now cools at the slower rate of about 6 C per km. Starting from 10 C at 2 km:

  • At 3 km: \(10 - 6 \times 1 = 4\) C.
  • At 4 km: \(10 - 6 \times 2 = -2\) C (minus 2 C).
  • At 5 km: \(10 - 6 \times 3 = -8\) C (minus 8 C).

Name the pattern

Two things stand out from that climb, and together they are the whole chapter.

First: P cooled even though nothing touched it. No cold wind blew on it, and no ice was added. It cooled all by itself, just from rising. Here is why. Air pressure drops as you go up (that was Chapter 5: Air Pressure). So as P rises, the outside air squeezing it from all sides gets weaker. With less to hold it in, P puffs out and takes up more room. Puffing out is work, and doing that work uses up some of the parcel's own energy. When a gas spends its own energy expanding, it gets colder. So rising air cools on its own.

Second: the cooling had two speeds, with a bend where cloud began. Below 2 km the temperature fell fast, about 10 C per km. Above 2 km it fell more slowly, about 6 C per km. The graph of temperature versus height has a visible kink right at the cloud base. Same parcel, two speeds, and the bend marks where the cloud starts to form.

Aha: a rising parcel cools by expanding, with no heat added or removed. It cools fast while it is still clear, then slower once it starts making cloud. The height where the two speeds meet is the flat bottom of the cloud.

The idea, named

Now we can name what we just watched.

Adiabatic. When a change happens with no heat added and no heat removed, we call it adiabatic. Rising air cools adiabatically: it loses temperature purely by expanding, not because any heat left it. Sinking air is the opposite. As it sinks into higher pressure, the outside air squeezes it smaller, that squeezing packs its energy tighter, and it warms up adiabatically.

Say it out loud so it sticks: rising air cools, sinking air warms. This is a common thing to get backwards, so tie it to the picture: going up means lower pressure means expand means cool.

The two lapse rates

A lapse rate is how fast temperature changes with height, in degrees per kilometer. The two speeds we just used each have a name, and which one a rising parcel uses depends on whether a cloud is forming inside it.

  • Dry adiabatic lapse rate (DALR), about 10 C per km. This is the speed for unsaturated air, meaning air that has not started making cloud droplets yet. A rising unsaturated parcel cools about 10 C for every kilometer it climbs. (The precise value is close to 9.8 C per km; we round it to 10 for arithmetic. "Dry" here just means "no condensation happening," even if there is water vapor mixed in.)
  • Moist adiabatic lapse rate (MALR), about 6 C per km. Once the parcel has cooled enough that its water vapor starts condensing into droplets, condensation releases latent heat (that stored heat was the subject of Chapter 3: Heat on the Move). That released heat warms the parcel a little from the inside, so it fights the cooling. The parcel keeps cooling, just slower: about 6 C per km instead of 10. (The exact value drifts between about 5 and 6 C per km. It is smallest in warm, humid air, where there is the most vapor to condense and so the most heat handed back.)

So the moist rate is the smaller number because condensation gives back heat. That is the whole reason: released latent heat slows the cooling.

We can write both the same way. If a parcel rises a height \(h\) (in km) at a lapse rate \(L\) (in C per km):

\[ \text{temperature at the top} = \text{start temperature} - L \times h \]

Use \(L = 10\) while the parcel is unsaturated, and \(L = 6\) once it is making cloud. The height where a rising parcel first reaches its dew point and cloud begins, where the rate switches from 10 to 6, has a name: the lifting condensation level (LCL). For Parcel P it was 2 km. The LCL is where the flat bottom of a fair-weather cumulus cloud sits. A handy shortcut: the cloud base height in km is roughly the surface temperature minus the dew point (the "spread"), divided by 10.

Do not confuse these with the environmental lapse rate. The two rates above (10 and 6) belong to a parcel that is moving up. The environmental lapse rate (ELR) is a totally different thing: it is the temperature of the still, surrounding air at each height, as actually measured by a weather balloon. It is whatever the atmosphere happens to be that day (often somewhere near 6 to 7 C per km, about 6.5 C per km on average, but it can be anything). It is a measurement of the surroundings, not a rule a parcel follows. That is the dashed gray line in the plot above. Keep three separate ideas: dry rate (10, a moving unsaturated parcel), moist rate (6, a moving cloud-making parcel), environmental rate (measured, the air standing around the parcel).

Worked examples

Example 1. Sinking warms it back. Suppose Parcel P does not make it far and sinks straight back down from 2 km to the surface, without losing any water. What happens to its temperature?

Sinking air moves into higher pressure, so the surroundings squeeze the parcel smaller. That squeezing warms it. As long as the parcel is unsaturated (no cloud), it warms at the dry rate, about 10 C per km, the exact reverse of the way up.

  • At 2 km it was 10 C.
  • Down to 1 km: \(10 + 10 \times 1 = 20\) C.
  • Down to the surface: \(10 + 10 \times 2 = 30\) C, right back where it started.
Aha: for unsaturated air the process runs both ways. Up cools, down warms, at the same 10 C per km. This is why sinking air under a high-pressure system (Chapter 5) warms and dries out, which clears the sky. (If P had rained out its water on the way up, it would come back down warmer and drier than it left, since it would warm at the dry rate the whole way. That is how a downslope wind like the Chinook warms the plains.)

Example 2. Three numbers, three meanings. On this same afternoon a weather balloon over Kansas City measures the surrounding air. It reports that the still air cools about 7 C per km going up. A student says, "So the parcel must cool at 7 C per km too." Why is that wrong?

The 7 C per km is the environmental lapse rate. It describes the air that is just standing there, and it is whatever the atmosphere happens to be today. It has nothing to do with how a moving parcel cools. Parcel P still cools at its own rates: 10 C per km while unsaturated, then 6 C per km once it is making cloud. So on this day there are three different numbers:

  • 10 C per km: the dry rate, for the rising unsaturated parcel.
  • 6 C per km: the moist rate, for the rising parcel once cloud forms.
  • 7 C per km: the measured environmental rate, for the still surrounding air.
Aha: the environmental rate is a measurement of the neighborhood, not a rule the parcel obeys. Comparing the parcel's temperature to the environment's at each height is exactly how you decide whether the parcel keeps rising or sinks back. That comparison is the whole next chapter, Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability.

Common pitfalls

  • Saying rising air warms. It is the reverse. Rising means lower pressure, so the parcel expands and cools. Sinking means higher pressure, so it is squeezed and warms. If you ever mix this up, redraw the pressure picture from Chapter 5.
  • Mixing up the three rates. The dry rate (10) and moist rate (6) belong to a moving parcel. The environmental rate (measured, often near 6 or 7) belongs to the still surrounding air. They are different ideas even when the numbers look close.
  • Forgetting to switch rates at the cloud base. Use 10 C per km only up to the lifting condensation level. Above it, once condensation starts, switch to 6 C per km. Using one rate the whole way gives the wrong temperature aloft.
  • Thinking heat sneaks in. Adiabatic means no heat is added or removed. The temperature changes only because the parcel expands or is squeezed. The latent heat in the moist case is not new heat from outside; it was already stored in the parcel's own water vapor and gets released inside it.

Practice

P1. Parcel P starts at the Kansas City surface at 28 C with a dew point of 18 C. It is lifted all the way to 3 km. What is its temperature at the top? (Assume the dew point stays about steady.)

Answer

The calculation. The spread between temperature and dew point is \(28 - 18 = 10\) C. At the dry rate of 10 C per km, the parcel closes that 10 C gap after 1 km, so the cloud base (LCL) is at 1 km, where the temperature is 18 C. From 1 km to 3 km (2 more km) it cools at the moist rate of 6 C per km: \(18 - 6 \times 2 = 6\) C. Temperature at 3 km is 6 C.

Why it works: a lifted parcel always cools in two stages. Below the cloud base it is unsaturated, so it uses the fast dry rate. The gap between its temperature and its dew point tells you how far it can climb before it saturates, because the temperature falls toward the dew point at the dry rate. Once they meet, cloud forms and every kilometer after that uses the slower moist rate, since the condensing water keeps handing heat back.

Pattern, the two-slope lift: for any "lift a parcel to height H" problem, first find the cloud base (drop at 10 C per km until temperature equals dew point), then finish the rest of the climb at 6 C per km. Do the dry part and the moist part separately and add up the heights. Reach for this whenever a climb crosses the cloud base. If the whole climb stays below the cloud base, you never switch and it is pure dry rate the entire way.

P2. Parcel P leaves the surface at 22 C with a dew point of 12 C. About how high is the cloud base (the lifting condensation level)? (Assume the dew point stays about steady.)

AnswerSpread is \(22 - 12 = 10\) C. At the dry rate of 10 C per km, the parcel reaches its dew point after \(10 \div 10 = 1\) km. Cloud base is at about 1 km.

P3. Two parcels each rise exactly 1 km. One is unsaturated the whole way. The other is already saturated and making cloud the whole way. Which one ends up colder, and why?

Answer

The calculation. The unsaturated parcel cools at the dry rate: 10 C in that 1 km. The saturated parcel cools at the moist rate: only 6 C in that 1 km. So the unsaturated (dry) parcel ends up colder, by about 4 C.

Why it works: both parcels lose energy by expanding as they rise, which is what cools them. But inside the saturated parcel, water vapor is condensing, and condensation releases latent heat. That released heat warms the parcel from the inside and partly cancels the expansion cooling. The dry parcel has no such heat source, so nothing slows its cooling and it drops the full 10 C.

Pattern, latent heat is a brake on cooling: whenever condensation is happening, expect slower cooling (about 6 C per km), and whenever it is not, expect the full cooling (about 10 C per km). The presence of condensation, not the amount of water alone, is what sets which rate you use. This is also why tall storm clouds can keep rising so far: the moist rate keeps the rising air relatively warm compared to a dry climb, so it stays buoyant longer. That buoyancy story is Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability.

9. Atmospheric Stability

Give a parcel of air a small push upward. Does it keep rising on its own, or does it sink back to where it started? That is what stability tells you.

This node: Atmospheric Stability
Unlocks: Chapter 10: Clouds (unstable air lifts parcels until they condense) · Chapter 15: Severe Weather I (deep instability builds thunderstorms)

See three parcels first

Picture Parcel P, a small bubble of air sitting over Kansas City. Give it a small shove upward, maybe by a hill, a warm patch of ground, or a front sliding underneath. Two things happen as it climbs. First, the parcel cools as it rises. While it is not yet forming cloud it cools at the dry adiabatic rate of about 10°C per kilometer, the rule from Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates. Second, the air around it, called the environment, already has its own temperature at each height, which a weather balloon measures that morning. These are two separate numbers, so keep them apart.

Do not reach for a rule yet. Work through three real Kansas City soundings below. At the new height, ask just one question each time: is the lifted parcel warmer or cooler than the air right next to it? After the third case the pattern will name itself.

Example 1. Unstable air, a hot Kansas City afternoon. The ground has baked all day. A weather balloon shows the environment is 30°C at the surface and 18°C one kilometer up (a steep environmental lapse rate of 12°C per km). Parcel P starts at the surface at 30°C and gets nudged up one kilometer. Does it keep rising?

Step 1, cool the parcel. Rising air cools at the dry rate of 10°C per km, so after one kilometer the parcel is \(30 - 10 = 20\)°C.

Step 2, read the environment. At one kilometer the air around it is 18°C.

Step 3, compare. Buoyancy \(= 20 - 18 = +2\)°C. The parcel is 2°C warmer than its surroundings.

Verdict: warmer means lighter means it keeps rising on its own. The air is unstable. The environment cooled 12°C while the parcel only cooled 10°C, so the parcel won the race and stayed warmer.

Aha: when the environment cools faster than the parcel, the parcel is left warmer and lighter, so it takes off on its own. Hot afternoons over Kansas City do exactly this, which is why summer storms fire in the heat.

Example 2. Stable air, a calm Kansas City morning. The balloon shows the environment is 10°C at the surface and 6°C one kilometer up (a gentle environmental lapse rate of 4°C per km). Parcel P starts at 10°C and is nudged up one kilometer. Does it keep rising?

Step 1, cool the parcel. At the dry rate of 10°C per km, one kilometer up it is \(10 - 10 = 0\)°C.

Step 2, read the environment. At one kilometer the surrounding air is 6°C.

Step 3, compare. Buoyancy \(= 0 - 6 = -6\)°C. The parcel is 6°C cooler than its surroundings.

Verdict: cooler means heavier means it sinks straight back down. The air is stable. The environment barely cooled (only 4°C), but the parcel cooled the full 10°C, so the parcel lost the race and ended up colder.

Aha: when the environment cools slowly, the parcel out-cools it and turns heavy, so it settles. Stable mornings give flat, layered clouds and haze, not towering storms.

Example 3. A temperature inversion, the strongest cap of all. On a still Kansas City morning the balloon shows the environment is 10°C at the surface but 14°C at half a kilometer up. The temperature is rising with height. Parcel P starts at 10°C and is nudged up half a kilometer. What happens?

Step 1, cool the parcel. Half a kilometer at the dry rate of 10°C per km is a 5°C drop, so the parcel is \(10 - 5 = 5\)°C.

Step 2, read the environment. At half a kilometer the surrounding air is 14°C, warmer than at the ground. That warm-over-cold layer is the inversion.

Step 3, compare. Buoyancy \(= 5 - 14 = -9\)°C. The parcel is a huge 9°C cooler than its surroundings.

Verdict: the parcel is much heavier than the warm layer above it, so it drops right back down. This is very stable. An inversion acts like a lid on the atmosphere. Air cannot punch up through it, so smoke, haze, and car exhaust pile up in the cool layer underneath.

Aha: an inversion is upside-down air, warm sitting on top of cold. It is the ultimate cap. Nothing rises through it, so it locks pollution near the ground until the sun or a front breaks it.

The pattern: compare the parcel to its neighbors

All three cases turned on the same single move. You lifted the parcel, cooled it at 10°C per km, then set its temperature beside the environment's temperature at that same height and subtracted. The sign of that one subtraction decided everything:

\[ \text{buoyancy} = T_{\text{parcel}} - T_{\text{environment}} \]
  • Parcel warmer (buoyancy positive): warm air is less dense, so it floats up like a hot-air balloon and keeps rising on its own. This is UNSTABLE air (Example 1). It favors clouds and storms.
  • Parcel cooler (buoyancy negative): it is heavier than its surroundings, so it sinks back to where it started. This is STABLE air (Examples 2 and 3). It favors calm, clear, layered skies.
  • Same temperature (buoyancy zero): it just floats there. This is neutral, and it is where a rising parcel finally runs out of push.

Notice what stability is not. It is never about whether the parcel is warm or cold on its own. The 20°C parcel rose in Example 1 because it sat next to 18°C air, but that very same 20°C parcel would sink next to 25°C air. Only the comparison at equal height matters.

The stability criteria

You do not always need both temperatures. Since the parcel's cooling rate is fixed, you can compare the environment's cooling rate directly to the parcel's. Three rates are in play, all measured in °C per km:

  • ELR, the environmental lapse rate: how fast the surrounding air actually cools with height (the balloon profile). It changes day to day, and even layer to layer.
  • DALR, the dry adiabatic lapse rate: about 10°C per km, the rate an unsaturated parcel cools.
  • MALR, the moist adiabatic lapse rate: about 6°C per km, the rate a saturated parcel cools once it is making cloud. It is slower because condensation releases latent heat (see Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates).

Slide the ELR against those two fixed parcel rates and four cases fall out:

  • Absolutely unstable: ELR greater than DALR (environment steeper than about 10°C per km). The environment cools faster than even a dry parcel, so any lifted parcel stays warmer and keeps rising, cloud or no cloud. Example 1 (ELR 12°C per km) is this case.
  • Absolutely stable: ELR less than MALR (environment gentler than about 6°C per km). The environment cools slower than even a saturated parcel, so any lifted parcel ends up cooler and sinks, cloud or no cloud. Example 2 (ELR 4°C per km) is this case.
  • Conditionally unstable: ELR between MALR and DALR (between about 6 and 10°C per km). Now the verdict depends on the parcel. A dry parcel cools at 10°C per km, faster than the environment, so it ends up cooler and the air is stable. But if that same parcel saturates and starts making cloud, it cools at only about 6°C per km, slower than the environment, so it ends up warmer and the air turns unstable. Condensation is the switch. This is the usual state of the real lower atmosphere, and it is why a humid day can stay quiet until a parcel is lifted far enough to make cloud, then erupt into a storm (Chapters 10 and 15).
  • Inversion: ELR negative (temperature rises with height). The strongest stable case of all, the far end of absolutely stable. A lifted parcel becomes far cooler than the warm layer above and is shoved straight back. It acts as a lid and traps haze. Example 3 is this case.

Why the rate test works. A parcel lifted from the surface starts at the same temperature as the air around it. From that shared start, whichever one cools faster per km is the colder one at every height above. So when the environment cools faster (large ELR), the parcel is left warmer and rises. When the parcel cools faster (small ELR), the parcel ends up colder and sinks. Comparing the rates is just the temperature comparison done once for all heights.

One assumption to state out loud: the dry rate (10°C per km) applies while Parcel P is unsaturated. The moment it saturates and condenses it switches to the moist rate (about 6°C per km), which is exactly what makes the conditionally unstable case flip. That boundary is the bridge into Chapter 10: Clouds.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1, comparing the parcel to the wrong thing. Stability is never about whether the parcel is warm or cold on its own. It is always the parcel versus the environment at the same height. A 20°C parcel is buoyant next to 18°C air but sinks next to 25°C air. Always line them up at the same height and subtract.
Pitfall 2, mixing up the two rates. The parcel cools at the adiabatic rate (about 10°C per km when dry). The environment follows the environmental lapse rate, which is whatever the balloon actually measured. These are two separate numbers. Never use one where you need the other.
Pitfall 3, thinking rising air warms. Rising air always cools, and sinking air always warms. If you ever find a parcel getting warmer as it goes up, you have made a sign error. Fix it before comparing.

Practice

P1. Read a full profile and decide stability at each level. A Kansas City balloon measures the environment as 28°C at the surface, 16°C at 1 km, 8°C at 2 km, and 10°C at 3 km. Parcel P leaves the surface at 28°C and is lifted at the dry rate of 10°C per km. At each level, is the air unstable, neutral, or stable, and where does the parcel stop?

Answer

The calculation. Cool the parcel at 10°C per km from 28°C: it is 28 at 0 km, \(28-10=18\) at 1 km, \(28-20=8\) at 2 km, and \(28-30=-2\) at 3 km (that is 2°C below zero). Now line the parcel up against the environment at each level and subtract:

  • 1 km: parcel 18°C, environment 16°C. Buoyancy \(=18-16=+2\)°C. Parcel warmer, so unstable. It keeps rising.
  • 2 km: parcel 8°C, environment 8°C. Buoyancy \(=8-8=0\). Neutral. The parcel has run out of push here.
  • 3 km: parcel is minus 2°C, environment 10°C. Buoyancy \(=-2-10=-12\)°C. Parcel far cooler, so stable. Notice the environment warmed from 8°C to 10°C between 2 and 3 km, so there is an inversion capping the top.

The parcel rises freely from the surface through the unstable lower layer, coasts to a stop around 2 km where it matches the environment, and cannot break through the inversion above.

Why it works. You are not judging the parcel's temperature on its own. At every height you place the parcel next to the real air around it and ask which is lighter. The parcel always cools at the same steady 10°C per km, but the environment cools by different amounts in different layers. Where the environment drops faster than the parcel (28 to 16 is 12°C in the first km), the parcel is left warmer and floats up. Where the environment drops slower or turns around and warms (8 up to 10), the parcel falls behind, turns colder and heavier, and can rise no more. The place where the two temperatures cross is where free rising ends.

Pattern, walk the profile level by level and compare, do not judge the whole thing at once: lay the parcel's cooling line (a steady 10°C per km) beside the measured environment line, then read them height by height. The air can be unstable low down and stable higher up in the very same sounding. The crossing point (equal temperatures) is the ceiling for free rising, and any inversion above it is a hard lid. Reach for this whenever you are handed a table of temperatures at several heights: never label the whole column with one word, because stability changes layer by layer.

P2. Why does a temperature inversion trap pollution near the ground?

Answer

In an inversion the air gets warmer as you go up, so warm light air sits on top of cool heavier air. Smoke and exhaust near the ground are in that cool bottom layer. To carry the pollution away, a parcel would have to rise, but the moment it rises it cools at the dry rate and becomes even colder than the warm lid above it. That makes it heavier than its surroundings, so it sinks right back. Nothing can lift up through the warm layer, so the pollution has nowhere to go and builds up underneath. That is why calm winter mornings with an inversion often bring the haziest, smoggiest air, and it usually clears only once the sun heats the ground enough to erase the inversion.

P3. Kansas City has a hot, humid afternoon and the environment is cooling quickly with height. Why does that raise the chance of a thunderstorm?

Answer

Quick cooling with height means the environment drops faster than the parcel does, so a lifted parcel stays warmer than its surroundings. That is unstable air, and an unstable parcel keeps rising on its own instead of sinking back. If it stays warmer for many kilometers, it climbs high and fast, and the humid air condenses into a tall cloud that can grow into a thunderstorm (Chapter 15). Strong instability is the fuel; the hot, humid surface loads that fuel. Calm, stable days do the opposite: parcels sink back, so storms cannot build.

10. Clouds

A cloud is what you see when rising air cools down to its dew point and its water vapor turns into millions of tiny floating droplets. Lift the air, cool it, and the cloud draws itself. Then every cloud you can name is sorted by just two things: how high its base sits and what shape it takes.

See two skies first

Do not reach for the cloud-naming chart yet. Watch two ordinary things happen in the sky over Kansas City. First, watch a single cloud switch on out of clear air. Then look up at four different clouds and see what tells them apart. After both, the whole system for how clouds form and how they are named will name itself.

Observation 1. A cloud switches on at one height. It is a warm Kansas City afternoon. A blob of humid air, Parcel P, sits at the ground at 30 C with a dew point of 10 C. The sun-baked ground makes P buoyant and it floats upward. There is nothing but clear blue sky, and then at one clean height a flat-bottomed cotton-ball cloud appears. Watch it happen, and find the height of that flat cloud base.

Step 1: lift. The hot ground warms P, P becomes lighter than the air around it, and it rises on its own. Nothing else is needed to start the process.

Step 2: cool. As P rises it moves into lower pressure, expands, and cools with no heat added. This is the adiabatic cooling from Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes. While it is still clear it cools at the dry rate of about 10 C per km. At 1 km it is \(30 - 10 \times 1 = 20\) C. At 2 km it is \(30 - 10 \times 2 = 10\) C.

Step 3: condense. At 2 km the parcel's temperature has fallen to 10 C, which is exactly its dew point. Now recall the rule from Chapter 7: Humidity: warm air can hold more water vapor than cold air. P has cooled until it can no longer hold all the vapor it started with, so the extra vapor changes back into liquid. That change from vapor to liquid is condensation. It condenses onto tiny floating specks into droplets, and a cloud appears. The base of that cloud sits right at 2 km.

That height where condensation begins has a name: the lifting condensation level, or LCL. It is the flat bottom of the cloud. You can also get it straight from the numbers. The gap between the temperature and the dew point at the ground is the spread. Since a clear parcel cools about 10 C for every kilometer it climbs, it takes this many kilometers to close that gap:

\[ \text{cloud base height (km)} = \frac{\text{temperature} - \text{dew point}}{10 \text{ C per km}} \]

For Parcel P the spread is \(30 - 10 = 20\) C, and \(20 \div 10 = 2\) km. Cloud base at 2 km, which matches. (To keep the arithmetic clean we treat the dew point as roughly steady while the parcel rises. In real air it drops a little too, so the true base is usually a bit higher. The method is the same.)

Aha: the cloud did not turn on because water was added. Nobody poured water into Parcel P. It turned on because the air got cold enough. Cooling to the dew point is the switch, and it flips at one clean height, the LCL, which is why fair-weather cumulus clouds all have flat bottoms at the same level.

Observation 2. Four clouds, told apart by height and shape. Look up at four different skies over Kansas City. Do not name them yet, just note two things about each: how high it sits, and what shape it has. (a) Thin, feathery white streaks very high up, so cold they are made of ice. (b) Puffy, flat-bottomed cotton balls low in the sky on a nice afternoon. (c) A gray, featureless sheet at mid height that turns the sun into a dim disk. (d) A huge cauliflower tower with a dark base, growing up past 12 km, with rain falling out the bottom.

Two facts sort all four: the height of the cloud (high, mid, or low) and its form (a puffy heap, a flat layer, or a thin wisp). Line them up:

  • (a) Very high, thin and wispy, made of ice. High wispy clouds are the cirrus family.
  • (b) Low, and a puffy heap. Low puffy heaps are cumulus.
  • (c) Mid height, and a flat featureless layer. A mid-level layer is an altostratus.
  • (d) A puffy heap again, but towering from a low base past 12 km and raining. That is a cumulonimbus, the thunderstorm cloud.
Aha: you did not have to memorize four whole names. You read two dials on each cloud, height and shape, and the name fell out. Every cloud in the sky is filed by those same two dials, plus one extra flag for whether it is raining. The chart below sorts the common clouds by height.

Both skies obeyed the same rule. A cloud is water vapor that turned back into liquid after rising air cooled to its dew point. That gives the two big ideas of the whole chapter:

  • How a cloud forms. Lift the air, and it cools on its own. When it cools to its dew point, its vapor condenses into droplets. The bottom of the cloud sits at the height where that happens, the lifting condensation level (LCL). Observation 1 was this rule at work.
  • How a cloud is named. Once clouds exist, every one you can point to is filed by just two things: how high its base sits and what form it takes (a puffy heap, a flat layer, or a thin wisp). A third flag says whether it is raining. Observation 2 was this filing system at work.

The rest of the chapter unpacks each idea: first how clouds form and why, then the naming scheme in full.

How clouds form, and why

Put the process in three steps. This is the whole recipe for a cloud, and every cloud runs it:

  1. Lift. Something pushes the air upward.
  2. Cool. The rising air expands and cools adiabatically, about 10 C per kilometer while it is still clear.
  3. Condense. When the air cools to its dew point, its vapor condenses into droplets. That height is the cloud base, the LCL.

Why cooling, not adding water, makes the cloud. This is the one idea to hold onto. Warm air can hold more water vapor than cold air. So as a parcel cools, its ability to hold vapor shrinks. At some height it gets so cold that it can no longer hold all the vapor it started with, and the extra has to change back into liquid. Nobody poured water in. The parcel simply got cold enough that the vapor it already carried could not stay a gas. Lift makes the cooling, and cooling makes the cloud.

Why droplets need a speck to grow on. Water vapor does not condense into thin air by itself. It needs a tiny solid surface to collect on, like a speck of dust, salt from sea spray, smoke, or pollen. These specks are called condensation nuclei, and there are millions of them in every breath of air. Vapor condenses onto them and builds up into cloud droplets, each far too small and light to fall. Billions of these droplets floating together are what we see as a cloud. (Getting them big enough to fall is the job of Chapter 11: Precipitation.)

Four ways to lift the air

Step 1 is the trigger. Anything that pushes air upward can start a cloud. There are four common lifters, and all four do the same job: raise the air until it cools to its dew point.

  • Convection. The sun heats the ground, the ground heats the air, and the warm buoyant air rises like a hot-air balloon. This makes the puffy fair-weather clouds you see on a sunny afternoon. This is how Parcel P gets lifted over a hot Kansas City field.
  • Fronts. When a cold air mass pushes in, the heavier cold air slides under the warm air and shoves it upward. When warm air advances, it glides up and over the cooler air ahead. Either way the warm air is forced to rise. Fronts are the subject of Chapter 12: Air Masses and Fronts.
  • Mountains (orographic lift). Wind hits a mountain or a range of hills and has nowhere to go but up the slope. The air rises as it climbs the terrain, cools, and forms clouds on the upwind side.
  • Convergence. When winds blow toward the same spot from different directions, the air piles up and the only escape is upward. This happens in a low-pressure center, which is why lows are cloudy (see Chapter 5: Air Pressure).

How clouds are named

Cloud names look like a foreign language, but they are just a few small word parts stuck together. Learn the handful of parts and you can read almost any cloud name. There are three kinds of parts: a form root for the shape, a height prefix for how high it sits, and a rain word for whether it is precipitating.

Form roots (what the cloud looks like). These come from Latin:

  • Cumulo- (or cumulus) means "heap." These are the puffy, lumpy, cotton-ball clouds with flat bottoms.
  • Strato- (or stratus) means "layer" or "spread out." These are flat, featureless sheets that spread across the sky.
  • Cirro- (or cirrus) means "curl" or "wisp." These are thin, feathery streaks high up. They are so cold up there that they are made of ice crystals, not water droplets.

Height families (how high the cloud sits). Meteorologists sort clouds into four height groups:

  • High clouds get the prefix cirro- (or they are the cirrus family). They sit above about 6 km and are made of ice.
  • Mid clouds get the prefix alto-. They sit roughly between 2 km and 6 km.
  • Low clouds get no height prefix at all. They sit below about 2 km. Plain "cumulus" and "stratus" are low clouds.
  • Clouds of vertical development do not fit one shelf, because they span many heights at once. A cumulus has a low base but can build tall, and a cumulonimbus runs from a low base all the way past 12 km. These are the towering, puffy clouds, and they are the ones that make storms.

The rain word. The part nimbo- at the front or -nimbus at the end means the cloud is raining or snowing right now. So a nimbostratus is a rain-producing layer cloud, and a cumulonimbus is a puffy cloud that has grown into a towering thunderstorm and is dumping rain. Snap the parts together and the name describes the cloud: alto- (mid) + stratus (layer) is a mid-level sheet, and cumulo- (heap) + nimbus (raining) is a raining thunderhead.

Worked examples

Example 1. Same recipe, four different lifters. On four different days, air over the Great Plains gets lifted in four different ways: (a) the sun bakes a Kansas City field on a July afternoon; (b) a cold front pushes into town; (c) wind blows up against the Rocky Mountains; (d) winds spiral into a low-pressure center. In each case, why does a cloud form?

The answer is the same every time. Each lifter raises the air, the rising air cools adiabatically, and when it reaches its dew point the vapor condenses. Only the push is different.

  • (a) Convection. The hot ground makes the air buoyant, so it rises on its own. Puffy cumulus clouds pop up over the warmest spots.
  • (b) Front. The advancing cold air is heavier, so it wedges under the warm air and shoves it up. The forced lift makes a line of clouds along the front.
  • (c) Mountains. The wind cannot go through the mountain, so it rides up the slope. Rising up the slope cools it, and clouds form on the upwind side.
  • (d) Convergence. Winds flowing in toward the low from all sides pile up, and the crowded air has to rise. That is why a low-pressure center is cloudy.
Aha: there is really only one way to make a cloud, and it is lifting the air. The four "mechanisms" are just four ways to get the air moving upward. Once it rises, the same lift, cool, condense recipe takes over.

Example 2. A humid morning, a lower cloud base. On a muggy Kansas City morning a parcel of air leaves the surface at 24 C with a dew point of 18 C. It is lifted by a gentle breeze up a slope. At what height does its cloud base form, and why is it lower than Parcel P's 2 km base from the afternoon?

Find the spread, then divide by the dry rate. The spread between temperature and dew point is \(24 - 18 = 6\) C. A clear parcel cools about 10 C per km, so it closes that 6 C gap after \(6 \div 10 = 0.6\) km. The cloud base forms at about 0.6 km.

It is lower than the afternoon base because the morning air is more humid. A small spread means the air starts close to saturation, so only a little lifting and cooling is needed to reach the dew point. Parcel P had a 20 C spread on a drier afternoon, so it had to climb all the way to 2 km before it saturated.

Aha: the cloud base tracks the spread. Humid air (small spread) makes low cloud bases; drier air (large spread) makes high ones. That is why cloud bases hang low on muggy days and ride high on dry ones.

Common pitfalls

  • Thinking a cloud forms because water was added. It is the reverse. The air cooled until the water it already carried could no longer stay a gas. Condensation comes from cooling, not from pouring in more moisture. Warm air holds more vapor, so cooling it is what pushes it past its limit.
  • Saying warm air holds less moisture. Warm air holds MORE. That is exactly why lifting and cooling a warm humid parcel makes a cloud: cooling shrinks how much it can hold until the extra condenses.
  • Mixing up the height prefixes. "Cirro" and the cirrus family mean high. "Alto" means mid, not high, even though the word sounds like "altitude." No prefix means low. When in doubt, sort by height first, then add the shape word.
  • Forgetting the rain word. "Nimbo" or "nimbus" in a name means it is precipitating right now. A nimbostratus is a raining layer, and a cumulonimbus is a raining thunderhead. A plain stratus or cumulus is not raining.
  • Thinking clouds need dust to be dirty air. Condensation nuclei are normal and everywhere: dust, sea salt, pollen, smoke. Without them the vapor would struggle to condense at all. Clean-looking air still has millions of these specks.

P2. Why is the cloud base flat? Over a Kansas City field on a calm afternoon, dozens of little cumulus clouds all have their flat bottoms lined up at the very same height. Why does every cloud base sit at the same level instead of at random heights?

Answer

The answer. Because they all start condensing at the same height, the lifting condensation level (LCL). Over one field the air near the ground has about the same temperature and the same dew point everywhere. Each rising parcel cools at the same dry rate (about 10 C per km) as it climbs, so each one reaches its dew point after climbing the same distance. That shared height is where every parcel's vapor begins to condense, so every cloud base lines up there.

Why it works: the cloud base is not a random height. It is the exact height where cooling equals the spread between temperature and dew point. Since the ground air is well mixed, all the parcels share nearly the same spread, so \(\text{cloud base} = (\text{temperature} - \text{dew point}) \div 10\) gives the same answer for all of them. Below that height the air is still clear (too warm to condense). At that height it condenses. So the bottom of the cloud is sharp and flat, not fuzzy.

Pattern, one condition sets one surface: whenever a single condition (here, "cooled to the dew point") switches on at one value across a whole region, it draws a flat surface at that value. This is the same reason a lake has a flat top: one shared rule (water level) marks one shared height. Reach for this idea whenever many things line up at the same level. Ask what one condition they all just crossed.

11. Precipitation

A cloud is made of droplets far too small to fall. Before anything can reach the ground, those droplets have to grow about a million times bigger. What lands (rain, snow, sleet, or freezing rain) is then decided by the warm and cold layers the falling drop passes through on the way down.

See what falls first

Two everyday puzzles open this chapter. First: a cloud is packed with water, yet it can float overhead for an hour without a single drop reaching you. Second: in one winter storm the very same snow forming up inside the cloud can land at your feet as snow, as ordinary rain, as little bouncing ice pellets, or as an invisible sheet of ice on the roads. Both are settled below with real Kansas City numbers.

Do not reach for a rule yet. Work through the two cases first, each with its own figure. After them, two patterns name themselves: nothing falls until the droplets grow enormously, and what type lands is decided by the warm and cold layers the falling particle passes through on the way down.

Example 1. Why the cloud does not just fall, and how it finally rains. A summer cumulus cloud over Kansas City is full of water, yet it floats for an hour before the first drops fall. Explain why, and describe the two ways its droplets can grow big enough to rain.

Why it floats. The droplets are about 0.02 mm across, so light that the cloud's own rising air holds them up, and they would evaporate before reaching the ground anyway. Nothing can fall until the droplets grow. A raindrop is about 2 mm across, 100 times wider, which is \(100\times100\times100 = 1{,}000{,}000\) times the volume. So each raindrop is roughly a million cloud droplets gathered together.

Route one, collision and coalescence. In the warm lower part of the cloud (above freezing), the slightly bigger droplets fall a little faster and run into the smaller ones, merging and growing as they go. This is how the warm base of a summer cloud makes rain.

Route two, the ice-crystal process. Near the cold top of the same tall cloud (below freezing), supercooled liquid droplets sit next to a few ice crystals. Water vapor moves from the droplets to the crystals, so the crystals grow while the droplets shrink. The crystals fall, clump into snowflakes, and if they melt in the warm air lower down they arrive as rain. Over Kansas City this ice route makes most of the precipitation, even in summer, because the tops of the taller clouds are colder than freezing.

Aha: both routes solve the same problem, which is gathering about a million droplets into one drop. Warm clouds do it by collision. Cold clouds do it by letting ice steal water from liquid.

Example 2. Same snow aloft, four different results. A late-winter storm is over Kansas City. A weather balloon shows snow forming in the cold cloud. Below the cloud the profile has a warm layer (above 0 C) from about 1 km up to 2 km, and below 1 km the air is below freezing all the way to the ground. What lands at the surface, and how would the answer change if the layers were different?

Read the stack from the top down. Snow leaves the cloud. From 2 km to 1 km it falls through the warm layer, so it melts into liquid drops. From 1 km to the ground it falls through a below-freezing layer that is a full kilometer thick, which is plenty of time to refreeze. So the drops freeze back into small ice pellets before landing. That is sleet.

Now change one thing at a time and the answer changes:

  • Make the surface cold layer very thin (say only the bottom 200 m below freezing). The drops do not have time to refreeze in the air, so they land as liquid and freeze on contact. That is freezing rain.
  • Remove the surface cold layer entirely, so it is above freezing all the way down. The melted drops stay liquid and land as ordinary rain.
  • Remove the warm layer, so it is below freezing all the way down. The snow never melts and lands as snow.
Aha: the cloud handed down the same snow in every case. The ground got four different kinds of weather. The difference lives entirely in the warm and cold layers in between, not in the cloud and not in the ground temperature by itself.

The two patterns

The two cases each turned on a single idea.

Pattern 1: nothing falls until a droplet grows about a million-fold. A cloud droplet is only about 0.02 millimeters across, roughly a fifth of the width of a human hair. It is so light that the cloud's own gently rising air (from Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates) holds it up, and it would evaporate in the drier air below long before reaching the ground. A raindrop is about 2 millimeters across, 100 times wider. Volume grows with the cube of the width, so:

\[ 100 \times 100 \times 100 = 1{,}000{,}000 \]

One raindrop is roughly a million cloud droplets gathered into one. Until nature collects that million, the cloud just floats. So precipitation is first of all a growth problem.

Pattern 2: the temperature profile on the way down sets the type. In Example 2 the cloud handed down the same snow every time, yet the ground got four different kinds of weather. The type is not read off the ground temperature. It is decided by the whole stack of warm layers (above 0 C) and cold layers (below 0 C) the falling particle crosses from cloud to ground. Read the profile from the top down, mark every place it crosses freezing, and the layers tell you the answer.

The mechanisms, and why

Two ways droplets grow

There are two routes to that million-fold growth, one for warm clouds and one for cold clouds.

  • Collision and coalescence (warm clouds, above freezing). In a cloud that is warmer than freezing all the way up, some droplets happen to be a little bigger than the rest. Bigger drops fall a little faster, so as they sink they run into the smaller, slower droplets below them and swallow them. Each collision makes the falling drop bigger, which makes it fall faster, which makes it sweep up even more. It snowballs. "Coalescence" just means small drops merging into a bigger one. This is the main way rain forms in warm places like the tropics, and in the lower part of a warm summer cloud.
  • The ice-crystal process, also called the Bergeron process (cold clouds, below freezing). Higher up, or in winter, the cloud is colder than freezing. Strangely, the tiny water droplets up there often do not freeze. They stay liquid below 0 C. We call that supercooled water. When a few ice crystals do appear among all those supercooled droplets, something one-sided happens: the air can hold a little less water vapor around ice than around liquid water. So water vapor leaves the liquid droplets and freezes straight onto the ice crystals. The crystals grow while the droplets shrink and disappear. The crystals get heavy, fall, bump into each other, and clump into snowflakes. This is the main way most rain and snow forms in the middle latitudes, including over Kansas City. If it stays frozen to the ground you get snow. If it melts on the way down you get rain.
Aha: a cloud does not rain just because it is full of water. It rains only once its droplets have grown about a million times in volume, either by bigger drops sweeping up smaller ones (warm clouds) or by ice crystals stealing water from supercooled droplets (cold clouds).

What type falls: read the temperature on the way down

In cold-season storms almost everything starts as snow high up. What it turns into depends on where the air is above freezing (warmer than 0 C) and where it is below freezing:

  • Snow: the air is below freezing the whole way down, so the snow never melts.
  • Rain: there is a deep warm layer, so the snow melts completely into liquid and stays liquid to the ground.
  • Sleet (ice pellets): the snow melts in a warm layer, then falls through a thick cold layer near the surface and refreezes into little clear ice pellets before it lands.
  • Freezing rain (glaze): the snow melts in a warm layer, then falls through only a thin cold layer near the surface. That layer is too shallow to refreeze the drop in the air, so it reaches the ground as supercooled liquid and freezes the instant it touches the cold ground, trees, and power lines, coating them in ice.

Notice that sleet and freezing rain both have a warm layer aloft and a cold layer at the surface. The only difference is how thick that surface cold layer is. Thick enough to refreeze in the air gives sleet. Too thin gives freezing rain.

The one habit to build: when someone asks what will fall, do not look at the ground temperature and guess. Read the temperature profile from the cloud down to the ground, and mark every place it crosses freezing. The layers tell you the answer.

Hail: ice from a strong updraft

Snow, sleet, and freezing rain are cold-season events built by quiet, layered air. Hail is the opposite. It is a warm-season thunderstorm product. A strong summer thunderstorm has a powerful updraft, a fast river of rising air. High in the storm the air is far below freezing, full of supercooled droplets. A small ice pellet starts to fall, the updraft catches it and lifts it back up, and each trip up adds a fresh coat of supercooled water that freezes onto it. Cutting a hailstone in half shows these layers, like the rings of an onion. The stone recycles up and down until it is too heavy for even the updraft to hold, then it crashes down. It falls so fast that it does not have time to melt in the warm air near the ground, so it can land as solid ice on a hot afternoon. Example 3 works this out in full.

Measuring it: the rain gauge

Once precipitation reaches the ground we measure how much fell with a rain gauge. The simplest gauge is just a straight-sided container left open to the sky. Whatever depth of water collects in it is the rainfall, reported in millimeters or inches. A key idea: for a straight-sided container the width does not matter. One inch of rain fills a wide bucket and a narrow cup to the same one-inch depth, because a wider opening catches proportionally more rain. The standard gauge used by the United States National Weather Service has an 8-inch (about 20 centimeter) opening and funnels the water into a narrow inner tube, which spreads a small depth over a taller column so you can read it precisely. Snow is measured differently. As a rough rule, about 10 inches of fresh snow melts down to about 1 inch of water, though very cold, fluffy snow gives even less water.

Worked example: hail on a hot day

Example 3. Ice from a hot afternoon. On a hot July afternoon in Kansas City, when a cold front arrives (that is Day 2 of our running episode, barometer near 1002 hPa), a towering thunderstorm can drop hailstones even though the surface temperature is well above 30 C. How does solid ice fall on such a hot day?

Hail is a thunderstorm story, not a winter story. A strong summer thunderstorm has a powerful updraft, a fast river of rising air. High in the storm the air is far below freezing, so there are plenty of supercooled droplets and ice up there. A small ice pellet starts to fall, but the updraft is strong enough to catch it and carry it back up. As it rises it collects a fresh coat of supercooled water that freezes onto it, adding a layer. It falls again, gets caught again, and grows another layer. Cutting a hailstone in half shows these rings, like an onion. The stone keeps cycling up and down until it is finally too heavy for even the updraft to lift, and then it crashes to the ground.

The surface being hot does not matter much. The stone spends its life high in the storm where it is well below freezing, and it falls the last stretch so fast that it does not have time to melt on the way through the warm air near the ground. In fact you need the hot afternoon: strong summer heating is what builds the tall storms with the violent updrafts that make hail possible in the first place.

Aha: hail needs strong updrafts, and strong updrafts come from hot, unstable summer air. So hail is a warm-season thunderstorm phenomenon, not a winter one. Sleet, which also arrives as ice pellets, is the winter cousin, but it forms in a totally different way (a melt-then-refreeze in a stack of quiet layers, with no thunderstorm and no updraft).

Common pitfalls

  • Guessing precipitation type from the ground temperature alone. A surface reading of minus 1 C could go with snow, sleet, or freezing rain. You cannot tell without the layers above. Always read the whole profile from cloud to ground.
  • Mixing up sleet and freezing rain. Both need a warm layer aloft and a cold layer at the surface. Sleet has a thick surface cold layer, so the drop refreezes into a pellet in the air. Freezing rain has a thin surface cold layer, so the drop stays liquid and freezes only when it hits something.
  • Calling hail a winter thing because it is ice. Hail is made by the strong updrafts of a warm-season thunderstorm. Winter ice pellets are sleet, which forms with no thunderstorm at all. Same shape, completely different cause and season.
  • Thinking a cloud rains just because it holds a lot of water. Cloud droplets are far too small to fall. They must first grow about a million times in volume, by collision or by the ice-crystal process. No growth, no precipitation.
  • Assuming supercooled water is impossible. Tiny cloud droplets often stay liquid well below 0 C. That supercooled water is exactly what feeds the ice crystals in a cold cloud, and it is what freezes on contact to make freezing rain.

12. Air Masses and Fronts

An air mass is a huge blob of air with one temperature and one humidity. A front is the boundary where two of them collide, and it is named for whichever one is on the move.

This chapter: Air Masses and Fronts
Unlocks: Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones · Chapter 18: Tools and Forecasting

Three days of one Kansas City front

Until now we followed one small bubble, "Parcel P," over Kansas City. Now zoom all the way out to the whole region. Here is what a weather station in Kansas City records over three spring days as a front sweeps through. Read the numbers first. We will not name any of the parts until the pattern is clear. These are representative values for a typical Kansas City front, not one exact historical date.

  • Day 1, warm and calm. Warm and muggy. The wind is light from the south. The sky is mostly clear. The barometer sits steady near 1013 hPa.
  • Day 2, the crossing. High wispy clouds thicken through the morning into towering thunderheads. A narrow line of heavy showers and thunderstorms hits in the afternoon. The wind swings around to the northwest. The temperature drops sharply. The barometer falls to its lowest point of the three days, about 1002 hPa, right as the storms go through. That is a fall of \(1013-1002=11\) hPa from Day 1.
  • Day 3, cool and clear. Much cooler and noticeably drier. The sky clears. The wind stays gusty from the northwest. The barometer climbs to about 1020 hPa.

Two pictures make the three days concrete. The first is the barometer trace with the weather that goes with it. The second is a slice through the atmosphere on Day 2, showing the wall of cold air that did all of this.

Do not name the mechanism yet. First, read what all three days have in common.

The pattern: a moving boundary between two air masses

The three days are not three different weathers. They are two different bodies of air, with a boundary sweeping past the city in between. Day 1 is one body of air: warm and moist. Day 3 is a different body of air: cold and dry. Day 2 is the boundary itself passing overhead. That sloping boundary between two bodies of air is called a front.

Every front passage writes the same signature on the instruments. Watch for these four things together:

  • The barometer falls to a minimum, then rises. The lowest reading marks the moment the boundary passes. In Kansas City it bottomed near 1002 hPa on Day 2, then climbed to 1020 hPa.
  • The wind shifts. It swung from the south to the northwest as the boundary went by.
  • The temperature jumps across the boundary. Here it dropped, because colder air moved in behind.
  • The sky runs through a set sequence: clouds build, a band of precipitation passes, then it clears (for the other kind of front, the reverse).

Because the cold air was the one advancing and shoving the warm air out, this particular boundary is a cold front. That naming rule, and the full set of front types, is what the rest of the chapter builds out. To use it, you first need to know the two masses on either side, so start there.

Air masses and where they are born

When air sits over one kind of surface for days, the whole region of air slowly takes on the character of that surface. It becomes an air mass: a very large body of air, often thousands of kilometers across, that has fairly uniform temperature and humidity throughout. The place where an air mass forms is called its source region. Air over the warm Gulf of Mexico turns warm and moist. Air over central Canada in winter turns cold and dry. The surface writes its signature onto the air above it.

Naming an air mass: two letters

We label an air mass with two letters. The first letter (lowercase) tells you the moisture, which comes from the surface it formed over. The second letter (uppercase) tells you the temperature, which comes from its latitude.

  • c = continental (formed over land, so it is dry)
  • m = maritime (formed over ocean, so it is moist)
  • P = polar (formed far north, so it is cold)
  • T = tropical (formed near the tropics, so it is warm)

Put them together and you get four common types that affect Kansas City:

  • cP (continental polar): cold and dry, from central Canada.
  • mT (maritime tropical): warm and moist, from the Gulf of Mexico.
  • cT (continental tropical): hot and dry, from the desert Southwest and Mexico.
  • mP (maritime polar): cool and moist, from the North Pacific or North Atlantic.

Read the two letters as moisture first (c dry or m moist), then temperature (P cold or T warm). The Day 1 warm muggy air is mT from the Gulf; the Day 3 cool dry air is cP from Canada. Those are the two masses that collide at the front.

The four fronts and their weather

Air masses do not blend together easily. Where a cold, dense mass meets a warm, light one, they stay separate and press against each other along a sloping boundary. That boundary is a front. The single most important rule in this chapter:

A front is named for the air mass that is ADVANCING, the one doing the pushing, not the one being shoved out of the way.

So a cold front is cold air advancing into warmer air. A warm front is warm air advancing into colder air. Never name the front for the air that is leaving.

Cold front: steep, fast, brief and violent

Cold air is denser and heavier, so it stays low and acts like a plow. Its leading edge is a steep wall. As it drives forward it forces the warm, moist air upward quickly. That fast lift shoots the warm air high, where it cools, condenses, and builds tall thunderstorm clouds (cumulonimbus). Because the lift happens in a narrow zone right at the front, you get a narrow band of heavy showers and thunderstorms. This is exactly Day 2 over Kansas City, and it is where Parcel P's storm from the earlier chapters fires. Once the front passes, the cold dry air arrives and the sky clears. The cross-section at the top of the chapter shows this: the steep blue cold wedge, the tall cumulonimbus, the narrow rain band. The barometer falls as it nears, bottoms out at passage near 1002 hPa, then rises sharply toward 1020 hPa. A cold front also moves fast, so its rough weather is over quickly.

Aha: a steep wedge means a fast lift, and a fast lift in a narrow zone means brief but violent weather. Then the cold dry air clears the sky.

Warm front: gentle, slow, wide and steady

Now suppose instead the warm air is the one advancing, sliding back into a region of retreating cold air. Warm air is lighter, so it cannot plow underneath. Instead it rides gently up and over the cold air ahead of it. The boundary has a gentle slope that stretches for hundreds of kilometers. Because the lift is slow and spread over a wide area, you get a wide band of layered clouds and steady, lighter rain that arrives long before the surface front. High wispy cirrus comes first, then lower, thicker cloud decks (altostratus, then nimbostratus), then a long spell of gentle rain. A warm front also moves slower than a cold front, so this steady weather lasts many hours. After the front passes, the air turns warmer.

Aha: a gentle slope means a slow lift over a wide area, so a warm front trades the cold front's brief violence for a long, steady soaking.

Stationary and occluded fronts

Two other kinds of front round out the set.

  • A stationary front is a boundary where two air masses meet but neither one is winning, so the front barely moves. It can leave a region under clouds and rain for days.
  • An occluded front forms when a fast cold front catches up to a slower warm front ahead of it. The cold air lifts the warm air completely off the ground, so the warm air no longer touches the surface at all.

Each front has its own map symbol. The little shapes always point in the direction the front is moving.

Example 1. On Day 1 of the Kansas City episode the air is warm and muggy with a wind from the south. On Day 3, behind the front, it is cool and dry with a wind from the northwest. Name each of these two air masses with its two letters, and say where each was born.

The warm, muggy Day 1 air. It is warm (so tropical, T) and moist (so maritime, m). It came north from over the warm water of the Gulf of Mexico. Name: mT, maritime tropical.

The cool, dry Day 3 air. It is cold (so polar, P) and dry (so continental, c). It came south from over the land of central Canada. Name: cP, continental polar.

Answer: Day 1 is maritime tropical (mT) from the Gulf; Day 3 is continental polar (cP) from Canada. These are the two air masses that collide at the Day 2 cold front.

Aha: the two letters just record where the air was born. Read moisture first (c dry or m moist), then temperature (P cold or T warm).

Example 2. A weather station somewhere else records this over about a day and a half: high wispy clouds appear, then lower and thicken into a solid gray deck, then a steady light rain sets in for many hours, well ahead of the boundary at the ground. Afterward the air turns warmer and the barometer, which had been slowly falling, levels off instead of jumping. Is this a cold front or a warm front, and how do you know?

Read the clues. A wide, slow, layered cloud sequence (high thin clouds first, then thick low decks), steady lighter rain over many hours, rain arriving far ahead of the surface boundary, and warming afterward. Every one of those is the gentle, wide, slow signature.

Name it. This is a warm front. The warm air is advancing and riding gently up over the cold air, so the lift is slow and spread across a wide band. A cold front would instead give a narrow line of brief, heavy thunderstorms, a sharp temperature DROP, and a barometer that bottoms out and then rises sharply, not one that just levels off.

Answer: a warm front, read from the wide steady rain, the slow layered clouds, and the warming behind it.

Aha: wide and gentle and slow and warming means a warm front; narrow and violent and brief and cooling means a cold front.

Example 3. The Kansas City barometer reads about 1013 hPa on Day 1, about 1002 hPa on Day 2, and about 1020 hPa on Day 3. How far did the pressure fall going into the front, how far did it rise behind it, and on which day did the front pass?

The fall. From Day 1 to Day 2 the pressure drops \(1013-1002=11\) hPa.

The rise. From the Day 2 minimum to Day 3 it climbs \(1020-1002=18\) hPa.

The timing. The pressure reaches its lowest point on Day 2, and the lowest point marks the moment the boundary passes. So the front went through on Day 2.

Answer: the barometer falls 11 hPa into the front, rises 18 hPa behind it, and the Day 2 minimum marks the passage.

Aha: the barometer does not just fall at a front. It falls to a minimum at passage, then rises. The turn from falling to rising is the signal that the front has gone by.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: naming the front for the wrong air. A front is always named for the air mass that is ADVANCING, not the one being pushed out. On Day 2 the cold air is advancing, so it is a cold front, even though warm air is present too. Find the mover, then name it.
Pitfall 2: swapping the two fronts' weather. Cold front = steep boundary, narrow band, heavy and brief, then clearing. Warm front = gentle boundary, wide band, lighter and long, then warmer. Do not give the cold front a wide, gentle rain or the warm front a narrow, violent storm.
Pitfall 3: thinking the barometer only falls. During a cold-front passage the pressure falls to a minimum right AT the front, then RISES behind it. A barometer that has stopped falling and started climbing is telling you the front has gone by.

13. Global Circulation and Jet Streams

Zoom out from Kansas City to the whole planet. The Sun heats the equator more than the poles, and Earth's spin bends that heat into three great wind loops in each hemisphere.

Prerequisites: Chapter 2: Energy from the Sun · Chapter 6: Wind and the Coriolis Effect
This chapter: Global Circulation and Jet Streams
Unlocks: Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones · Chapter 17: Climate and Climate Zones

Start with what you can see

For the whole course so far we have watched one small parcel of air, "Parcel P," over Kansas City. In this chapter we step all the way back and look at the entire planet at once. Before any theory, look at three plain facts anyone can observe about the planet's winds and weather.

  1. The tropics are soaking wet, but a ring of desert sits just poleward of them. Right on the equator lie the planet's great rain forests: the Amazon, the Congo, and Indonesia. Yet only about 25 to 30 degrees away, in both hemispheres, runs an almost unbroken belt of desert: the Sahara and Arabian deserts to the north, the Kalahari and Australian deserts to the south. Same Sun, same planet, and the two zones sit close together, but one drowns in rain while the other almost never sees any.
  2. Kansas City's weather almost always arrives from the WEST. A storm over Denver today is usually over Kansas City tomorrow. Television forecasters draw systems marching left to right across the map, west to east, day after day. At Kansas City's latitude (about 39 degrees north) the prevailing surface wind blows from the west, so weather rides in from the west, not from the east.
  3. A narrow, fast river of wind high overhead steers the winter storms. When a cold front slams into Kansas City, the forecaster points to the jet stream dipping south. When that same ribbon of wind bulges north instead, Kansas City stays warm and quiet. This wind sits about 10 kilometers up and blasts from west to east, and where it goes, the storms follow.

All three facts come from one global machine. Here is a slice through one hemisphere, from the equator on the left to the pole on the right. We will spend the rest of the chapter explaining it.

The pattern

Read the three observations off the figure and one pattern ties them together:

  • Rising air near the equator makes rain; sinking air near 30 degrees makes deserts. Air goes up where it is hottest (the equator), cools, and rains itself out. It comes back down near 30 degrees, warms, and dries. Rising branch means wet, sinking branch means dry.
  • The surface wind does not blow straight from the poles to the equator. It is broken into steady belts, and each belt blows mostly sideways, toward the east or the west, rather than straight toward the equator. Kansas City sits in the belt that comes from the west.
  • The whole flow is stacked into loops, called cells, three per hemisphere, with fast ribbons of wind (the jet streams) riding along the boundaries between the cells, high near the top of the weather layer.

The rest of the chapter earns this pattern: WHY there are three cells, WHY the belts point the way they do, and WHY the jets sit where they do. It all traces back to two things you already know: uneven heating and the Coriolis effect.

The mechanism: three cells, wind belts, and jets

In Chapter 2 we learned that the Sun heats the equator much more strongly than the poles. At the equator sunlight comes almost straight down, so a lot of energy lands on each square meter of ground. Near the poles that same sunlight arrives at a low, slanting angle and spreads over much more ground, so each square meter gets far less. The result is a hot equator and cold poles. That single temperature difference is the engine for everything below.

If Earth stood still: one giant loop

Warm air is light and rises; cold air is heavy and sinks (this is convection, from Chapter 3). If the planet did not spin, that one temperature difference would drive a single huge loop in each hemisphere: warm air rising at the equator, drifting toward the pole high up, sinking at the cold pole, and crawling back along the surface to the equator. One loop, top to bottom.

Because Earth spins: three cells instead

But Earth does spin, and from Chapter 6 we know the Coriolis effect bends moving air to its RIGHT in the Northern Hemisphere (to the LEFT in the Southern, and not at all at the equator). Air trying to travel all the way from the equator to the pole gets turned so strongly that it cannot finish the trip in one loop. Instead the flow breaks into three smaller loops, called cells, in each hemisphere. The globe spans \(90^\circ\) of latitude from the equator to the pole, and the three cells split that up, roughly \(90^\circ \div 3 = 30^\circ\) each:

  • Hadley cell (0 to 30 degrees): warm, wet air rises at the equator. Rising air cools, its water vapor condenses, and it pours down heavy rain, which is why the great rain forests sit on the equator. That air drifts poleward high up, then sinks back down near 30 degrees. Sinking air warms and dries out, so belts of desert ring the 30 degree line (the Sahara, the Arabian and Australian deserts).
  • Ferrel cell (30 to 60 degrees): the middle loop, the one Kansas City lives in at about 39 degrees north. It turns the opposite way from its neighbors, like an idler gear caught between the Hadley and Polar cells: its air sinks near 30 degrees and rises near 60 degrees.
  • Polar cell (60 to 90 degrees): very cold air sinks at the pole and rises again near 60 degrees.

The ITCZ: where the trade winds collide at the equator

The rising branches of the two Hadley cells, one from each hemisphere, meet in a narrow band near the equator called the Intertropical Convergence Zone, or ITCZ. The surface trade winds from the north and from the south flow together here, which is what "convergence" means, and that colliding air has nowhere to go but up. The rising, converging air is why the equator carries a near-permanent band of towering clouds and heavy thunderstorms. The band drifts a little north in July and a little south in January, following the point where the Sun is overhead.

The surface wind belts

The bottom of each cell is a steady surface wind, and Coriolis bends each one sideways. Remember from Chapter 6 that a wind is named for the direction it comes FROM.

  • Trade winds (0 to 30 degrees): surface air flowing back toward the equator is bent to the right, so it comes from the northeast. These tropical easterlies are the trade winds.
  • Westerlies (30 to 60 degrees): surface air flowing toward the pole is bent to the right, so it comes from the southwest. These are the mid-latitude westerlies, and they carry weather across the United States from west to east, right over Kansas City.
  • Polar easterlies (60 to 90 degrees): cold surface air flowing away from the pole is bent right, so it comes from the northeast.

Jet streams

Where two cells meet, high above the surface near the tropopause (the top of the weather layer, roughly 10 to 12 kilometers up), the wind is squeezed into a fast, narrow ribbon called a jet stream. Each hemisphere has two main ones: the subtropical jet near 30 degrees, on the Hadley to Ferrel boundary, and the polar jet near 60 degrees, on the Ferrel to Polar boundary. Both run roughly west to east, and they can blow well over 160 km per hour (about 100 miles per hour). The polar jet sits on the boundary between cold polar air and warmer air to the south, and its dips and bends steer the storms and cold fronts that reach Kansas City.

Zooming back in: local winds from uneven heating

The very same rule that builds the global cells, warm air rises and cooler surface air flows in toward it, also makes small, local winds wherever one surface heats faster than the one next to it.

  • Sea and land breezes (day versus night): By day, land heats up faster than water. Air rises over the warm land, and cooler air from over the sea flows in to take its place. That is a sea breeze, blowing from the sea toward the land. At night it flips: the land cools faster, the sea is now the warmer surface, air rises over the water, and the breeze blows from the land toward the sea (a land breeze).
  • Monsoons (season by season): the same idea, but spread over a whole continent and a whole season. In summer a large landmass heats far more than the nearby ocean, so moist ocean air is pulled inland and brings a rainy season. In winter the land cools, the flow reverses, and the season turns dry. India's summer monsoon is the famous example.

Worked examples

Example 1. Using the diagram, explain why the Northern Hemisphere has three circulation cells instead of one single loop from the equator to the pole.

Step 1, the engine. The equator is hot, so air rises there. The pole is cold, so air sinks there. On its own, that difference wants to make one big loop, top to bottom.

Step 2, the wrench. As the high air heads north, the Coriolis effect bends it to the right, which means toward the east. By about 30 degrees north the air is blowing almost straight west to east and can no longer make progress toward the pole. It bunches up and sinks. That sinking near 30 degrees closes off the first loop, the Hadley cell.

Step 3, the leftovers. The same turning traps the flow again, so two more loops form: the Ferrel cell from 30 to 60 degrees and the Polar cell from 60 to 90 degrees. That makes three cells in all.

Answer: Earth's spin, through the Coriolis effect, keeps bending poleward-moving air toward the east until it cannot travel any farther north, so one loop breaks into three.

Aha: a spinning planet cannot run one smooth loop from the equator to the pole. Coriolis chops it into three cells per hemisphere.

Example 2. A ship sails in the tropics near 15 degrees north. A weather system moves across Kansas City near 39 degrees north. What prevailing surface wind does each feel, and from which direction does it come?

The ship (Hadley cell surface). Down at the bottom of the Hadley cell, surface air flows from about 30 degrees back toward the equator, so it heads south. Coriolis bends moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, which turns that southward flow toward the west. Air that ends up coming from the northeast is a northeasterly. These are the trade winds.

Kansas City (Ferrel cell surface). At about 39 degrees north, surface air flows from around 30 degrees toward 60 degrees, so it heads north. Coriolis bends it to the right, toward the east. Air coming from the southwest is a westerly.

Answer: the ship feels the northeast trade winds; Kansas City sits in the westerlies, which push its weather from west to east.

Aha: the very same Coriolis rule (bend to the right) produces two opposite-looking belts, because the two surface flows head opposite ways, one toward the equator and one toward the pole.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: swapping which branch is wet and which is dry. Rising air cools, so its water vapor condenses and falls as rain. The rising branches (the equator and near 60 degrees) are cloudy and wet. Sinking air warms and dries out, so the sinking branches (near 30 degrees and the poles) are clear and dry. That is why deserts, not rain forests, ring the 30 degree belt.
Pitfall 2: naming a wind for where it is going. A wind is named for where it comes FROM. The trade winds blow toward the southwest but come from the northeast, so they are northeasterlies. The westerlies blow toward the east but come from the west.
Pitfall 3: getting the Coriolis direction backward at the planet scale. It bends moving air to the RIGHT in the Northern Hemisphere and to the LEFT in the Southern Hemisphere, and it is zero right at the equator. This is the same rule from Chapter 6, just applied across the whole globe.

  • From Chapter 6: Wind and the Coriolis Effect: take the same push and turn that shaped one wind and stretch it across the whole planet, and you get the trade winds, the westerlies, and the jet streams.

Answer

Answer: because 30 degrees is the latitude where the Hadley cell's air sinks back to the ground.

This is the same rule as the global cells, just small and fast, and it is why monsoons flip from wet in summer to dry in winter.

14. Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones

A low is a place where surface air piles in, rises, and makes clouds. A high is a place where air sinks and skies clear. Put a low on a front and it becomes the traveling storm that drives Kansas City's three-day episode.

Start here: a storm crosses the plains

Follow a single low-pressure system as it travels across the middle of the United States and you see this whole chapter in action. On Day 1, Kansas City sits in warm, humid air with the barometer near 1013 hPa and mostly clear skies. A spinning low is approaching from the west. By Day 2 its cold front sweeps through, the barometer bottoms out near 1002 hPa, and a line of thunderstorms fires along the front. By Day 3 the low has moved off to the east, a high has built in behind it, the barometer climbs to about 1020 hPa, and the air turns cooler, drier, and clear. Those are representative values for a typical Kansas City episode, not a specific date. That three-day parade is the running story of this whole course, and it is one traveling low doing all of it.

The surface map below is what a forecaster sees. A big blue L marks the low center up to the north. Two fronts trail from it: a cold front to the southwest and a warm front to the east. The humid wedge between them, where Kansas City sits, is the warm sector. Air circles the low counterclockwise, and the whole system slides east. Behind the cold front, out to the west, a big red H marks the high that follows. There the air is sinking, and the sky is fair, calm, and clear. One map holds both characters of this chapter, the stormy low and the fair-weather high. The rest of the page explains why each behaves as it does.

Name the pattern

Every case in this chapter comes down to two opposite machines, one that makes weather and one that kills it.

  • A LOW is converging, rising air, so it makes clouds, rain, and storms. In the Northern Hemisphere its surface winds turn counterclockwise. A low with this inward, turning flow is called a cyclone.
  • A HIGH is sinking, spreading air, so it brings clear, calm, dry skies. In the Northern Hemisphere its surface winds turn clockwise. A high with this outward, turning flow is called an anticyclone.

The traveling lows that cross the country are a special member of this family called mid-latitude cyclones (also called extratropical cyclones). They are born as waves on the polar front, the boundary where cold polar air meets warm subtropical air, and they ride the jet stream and the prevailing westerlies from west to east. The rest of the page explains why each machine works, then follows one mid-latitude cyclone through its entire life.

The mechanism: why a low storms and a high clears

In Chapter 5 you learned that some places on a weather map have higher air pressure than others. We label the centers with a big L for a low and a big H for a high. In Chapter 6 you learned that near the ground friction turns the wind slightly across the isobars, so surface air does not just circle a low forever. It slowly leaks inward toward the low. This section takes those two facts and looks at a pressure system in three dimensions, not just as a flat map. The up-and-down motion is where the weather comes from.

A LOW is a chimney: air comes in, then goes up

Picture the low from the side. All around it, surface air is drifting inward toward the center. When air pours in from every direction toward one spot, it has nowhere to go but up. This piling in is called convergence, and the only escape is to rise. As the air rises it cools (rising air always cools, from Chapter 8). Cooler air cannot hold as much water vapor, so the vapor condenses into cloud droplets (from Chapter 7). That is why:

A LOW means converging, rising air, which makes clouds, rain, and storms.

Looking down from above in the Northern Hemisphere, that inflow does not spiral straight in. The Coriolis effect from Chapter 6 bends every moving parcel to its right, and the result is that the whole inflow turns counterclockwise. A low with this inward, turning flow is called a cyclone. Counterclockwise flow around a low in the Northern Hemisphere is called cyclonic.

A HIGH is a fountain: air sinks, then spreads out

A high is the mirror image. At the center, air is sinking from above. Sinking air warms up (sinking air always warms, from Chapter 8), and warmer air can hold more water vapor, so clouds evaporate away. When the sinking air reaches the ground it has to spread outward, away from the center. This spreading out is called divergence. So:

A HIGH means sinking, spreading air, which brings clear, calm, dry skies.

Looking down from above in the Northern Hemisphere, that outflow turns clockwise. A high with this outward, turning flow is called an anticyclone. Notice the two rules are exact opposites: a low is inflow and counterclockwise, a high is outflow and clockwise.

Word check: in everyday news the word "cyclone" often means a tropical storm or even a tornado. In meteorology it means something broader. A cyclone is any low-pressure system with inward, turning flow, including the ordinary traveling lows that cross the United States every few days. Tropical cyclones (Chapter 16) and tornadoes (Chapter 15) are special, violent members of the family, but the plain low over Kansas City is a cyclone too.

The life cycle: how a mid-latitude cyclone is born, grows, and dies

A mid-latitude cyclone is not a permanent feature. It grows from a small kink on the polar front, matures into the classic two-front storm, then chokes off its own energy supply and fades, usually over about three to five days. Meteorologists split that life into four stages.

  1. Cyclogenesis (the wave). The polar front starts as a nearly straight boundary with cold air on the north side and warm air on the south side (you met air masses and fronts in Chapter 12). A small wave, or kink, forms on it, often where the jet stream aloft is helping pull air away from the top of the column. The counterclockwise turn begins and a weak low appears at the tip of the wave. Cyclogenesis simply means the birth of a cyclone.
  2. Open wave (mature). The low deepens and the wave opens into the familiar pattern. The counterclockwise spin drags cold air south on the west side and warm air north on the east side, which splits the boundary into two fronts: a warm front reaching east, where warm air rides up over the retreating cool air, and a cold front trailing southwest, where cold air charges in under the warm air. The wedge of warm, humid air between them is the warm sector. This is the stage drawn on the opening map, and the stage that gives Kansas City its warm sector, cold front, and storms.
  3. Occlusion. A cold front travels faster than a warm front. So the cold front slowly catches up to the warm front and begins to overtake it, starting near the low center. Where it does, it wedges under the warm air and lifts the entire warm sector up off the ground. The new merged boundary is called an occluded front. With its warm, humid fuel now lifted away from the surface, the storm is at its strongest, but it has begun to cut off its own supply.
  4. Dissipation. Once the warm sector is fully lifted aloft, the surface low is surrounded by cold air on all sides. With no warm air feeding in, the temperature contrast that powered the storm is gone. The low slowly fills, the winds ease, and the cyclone dies.

Why occlusion matters: the moment of occlusion is both the peak and the beginning of the end. The storm is deepest and its clouds are most extensive, but by lifting the warm sector off the surface it removes the warm-versus-cold contrast that was its energy source. This birth-to-death sequence, wave to open wave to occlusion to dissipation, is the standard picture of how these storms evolve. It is often called the Norwegian cyclone model, after the meteorologists who first drew it.

Worked examples

Example 1. Two cities report their pressure. City A sits under a low of about 1002 hPa; City B sits under a high of about 1020 hPa. Just from that, predict each city's sky, and explain the reason in terms of up-and-down air motion.

City A, the low. Surface air is converging toward the low and has nowhere to go but up. Rising air cools. Cooling air reaches its dew point and the water vapor condenses into clouds. So City A should be cloudy, likely with rain or storms.

City B, the high. Air is sinking over the high. Sinking air warms, and warm air holds more vapor, so clouds evaporate instead of forming. The sinking air spreads outward when it reaches the ground. So City B should be clear, calm, and dry.

Answer: the low (City A) brings clouds and storms; the high (City B) brings clear, calm skies.

Aha: the number on the map matters less than the direction the air is moving. Low equals up (storms); high equals down (clear). Never swap them.

Example 2. Look again at the surface map that opened this chapter: a mid-latitude cyclone crossing the Great Plains, with a low (L) to the north, a warm front reaching east, a cold front trailing southwest, and Kansas City in the warm sector with the cold front to its west. Name the two fronts, say which way the air circles the low, and describe the weather Kansas City will feel as the whole system slides east over the next day.

The two fronts. The red line with half-circles reaching east is the warm front. The blue line with triangles trailing southwest is the cold front. The warm, humid warm sector is the wedge between them, and Kansas City is inside it right now.

The spin. This is a low, so the air circles it counterclockwise (cyclonic, Northern Hemisphere). That counterclockwise turning is what pulls warm air north on the east side and cold air south on the west side.

What Kansas City feels as the system moves east. The cold front is to the city's west and the whole system is drifting east, so the cold front is heading straight for the city. First Kansas City stays warm and humid in the warm sector. Then the cold front arrives, wedges under the warm air, and forces it up fast, firing a line of thunderstorms. After the front passes, the wind shifts, cooler and drier air moves in from the northwest, and the sky clears as the following high builds in.

Aha: the mid-latitude cyclone is just a low from Example 1 with fronts wrapped around it. The counterclockwise spin builds the fronts, and the jet stream drives the storm past the city one piece at a time.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: swapping the weather of highs and lows. A low is rising air, so it makes clouds and storms. A high is sinking air, so it clears the sky. Students often reverse this because "high" sounds nice. Anchor it to the air motion: up equals cloudy, down equals clear.
Pitfall 2: thinking a cyclone must be a tropical storm or a tornado. A cyclone is any low with inward, turning flow. The plain traveling low over Kansas City is a cyclone. Hurricanes and tornadoes are dramatic relatives, not the whole definition.
Pitfall 3: getting the rotation backward. In the Northern Hemisphere, air turns counterclockwise around a low and clockwise around a high. It is the reverse in the Southern Hemisphere. The Coriolis effect from Chapter 6 sets the direction, so if you can recall "deflect to the right in the north," you can rebuild the spin.
Pitfall 4: thinking a deepening storm just keeps growing. It is tempting to assume a strengthening low keeps intensifying without end. But once the faster cold front overtakes the warm front and lifts the warm sector aloft (occlusion), the low is cut off from the warm, humid air that fed it. From there it fills and dies. Occlusion is the peak and the start of the decline, not a fresh burst of growth.

Answer

Why it works: a cold front does not exist on its own. It is the trailing edge of a spinning low, one of the two fronts that wrap around the warm sector. So finding a front on a map is finding a cyclone. The barometer is your depth gauge for that low. Falling pressure means you are moving toward the center; the minimum reading is closest approach; rising pressure means the low has gone by and the following high is arriving. The canonical episode numbers, about 1013 hPa on Day 1, about 1002 hPa at frontal passage on Day 2, and about 1020 hPa on Day 3, trace exactly that dip-then-recovery.

Falling pressure equals low approaching (worsening weather); rising pressure equals high approaching (improving weather). This is the same reasoning a forecaster uses to turn a single station's trend into the position of the whole system.

P4. A mid-latitude cyclone has been deepening for two days. Now its cold front has caught up to its warm front near the low center, forming an occluded front, and the warm sector no longer touches the ground near the center. Is this storm about to get stronger or about to weaken, and why?

Answer

Answer: it is about to weaken. The storm has reached occlusion, which is its peak and the start of its decline.

Why it works: a mid-latitude cyclone runs on the contrast between warm and cold air. The warm sector is the fuel: warm, humid air that rises, cools, and releases energy as its vapor condenses. A cold front moves faster than a warm front, so it eventually overtakes it and lifts the whole warm sector up off the ground, leaving an occluded front. Once the warm air is aloft and cold air surrounds the surface low on all sides, there is no warm-versus-cold contrast left near the ground to power the storm. The low fills, the winds ease, and the system dies.

Pattern, occlusion equals peak-then-decline: to judge where a cyclone is in its life, find the warm sector. A wide warm sector at the surface means an open, still-strengthening wave. An occluded front and a warm sector lifted aloft mean the storm has peaked and is cutting off its own fuel. This is the birth-to-death arc of the Norwegian cyclone model: wave, open wave, occlusion, dissipation.

15. Severe Weather I: Thunderstorms, Lightning and Tornadoes

A thunderstorm is warm, humid, unstable air rising fast and building one giant cloud. Inside that cloud, charge splits apart to make lightning, and the strongest storms can spin up a tornado.

Prerequisites: Chapter 3: Heat on the Move · Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability · Chapter 10: Clouds
This chapter: Severe Weather I: Thunderstorms, Lightning and Tornadoes
Unlocks: Chapter 16: Severe Weather II

Start with the sky: two afternoons on the Plains

Before any theory, watch what actually happens on a spring afternoon in Tornado Alley, the stretch of the Great Plains across Oklahoma and Kansas just southwest of Kansas City. On most days the sky stays quiet. On a few days each spring, four things line up at once, and by evening a single storm can drop hail and a tornado. Here are two of those afternoons.

Afternoon 1: a supercell builds

It is early May near Kansas City. Warm, humid air is streaming north from the Gulf of Mexico, so the surface is about 30 C with a dew point near 22 C (very muggy). That is the moisture. Above that warm, moist layer sits much colder, drier air, so a lifted parcel stays warmer than its surroundings and keeps rising on its own. That is the instability from Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability. A cold front is sweeping in from the west and shoves the warm air upward: that is the lift that gets it started. And the wind is not the same at every height. Near the ground it blows from the southeast at maybe 20 km/h; three kilometers up it blows from the southwest at 60 km/h. That change of wind speed and direction with height is called wind shear.

Give the sky those four things and this is what unfolds. First a puffy cumulus tower shoots up. Within about half an hour it has climbed all the way to the tropopause near 12 km and flattened into a wide anvil: a cumulonimbus, the thunderstorm cloud. Because the wind shear tilts the rising column over, the rain and hail fall to one side instead of straight back down the middle, so the storm never chokes off its own warm inflow and it lives for hours. The shear also sets the whole updraft slowly rotating. A long lived, rotating storm like this has its own name: a supercell. It is the kind that makes the biggest hail and the strongest tornadoes.

Afternoon 2: a tornado drops

Now zoom into the heart of that supercell. Its rotating updraft, a slow and broad spin a few kilometers across, is called the mesocyclone. As the storm intensifies, that broad spin stretches and narrows toward the ground. Just like a spinning skater who pulls in their arms and speeds up, the column spins faster as it tightens. When a narrow, violently spinning column reaches from the cloud base down to the ground, it is a tornado. Winds in the worst ones top 200 miles per hour (about 320 km/h). After it passes, surveyors rate it on the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, from EF0 to EF5, by looking at the damage it did.

Name the pattern

Both afternoons follow one recipe. An ordinary thunderstorm needs three ingredients: moisture, instability, and a lift to trigger it. Take away any one and no storm fires. To turn that ordinary storm into a severe, long lived, rotating supercell (the kind that spawns strong tornadoes and giant hail) add a fourth ingredient: wind shear. So the whole chapter in one line: moisture plus instability plus lift make a thunderstorm; add wind shear and you can get a supercell, and from its rotation, a tornado. The rest of this chapter explains WHY each ingredient matters and how the storm turns them into lightning, hail, and tornadoes.

The four ingredients: moisture, instability, and lift make any thunderstorm; wind shear is the extra ingredient that organizes it into a rotating supercell. Tornadoes come from that rotation.

Why it works: the mechanisms

Start with the parcel we already know. Back in Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability, Parcel P over Kansas City was warm, humid, and sitting under air that cooled quickly with height. That made P unstable: once it got a nudge upward, it stayed warmer than the air around it, so it kept rising on its own. In Chapter 10: Clouds, that rising, cooling parcel reached its dew point and made a cumulus cloud. On a hot summer afternoon, that same little cloud does not stop. It grows into a thunderstorm.

A thunderstorm is just deep moist convection. Let us unpack those three words. "Convection" means warm air rising and cool air sinking (that was Chapter 3: Heat on the Move). "Moist" means the rising air is full of water vapor. "Deep" means the rising goes all the way up through the troposphere, many kilometers high, instead of stopping at a shallow fair weather cloud.

The engine that keeps it going is latent heat. When the water vapor in the rising parcel condenses into cloud droplets, it releases the heat it had stored (again from Chapter 3: Heat on the Move). That released heat warms the parcel a little from the inside. A warmer parcel is lighter than its surroundings, so it rises even faster. Faster rising lifts more vapor, which condenses and releases still more heat. This feedback builds a strong, steady rising current called the updraft, the beating heart of every thunderstorm.

Why storms love hot, muggy afternoons: the day's heat makes the air unstable and the humidity supplies the moisture, so two of the three thunderstorm ingredients peak at the same time. All the sky needs then is a lift to trigger it. This is the same recipe (moisture, instability, lift) we named in the opening.

The cloud that results: the cumulonimbus

The tall storm cloud has a name: the cumulonimbus. The updraft carries the cloud up until it hits the top of the troposphere (the tropopause, about 12 km over Kansas City in summer). It cannot punch much higher, so it spreads sideways into a flat, wide top shaped like a blacksmith's anvil. That anvil is the classic sign of a mature thunderstorm. Inside the cloud, cloud droplets grow into raindrops, and in the strongest updrafts, into hail. Hail needs a powerful updraft to hold ice up long enough to grow, so it is a warm season thunderstorm event, not a winter one, even though the hailstones themselves are ice.

Two kinds of storm: the ordinary cell and the supercell

Not all thunderstorms are equal.

  • Ordinary storm (also called a single cell or air mass thunderstorm). This is the common storm that pops up in a warm, humid air mass on a summer afternoon with little wind shear. The updraft and the rain sit in the same place. As the rain falls, it drags cool air down (a downdraft) right through the updraft, which chokes off the warm air feeding the storm. So the storm cuts its own fuel line and usually dies in under an hour.
  • Supercell. When the wind changes speed and direction with height (called wind shear), it tilts the updraft over. Now the rain falls away from the updraft instead of into it, so the warm inflow is never choked. The storm can last for hours. The tilting and turning wind also sets the whole updraft slowly rotating. That rotating updraft has a name: the mesocyclone. A supercell is the rare, long lived, rotating kind of storm, and it is the main producer of the strongest tornadoes and the biggest hail.

Lightning and thunder

Inside the storm, ice crystals and soft hail pellets (called graupel) are thrown around by the updraft and collide again and again. Those collisions scrape electric charge off one and onto the other. The lighter ice crystals carry positive charge to the top of the cloud, while the heavier graupel carries negative charge lower down. The cloud has now split its charge apart, top from bottom, like pulling apart the two ends of a battery.

When enough charge builds up, the air can no longer hold the two sides apart, and a giant spark jumps to even things out. That spark is lightning. It heats the narrow channel of air it passes through to tens of thousands of degrees in an instant, far hotter than the surface of the Sun. The air explodes outward, and that explosion is the sound we call thunder.

Light and sound leave the lightning bolt at the same moment, but they travel at very different speeds. Light reaches your eyes almost instantly. Sound is slow: it travels about 340 meters every second, which is roughly 1 km every 3 seconds (or 1 mile every 5 seconds). So the gap between seeing the flash and hearing the bang tells you how far away the bolt struck. Count the seconds, then divide.

\[ \text{distance in km} = \frac{\text{seconds between flash and bang}}{3}, \qquad \text{distance in miles} = \frac{\text{seconds}}{5} \]
Safety, the 30 second rule: if the gap between flash and bang is 30 seconds or less, the lightning is within about 10 km (6 miles), close enough to strike you next. Go indoors and stay there.

Tornadoes

A tornado is a violently rotating column of air that reaches from the base of a thunderstorm down to the ground. The strongest ones grow out of the rotating updraft (the mesocyclone) of a supercell: the storm's broad, slow spin tightens into a narrow, fast, furious spin, the same way a spinning skater speeds up by pulling their arms in. Winds in the worst tornadoes top 200 miles per hour.

We rate a tornado on the Enhanced Fujita scale, called the EF scale, which runs from EF0 to EF5. It is a damage scale: surveyors look at what the tornado did to buildings and trees after it passes, then work backward to estimate the wind speed. EF0 is the weakest (light damage, winds about 65 to 85 mph) and EF5 is the strongest (total destruction, winds above 200 mph).

Do not mix up the storm scales. Tornadoes use the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, 0 to 5. Hurricanes use a completely different scale, the Saffir-Simpson scale, 1 to 5. They measure different storms of wildly different sizes: a tornado is usually less than a mile wide and lasts minutes, while a hurricane is hundreds of miles wide and lasts days. Never rate a tornado on Saffir-Simpson, and never rate a hurricane on the EF scale.

Tornadoes can happen almost anywhere, but the United States gets more of them than anywhere on Earth, and most cluster in the Great Plains in spring. This stretch of the central plains is nicknamed Tornado Alley. It sits just to the southwest of Kansas City, across Oklahoma and Kansas, where warm, humid air flowing north from the Gulf of Mexico meets dry air from the west and cool air from the north. That clash builds the unstable, sheared conditions supercells love, and it peaks from April through June.

Watch versus warning

When severe weather threatens, the National Weather Service issues two different alerts, and the difference is exactly which of the four ingredients has shown up. A watch means conditions are favorable for severe storms or tornadoes over a large area (think of a whole set of counties), so be ready. A warning means a severe storm or tornado is actually happening now or is imminent for a specific spot, shown on radar or spotted by an observer, so take cover immediately. The easy way to remember it: a tornado watch is like the ingredients being laid out on the counter, while a tornado warning means the storm is already at your door.

Watch versus warning, in one line: a watch means it could happen soon, so stay alert and have a plan. A warning means it is happening or about to, so act now: get to an interior room on the lowest floor, away from windows.

Worked examples

Example 1. From a fair weather cloud to a thunderstorm. On a hot, muggy Kansas City afternoon in July, Parcel P leaves the surface at 32 C with a dew point of 22 C. The surrounding air is unstable (it cools quickly with height, so a rising parcel stays warmer than its neighbors). Explain, step by step, how P's small cloud grows into a full thunderstorm.

Step 1: find where the cloud starts. Using the method from Chapter 8: Adiabatic Processes and Lapse Rates, the spread between temperature and dew point is \(32 - 22 = 10\) C. At the dry rate of about 10 C per km, P closes that 10 C gap after 1 km. So the flat cloud base forms at about 1 km. That is the bottom of an ordinary cumulus cloud.

Step 2: latent heat takes over. Above the cloud base, P is condensing its vapor, so it cools at the slower moist rate (about 6 C per km) and, more importantly, the condensation keeps releasing latent heat inside the parcel. Because the environment is unstable, P stays warmer than the air around it at every height. Warmer means lighter, so P keeps rising on its own, faster and faster. This is the updraft building.

Step 3: the cloud goes deep and spreads. The updraft carries P all the way up to the tropopause, near 12 km. It cannot rise much past that, so the cloud flattens into an anvil. The little cumulus has become a deep cumulonimbus: a thunderstorm.

Aha: the storm is powered by the same buoyancy from Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability, but now the condensing water feeds heat back in, so the rising never runs out of push. Instability is the fuel, and latent heat is the pump. On Day 2 of our three day episode, a cold front lifts many parcels like P at once, and a whole line of these storms fires along the front.

Example 2. Ordinary storm or supercell? Two storms form on the same afternoon. In storm A, the wind is about the same speed and direction all the way up. In storm B, the wind gets much faster and swings around as you go higher (strong wind shear). Which storm is likely to last longer and be more dangerous, and why?

Storm A (little shear) is an ordinary single cell storm. With no shear to tilt it, the updraft goes straight up, and the rain falls straight back down through it. The falling rain drags cool air down right into the warm inflow and chokes off the storm's fuel. Storm A rains hard for maybe half an hour, then dies.

Storm B (strong shear) can become a supercell. The shear tilts the updraft over, so the rain and its cool downdraft fall to the side instead of back into the updraft. The warm inflow is never cut off, so the storm keeps going for hours. The turning wind also sets the updraft rotating, forming a mesocyclone. Storm B is the dangerous one: long lived, capable of big hail, and the type that spawns strong tornadoes.

Aha: wind shear is the difference between a storm that chokes itself in an hour and one that organizes into a rotating, long lived supercell. Separating the updraft from the downdraft is the whole trick.

Example 3. How far is the lightning? You are watching a storm over Kansas City. You see a bright flash of lightning, then start counting. You hear the thunder 9 seconds later. About how far away did the bolt strike, in kilometers and in miles?

The calculation. Sound travels about 1 km every 3 seconds, so divide the seconds by 3 for kilometers: \(9 \div 3 = 3\) km. For miles, sound covers about 1 mile every 5 seconds, so divide by 5: \(9 \div 5 = 1.8\) miles.

So the strike was about 3 km, or roughly 1.9 miles, away.

Aha: light is basically instant, so the delay you count is almost entirely the slow trip of the sound. A shrinking count between flash and bang means the storm is coming toward you. If the count drops to 30 seconds or less, the storm is close enough to be dangerous, so head inside.

Common pitfalls

  • Thinking hail is a winter thing. Hail is ice, but it forms in the strong updrafts of a warm season thunderstorm, which hold ice aloft long enough to grow. Winter brings snow and sleet, not hail. (Which winter precipitation you get depends on the warm and cold layers a drop falls through, covered in Chapter 10: Clouds and the precipitation chapter.)
  • Rating a tornado on the wrong scale. Tornadoes use the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, 0 to 5. The Saffir-Simpson scale, 1 to 5, is for hurricanes. Different storms, different sizes, different scales.
  • Thinking every thunderstorm makes a tornado. Most storms are ordinary single cells that never rotate. Strong tornadoes come from the rare supercell, which needs wind shear to tilt and spin its updraft into a mesocyclone.
  • Counting the flash and the bang as separate trips. Light and sound leave the bolt together. Light arrives so fast it counts as instant, so the seconds you count are just the sound's travel time. Divide by 3 for km, by 5 for miles.
  • Confusing a watch with a warning. A watch means conditions are favorable over a large area, so stay alert. A warning means it is happening now or is imminent for your specific location, so take cover. Watch equals be ready; warning equals act now.

Practice

P1. Kansas City often gets its thunderstorms in the late afternoon on hot summer days, not at sunrise. Using instability, explain why the afternoon is prime time for storms.

Answer

The answer. Through the morning and into the afternoon, the Sun heats the ground, and the ground heats the air just above it. That makes the surface air warm and, over a humid place like the plains, muggy. Warm surface air under cooler air aloft is exactly the unstable setup from Chapter 9: Atmospheric Stability. By mid to late afternoon the surface heating peaks, the air is most unstable, and any nudge upward sends a parcel rising on its own into a thunderstorm. At sunrise the ground has been cooling all night, so the surface air is cool and stable, and storms are far less likely.

Why it works: instability is a comparison between the warm parcel at the surface and the cooler air above it. The bigger that difference, the more buoyant a lifted parcel is, and the harder it rises. Daytime heating grows that difference by warming the bottom of the atmosphere, so instability builds through the day and is strongest a few hours after noon. Add the day's humidity, and a rising parcel also carries lots of latent heat to release once it condenses, which pumps the updraft even harder.

Pattern, follow the heating: for any "why do storms fire at this time or place" question, ask what is warming the low air (afternoon sun, or a warm humid inflow) and what is sitting above it (cool air aloft). Storms fire when the low air is warmest and most humid and the air above stays cool, because that is when instability peaks. This is the same reasoning behind spring and summer being storm season, and behind storms lining up along a cold front, where cool air is being shoved over warm humid air.

P2. During a storm you see a lightning flash and count 15 seconds before you hear the thunder. How far away was the strike, in kilometers and in miles? A minute later, the next flash gives a count of only 6 seconds. What does that tell you, and what should you do?

Answer

The calculation. First strike: \(15 \div 3 = 5\) km, and \(15 \div 5 = 3\) miles. Second strike: \(6 \div 3 = 2\) km, and \(6 \div 5 = 1.2\) miles. The count dropped from 15 seconds to 6 seconds, so the storm moved from about 5 km away to about 2 km away. It is coming toward you.

Why it works: light reaches you almost instantly, so the seconds you count are essentially just the time sound needs to travel from the bolt to your ears. Sound covers about 1 km every 3 seconds (or 1 mile every 5 seconds), so dividing the count by 3 gives kilometers and dividing by 5 gives miles. A smaller count on a later flash means a shorter sound trip, which means the lightning is closer than before.

Pattern, flash to bang as a range finder: treat the flash as the starting gun and the thunder as the finish. The delay times the sound's one way trip, so seconds divided by 3 is kilometers and seconds divided by 5 is miles. Track how the count changes over several flashes to tell whether a storm is approaching or moving off. Once the count is 30 seconds or less (about 10 km, 6 miles), the lightning is close enough to be dangerous, so go indoors. A 6 second count is well inside that, so you should already be inside.

P3. A news report says a tornado was rated EF3, and later that a hurricane out at sea was rated Category 4. A classmate asks whether EF3 or Category 4 is the stronger storm. Explain why the question does not quite make sense.

AnswerThe two ratings come from different scales for different storms, so you cannot line them up directly. A tornado is rated on the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, 0 to 5, based on the damage it does. A hurricane is rated on the Saffir-Simpson scale, 1 to 5, based on its sustained winds. They also differ hugely in size and lifetime: a tornado is usually under a mile wide and lasts minutes, while a hurricane is hundreds of miles wide and lasts days. EF3 and Category 4 both mean "strong for that kind of storm," but they are not the same measuring stick.

P4. On a spring afternoon near Kansas City, the National Weather Service issues a tornado watch for your county. Two hours later, that becomes a tornado warning. What changed between the two alerts, and what should you do differently once the warning is issued?

AnswerA watch means conditions are favorable for tornadoes across a large area (the ingredients are in place), so you should stay alert, keep an eye on the sky, and have a plan. A warning means a tornado has now been spotted or shown on radar for your specific area, so it is happening or imminent. Once the warning is issued, stop waiting: take cover immediately in an interior room on the lowest floor, away from windows. Watch equals be ready; warning equals act now.

The pattern

Each storm runs on a short recipe, and the rest of the chapter is the machinery behind them:

  • Hurricanes need a warm ocean. A hurricane is a heat engine fueled by warm seawater, roughly 26.5 C (about 80 F) or warmer, plus low wind shear so the storm can stack straight up, plus enough distance from the equator for the Coriolis effect to start the spin. Cut off the warm water and the engine stalls (Storm 1).
  • Floods are too much water too fast. Whether the water falls as rain the ground cannot soak up, streams in along an atmospheric river, or is pushed ashore as storm surge, a flood is water arriving faster than the land can carry it away. Soak steep ground long enough and it slides downhill as a mudslide (Storm 2).
  • Winter storms are moisture plus a below-freezing profile. The same precipitation falls as snow, sleet, or freezing rain depending on the stack of warm and cold layers the drop passes through, not the temperature at the ground alone (Storm 3).

This chapter also covers two hazards that ride on heavy rain: atmospheric rivers, the corridors of water vapor that stream in from the tropics and unload prolonged rain at a coast, and mudslides, the downhill collapse of rain-soaked ground. Under the surface, each system is just extreme machinery you already met. A hurricane is a low-pressure system (Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones) turned into a giant heat engine over warm water. A flood is precipitation (Chapter 11: Precipitation) plus water that has nowhere to go. A winter storm is that same precipitation, sorted by the vertical temperature profile into snow, ice, or rain.

The mechanisms

Hurricanes: a warm-ocean heat engine

A hurricane (also called a tropical cyclone) is a large spinning low-pressure storm that forms over warm tropical oceans. Its fuel is warm ocean water. The water has to be roughly 26.5 C (about 80 F) or warmer, and warm to some depth, not just a thin skin on top. Here is the chain. Warm seawater evaporates easily, so the air just above the ocean becomes very warm and very humid. That humid air carries a lot of latent heat (the hidden heat that went into evaporating the water, from Chapter 3: Heat on the Move). When that air spirals in and rises, its vapor condenses into clouds and rain, and condensation releases that latent heat high in the storm. The released heat warms the core, which makes the air there lighter, which lowers the surface pressure even more, which pulls in even more warm humid air. The storm feeds itself.

Because the released heat makes the center of the storm warmer than its surroundings, a hurricane is called a warm-core low. This is the key contrast with the mid-latitude cyclones from Chapter 14: Pressure Systems and Mid-latitude Cyclones. Those are cold-core lows: they get their energy from the temperature contrast between warm and cold air masses along a front, not from warm ocean water. Same word, "cyclone," but two different engines. Like every low in the Northern Hemisphere, a hurricane's winds spiral inward and turn counterclockwise (that is cyclonic flow, from Chapter 14).

Two more ingredients. Warm water is necessary but not enough. A hurricane also needs low wind shear. Wind shear is a change in wind speed or direction with height, the same idea that tilted the supercell updraft in Chapter 15: Severe Weather I. For an ordinary thunderstorm some shear helps; for a hurricane it hurts. Strong shear leans the storm over and blows the warm released heat away from the core, so the tall stack of clouds cannot line up and the engine never organizes. A hurricane also needs the Coriolis effect (Chapter 6: Wind and the Coriolis Effect) to start the rotation, and Coriolis is zero right at the equator. That is why hurricanes form a few degrees of latitude away from the equator, never on it. The full recipe is warm ocean water, plenty of humidity, low wind shear, and enough distance from the equator.

Structure. A mature hurricane has three parts. In the middle is the eye: a calm, clear column where air is gently sinking, and where the pressure is lowest (the central pressure can drop below 920 hPa, far below the roughly 1013 hPa of the calm Kansas City warm sector). Around the eye is the eyewall: a ring of the tallest clouds and the strongest, most violent winds, where the warm humid air rises fastest. Spiraling outward from the eyewall are the rain bands: long curved arms of showers and gusty wind.

Rating. Hurricanes are rated on the Saffir-Simpson scale, categories 1 to 5, by their peak sustained wind speed. Do not confuse this with the tornado scale. Tornadoes use the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, 0 to 5, which is judged from the damage a tornado leaves behind. They are different phenomena at completely different sizes: a hurricane is hundreds of kilometers wide and lasts for days, while a tornado is usually only a few hundred meters wide and lasts minutes.

Why it dies over land or cold water. Cut off the warm ocean and you cut off the fuel. When a hurricane moves over land or over cold water, the supply of warm humid air stops, and friction over rough land slows the wind. The storm weakens fast. This is also why Kansas City, deep in the middle of the continent, never takes a direct hurricane hit. What can reach Kansas City is a hurricane's leftovers: a weakened, rained-out storm whose moisture still produces heavy flooding rain.

Floods: water with nowhere to go

A flood is simply water covering land that is usually dry. There are three common kinds, and they run on different clocks.

  • Flash flood. Fast and local. Intense rain falls on ground that cannot absorb it, either because the soil is already saturated from earlier rain, or because the ground is paved city surfaces. The water piles up in minutes to hours. Flash floods are dangerous exactly because they are so sudden.
  • River flood. Slow and large scale. After days of rain or a big snowmelt over a whole river basin, the rivers rise past their banks and spill over. This can take days to build and days to drain.
  • Storm surge. This one comes with a hurricane. The storm's powerful winds push a mound of seawater ahead of it and pile it against the coast, raising the sea far above its normal level and flooding the shore. Storm surge is one of the deadliest parts of a hurricane, often more dangerous than the wind itself.

Atmospheric rivers: rivers of vapor in the sky

An atmospheric river is a long, narrow corridor of concentrated water vapor in the lower atmosphere that carries a huge amount of moisture from the tropics toward the middle latitudes. It is not liquid water and it is not an actual river. It is a band of very humid air, typically a few hundred kilometers wide but well over a thousand kilometers long, streaming like a river of vapor across the sky. Warm tropical air holds a great deal of water vapor (Chapter 7: Humidity), and an atmospheric river is the conveyor belt that moves that vapor toward the poles. A strong one can carry more water, in vapor form, than the flow of a large surface river.

The trouble comes at landfall. When the corridor reaches a coast and the moist air is forced to rise over coastal mountains, it cools, its vapor condenses, and it rains (the same rising-and-cooling chain from Chapter 11: Precipitation). Because the corridor keeps feeding in fresh tropical moisture for hours or days, the rain (or mountain snow) is both heavy and prolonged. The US West Coast, from California to the Pacific Northwest, is a frequent target. One familiar atmospheric river that draws its moisture from the tropical Pacific near Hawaii is nicknamed the "Pineapple Express." Atmospheric rivers are a leading cause of West Coast flooding, and because they soak the ground for so long, they are also a major trigger for the mudslides in the next section. They are not all bad news: they also deliver much of the West's rain and mountain snowpack, so the region's water supply depends on them too.

Mudslides: when rain-soaked ground gives way

A landslide is the downhill movement of soil, rock, and debris under gravity. A mudslide (also called a debris flow) is a fast-moving, water-saturated flow of mud, soil, and rock that rushes down a slope. The trigger is water. Heavy or prolonged rain, often from an atmospheric river, or a fast snowmelt, soaks into the ground until water fills the tiny spaces between the soil grains. That water pushes the grains apart and cuts the friction that was holding the slope together, so the soil loses its grip. Once the saturated mass can no longer resist gravity, it slides.

Three things make a slope far more likely to fail:

  • Steep slopes. The steeper the ground, the more of gravity's pull acts along the slope instead of straight into it, so saturated soil slides more easily.
  • Wildfire-burned ground. After a fire the plant roots that anchored the soil are gone, and burned soil can repel water instead of absorbing it. Rain then runs off the surface and picks up loose ash and debris, turning readily into a debris flow.
  • Deforestation. Removing trees and other plants strips out the roots that hold soil in place and the vegetation that would have soaked up some of the rain, leaving the bare slope weaker and wetter.

A mudslide is the ground-failure cousin of a flood. The same prolonged rain that fills a river basin can also collapse a steep hillside, which is why a big atmospheric river on the West Coast often brings flooding and mudslides at the same time.

Winter storms: the temperature profile decides everything

In winter the big question is not "will it precipitate" but "what will it fall as." The answer depends on the vertical temperature profile: the pattern of warm and cold layers the falling drop passes through on its way down. This is exactly the idea from Chapter 11: Precipitation. Three winter storm types matter.

  • Blizzard. A blizzard is defined by wind and visibility, not by how much snow falls. It needs strong wind (about 35 mph, roughly 56 km/h, or more) plus falling or blowing snow that cuts visibility very low (under about a quarter mile, roughly 400 m) for at least three hours. You can have a blizzard with little new snow if wind whips the old snow around.
  • Nor'easter. A strong coastal low-pressure system that tracks up the eastern seaboard of the United States. Its winds blow from the northeast (that is where the name comes from), dragging cold air and ocean moisture together to dump heavy snow or rain and batter the coast. It is a mid-latitude cyclone (Chapter 14) that happens to sit right off the Northeast coast.
  • Ice storm. Caused by freezing rain. Freezing rain happens when there is a warm layer above freezing sitting on top of a thin cold layer near the ground. Snow forms up high, melts into liquid rain in the warm layer, then falls through a cold surface layer that is too thin to refreeze it. The drop stays liquid until it hits a below-freezing surface, where it freezes on contact into a coat of clear ice. Enough of that ice brings down tree limbs and power lines.
The winter rule to remember: snow, sleet, and freezing rain differ by the warm and cold layers the drop falls through, not just by the temperature at the ground. Hail is a completely separate thing: it is a warm-season thunderstorm phenomenon, built by strong updrafts (Chapter 15), not a winter event.

Worked examples

Example 1. Follow a hurricane from birth to landfall. A cluster of thunderstorms drifts over the Gulf of Mexico, where the sea surface is 29 C. Over several days it organizes into a spinning storm with a clear eye, and its sustained winds reach 120 mph. It then makes landfall on the coast. Name the storm's category, label its three parts, and explain what happens to it after landfall.

The category. Sustained winds of 120 mph fall in the 111 to 129 mph band, so this is a Saffir-Simpson Category 3 hurricane, which counts as a major hurricane.

Why it strengthened over the Gulf. The 29 C water is well above the roughly 26.5 C threshold, so evaporation loads the low-level air with vapor and latent heat. As that air spirals in and rises in the eyewall, condensation releases the latent heat, warms the core, and drops the central pressure. Lower pressure pulls in more warm humid air, and the loop repeats. That feedback is what spun a loose cluster of storms into an organized Category 3.

The three parts. The calm clear center is the eye (lowest pressure, gently sinking air). The ring of tall clouds and fiercest winds around it is the eyewall. The curved arms of showers reaching outward are the spiral rain bands. See the cross-section in Storm 1 above.

After landfall. Over land the warm ocean fuel is gone and friction slows the wind, so the storm weakens quickly from a Category 3 toward a tropical storm and then a rainy low. The wind threat fades first; the heavy rain and inland flooding can last much longer.

Aha: a hurricane is a machine that turns warm ocean water into wind. Take away the warm water (landfall, or cold water) and the machine stalls. Everything about the storm, its low pressure, its eyewall winds, its whole life span, traces back to that one fuel source.

Example 2. Three floods from one hurricane. The Category 3 storm from Example 1 comes ashore at high tide. Its winds pile up a wall of seawater 3 m high, and the high tide itself is 0.5 m above the normal low-water mark. Its rain bands drop 25 cm of rain in a few hours over a paved coastal city, and over the next three days that rain drains into a river basin upstream. Describe the three separate flood threats and find how high the coastal water rises.

Storm surge (coast, right away). The wind-driven surge stacks on top of the tide. Total height above the normal low-water mark is \(3 + 0.5 = 3.5\) m of water pushed over the shore. That is why landfall timing matters: at high tide the surge rides even higher. Storm surge is often the deadliest part of a hurricane.

Flash flood (city, within hours). 25 cm of rain in a few hours on paved ground cannot soak in, so it runs off fast and ponds in streets and low spots. This is a flash flood: sudden and local.

River flood (upstream, days later). All that rain eventually drains into the rivers of the basin. Days after the storm has gone, the rivers crest above their banks and flood far inland. Slow and large scale.

Aha: one hurricane can flood you three different ways on three different clocks: surge in the first hours at the coast, flash flooding during the rain, and river flooding days later inland. The wind gets the headlines, but water is usually the bigger killer.

Example 3. An atmospheric river hits the California coast. A long, narrow corridor of very humid air streams in from the tropical Pacific near Hawaii and makes landfall on the California coast, where the land rises quickly into a coastal mountain range. It rains steadily for two days and drops 30 cm of rain on a steep hillside whose plant cover burned away in a wildfire the previous summer. Explain what an atmospheric river is, why the rain is so heavy and so long-lasting here, and why this sets up a mudslide.

What it is. The corridor of humid air is an atmospheric river: a narrow band, a few hundred kilometers wide but well over a thousand long, carrying a huge load of water vapor from the tropics toward the mid-latitudes. Because it draws its moisture from the tropical Pacific near Hawaii, this kind is nicknamed the "Pineapple Express."

Why the rain is heavy and prolonged. At the coast the moist air is forced up over the mountains. Rising air cools, its vapor condenses, and it rains (the rising-and-cooling chain from Chapter 11: Precipitation). Two things make it extreme. First, the air is loaded with tropical moisture, so there is a great deal of vapor to wring out. Second, the corridor keeps streaming fresh moist air in for two full days, so the rain does not let up. That is how a single system drops 30 cm of rain.

Why it becomes a mudslide. Two days of rain soak the hillside until water fills the spaces between the soil grains and cuts the friction holding the slope together. The slope is steep, so gravity pulls the saturated soil downhill. And the wildfire stripped away the roots that used to anchor the soil, while the burned ground sheds water instead of soaking it in, so runoff picks up loose debris. Saturated, steep, and burned bare, the hillside gives way as a mudslide (a debris flow). See the sketch in Storm 2 above.

Aha: an atmospheric river is a flood and a landslide in one. The same prolonged soaking that fills the rivers also collapses the hillsides, and the steepest, most recently burned ground fails first.

Example 4. An ice storm over Kansas City. A winter storm approaches Kansas City. A weather balloon shows the temperature is below freezing high up, then a layer around 3 C (above freezing) in the middle, then a thin layer near the ground around minus 2 C. What kind of precipitation reaches the ground, and why? What would change it to sleet, or to plain snow?

Trace one drop down. High up, below freezing, so snow forms. In the middle warm layer at 3 C, the snowflake melts completely into a liquid raindrop. In the thin cold surface layer at minus 2 C, the drop chills below freezing but the layer is too thin to refreeze it, so it stays liquid (this is called supercooled). The moment it lands on a cold surface, it freezes into clear ice. That is freezing rain, and enough of it is an ice storm.

What would change it. Make that cold surface layer deep instead of thin, and the drop has time to refreeze in the air into a little ice pellet before it lands. That is sleet. Take the warm middle layer away entirely, so it is below freezing the whole way down, and the snowflake never melts. That is plain snow. And if it were above freezing the whole way down, it would just be cold rain.

Aha: the same storm can drop snow, sleet, freezing rain, or rain over towns only a few miles apart. What differs is the stack of warm and cold layers, not the storm. Read the vertical temperature profile and you can predict the precipitation type.

Common pitfalls

  • Using the wrong storm scale. Hurricanes use the Saffir-Simpson scale, 1 to 5, from wind speed. Tornadoes use the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, 0 to 5, from damage. Different storms, different scales, wildly different sizes. Never rate a hurricane in EF or a tornado in categories.
  • Thinking a hurricane runs on wind or on land. The fuel is warm ocean water and the latent heat in the humid air above it. That is why hurricanes form over tropical oceans and weaken over land or cold water. No warm water, no hurricane.
  • Thinking warm water alone makes a hurricane. Warm sea (about 26.5 C or warmer) is necessary but not enough. The storm also needs low wind shear so its clouds stack straight up, and enough distance from the equator for the Coriolis effect to start the spin. Strong shear tears a would-be hurricane apart, which is the opposite of what shear does to a supercell (Chapter 15).
  • Confusing a hurricane with a mid-latitude cyclone. A hurricane is a warm-core low fed by ocean heat. A mid-latitude cyclone (Chapter 14) is a cold-core low fed by the clash of warm and cold air masses along a front. Both are cyclones (lows with inward, counterclockwise flow in the Northern Hemisphere), but the engines are different.
  • Thinking an atmospheric river is a band of rain or a real river. It is a corridor of water vapor high in the air. The heavy rain happens only where that moist air is forced to rise and cool, usually over coastal mountains at landfall.
  • Thinking a mudslide needs a slope to simply be wet. The soil has to become saturated, so the water fills the pore spaces and cuts the friction holding the slope together. Steep ground, wildfire-burned ground, and cleared ground all fail sooner because they hold less water and have fewer roots.
  • Judging winter precipitation by the surface temperature alone. Snow, sleet, and freezing rain are set by the whole vertical temperature profile, the stack of warm and cold layers, not just the reading at the ground. Two towns with the same surface temperature can get different precipitation.
  • Calling a blizzard "a lot of snow." A blizzard is defined by strong wind plus low visibility for a few hours. You can have a blizzard with little new snow if wind is blowing the old snow around.
  • Filing hail under winter. Hail is a warm-season thunderstorm phenomenon built by strong updrafts (Chapter 15), not a winter storm.

Practice

P1. Why do hurricanes need warm ocean water to form, and why do they weaken so fast once they move over land?

Answer

The answer. A hurricane runs on the heat carried by water vapor evaporated from warm ocean water (roughly 26.5 C or warmer). Over warm water it keeps getting fuel and can strengthen. Over land or cold water the fuel is cut off and friction slows the wind, so it weakens fast.

Why it works: warm seawater evaporates easily, so the air just above it becomes very warm and very humid, loaded with latent heat. When that air spirals in and rises, the vapor condenses and releases the latent heat high in the storm. The released heat warms the core, lowers the surface pressure, and pulls in still more warm humid air, which is a self-feeding loop. The moment the storm leaves the warm water, that inflow of fresh warm humid air stops, the loop breaks, and rough land also drags on the wind. So the same fuel line that builds the storm is what starves it at landfall.

Pattern, follow the energy source: for any weather system, ask what its energy source is and what happens when that source is removed. A hurricane's source is warm-ocean heat, so it lives over warm seas and dies without them. Contrast this with a mid-latitude cyclone (Chapter 14), whose energy source is the temperature contrast between air masses along a front, so it can live over land. Same "cyclone" label, different fuel, different life story.

P2. One storm has sustained winds of 100 mph, is about 500 km wide, and lasts for days over the ocean. Another is a narrow spinning column about 200 m wide that tears across Oklahoma for ten minutes. Which scale rates each, and what would the first storm's rating be?

AnswerThe wide, long-lived ocean storm is a hurricane, rated on the Saffir-Simpson scale (1 to 5) by wind speed; 100 mph falls in the 96 to 110 mph band, so it is a Category 2. The narrow short-lived land column is a tornado, rated on the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale (0 to 5) from the damage it leaves behind.

P3. A winter sounding over Kansas City reads: below freezing high up, a layer at about 2 C (above freezing) in the middle, and a thin layer at about minus 1 C right at the surface. What precipitation type reaches the ground, and how would you change the profile to get sleet instead?

Answer

The answer. This profile gives freezing rain (an ice storm setup). To get sleet instead, make the cold surface layer deep rather than thin.

Why it works: trace one drop. It forms as snow in the cold air aloft. It melts into a liquid raindrop in the warm middle layer at 2 C. Then it enters the thin surface layer at minus 1 C. Because that cold layer is thin, the drop does not have time to refreeze in the air, so it lands still liquid and freezes on contact into clear ice. That is freezing rain. If instead the cold surface layer were deep, the drop would spend long enough in below-freezing air to refreeze into a small ice pellet before landing, which is sleet.

Pattern, read the profile top down: for any winter precipitation question, walk one drop from the cloud to the ground and track melting and refreezing layer by layer. Cold all the way is snow. A warm layer then a deep cold layer is sleet. A warm layer then only a thin cold layer is freezing rain. Warm all the way is plain rain. The surface temperature alone is never enough; it is the whole stack that decides.

P4. An atmospheric river makes landfall on a steep, mountainous coast and rains hard for two days. Why does one such system cause both flooding and mudslides, and why does a recently burned, steep hillside fail before gentler, forested ground does?

Answer

The answer. The atmospheric river forces heavy, prolonged rain by lifting moist tropical air up over the coastal mountains. That rain does two things at once: it runs off and fills rivers and low ground (a flood), and it soaks into slopes until they lose their grip and slide (a mudslide). A steep, burned hillside fails first because it has lost its anchoring roots, its burned soil sheds water instead of absorbing it, and its steepness gives gravity more pull along the slope.

Why it works: follow the water. When rain lands, some runs across the surface and some soaks in. The water that runs off collects in streets, low spots, and river basins, which is flooding. The water that soaks in fills the pore spaces between soil grains, pushes the grains apart, and cuts the friction that held the slope together, so the saturated mass slides downhill. Two days of atmospheric-river rain is enough to do both. A burned slope has no roots to hold the soil and a water-repelling surface, so more rain runs off carrying loose debris and the little that soaks in weakens the ground fast. A steep slope adds more of gravity's pull along the surface, so it needs less water to fail.

Pattern, follow the water into the ground, not just across it: rain that runs off causes floods, and rain that soaks in causes slope failure, so one long soaking storm can do both. Ask two questions of any heavy-rain event: where does the runoff collect (flood risk), and which slopes are steep, burned, or cleared (mudslide risk). Contrast this with a hurricane's storm surge, which is seawater pushed ashore by wind, not freshwater soaking in from above; different water, different mechanism, same theme that water is usually the bigger killer.

17. Climate: Zones, Oceans, ENSO and Change

Weather is what you get on a single day. Climate is the long-run average, and the range, of a whole place. This chapter steps all the way back to the globe.

Prerequisites: Chapter 2: Energy from the Sun · Chapter 13: Global Circulation and Jet Streams
This chapter: Climate: Zones, Oceans, ENSO and Change
Unlocks: the horizon of the course, where classroom weather meets real climate science and paleoclimatology (the landmark below).

Start with three real cases

For the whole course so far we tracked weather: the state of the air over Kansas City on a given day. On Day 1 of our cold-front story the barometer read about 1013 hPa, warm and humid. On Day 2 a front dropped it to about 1002 hPa with thunderstorms. On Day 3 it climbed to about 1020 hPa, cooler and drier. In this chapter we step all the way back to the whole planet and ask a different question: not what happens on one day, but what a place is like on average, year after year. Before any framework, look at three concrete things that any records will show you. All the theory in this chapter exists to explain them.

Case 1: two cities at the same latitude, two different climates

San Francisco sits on the Pacific coast near 38 degrees north. Kansas City sits inland near 39 degrees north. They are at almost the same latitude, so the Sun climbs to nearly the same height in their skies and they receive nearly the same sunlight. Their yearly average temperatures are close too, both near 13 to 14 degrees Celsius (C). Yet their climates feel nothing alike. Here are representative monthly averages.

  • San Francisco: coldest month about 11 C, warmest month about 17 C. The whole year spans only about 6 C.
  • Kansas City: coldest month about 1 C below zero, warmest month about 27 C. The year spans about 28 C.

Same latitude, same sunlight, almost the same yearly average, and yet one city barely changes across the seasons while the other swings from freezing winters to hot summers. Latitude alone clearly does not decide a climate.

Case 2: an El Nino year and a La Nina year

Now watch the tropical Pacific Ocean, the biggest single region of water on the planet. Most years the trade winds blow steadily from east to west there, piling warm surface water toward Indonesia and Australia in the west and letting cold deep water rise off South America in the east. But every few years the pattern flips into one of two moods, and when it does, weather changes on the far side of the world.

  • An El Nino year: the trade winds weaken, and the pool of warm water slides back east across the Pacific. The eastern tropical Pacific can run 1 to 3 C warmer than normal. The heavy tropical rain follows the warm water east. Typical result: drought in Indonesia and Australia, extra rain and storms across parts of the Americas, and a warmer global average for that year.
  • A La Nina year: the trade winds strengthen, and even more warm water heaps up in the west while cold upwelling in the east grows stronger. Roughly the mirror image: wetter in Indonesia and Australia, drier across the southern United States.

Nothing about Kansas City's latitude changed between those two years. The same city can get a wet, stormy season in one and a dry one in the next, because one ocean thousands of kilometers away shifted its warm water east or west.

Case 3: the carbon dioxide and temperature record

Finally, look at a single measurement taken continuously since 1958 at Mauna Loa in Hawaii, and reconstructed further back from air bubbles trapped in ice. It is the amount of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the air, counted in parts per million (ppm). Before the industrial era, around 1850, it held near 280 ppm. Today it is about 420 ppm and still climbing. Over the same stretch, Earth's average surface temperature has risen about 1.2 C. Two numbers, both rising together.

Name the pattern

Step back from the three cases and one picture emerges. Climate is the long-run average, and the range, of weather at a place, taken over about 30 years. Three layers set it:

  • The baseline is set by latitude, oceans, and terrain. Case 1 shows it: latitude fixes the sunlight, but the ocean, the land, and the shape of the ground decide the actual climate. That is why two cities on the same latitude line can differ so much.
  • ENSO reshuffles it year to year. Case 2 shows it: even with the baseline fixed, the tropical Pacific tips between El Nino and La Nina every few years and swings rainfall and storms around the globe.
  • Greenhouse gases shift the whole baseline. Case 3 shows it: rising CO2 traps more of Earth's outgoing heat, so the entire baseline drifts warmer over time.

The rest of the chapter builds the framework behind each layer: what climate really means, the zones it forms, the controls that set the baseline, the oceans and their currents, the ENSO seesaw, and the greenhouse mechanism with its evidence.

The framework

Weather versus climate

Start with the distinction the three cases lean on. All through this guide we followed weather: the state of the air at Kansas City on a given day, like the 1013 then 1002 then 1020 hPa march of our cold-front story. That day-to-day change is weather.

Climate is different. Climate is the long-run pattern of weather at a place, usually the average and the spread taken over about 30 years. Kansas City's climate is "cold snowy winters, hot muggy summers, thunderstorms in spring." Any one day can surprise you, but the climate tells you what to pack for the season. A short slogan captures it: climate is what you expect, weather is what you get.

Notice that climate is not just the average. It is also the variability, the normal range from the coldest nights to the hottest afternoons, and how often storms come through. That is exactly what Case 1 measured: San Francisco and Kansas City share a yearly average but have completely different ranges, so they have completely different climates.

Climate zones: the Koppen system

If you circle the whole globe you find the same broad kinds of climate showing up again and again. The most common way to sort them is the Koppen climate classification, which groups places by their temperature and their precipitation through the year. It uses five main letters:

  • A, tropical: hot all year (every month averages 18 degrees Celsius (C) or warmer) with plenty of rain. Rainforests and jungles.
  • B, dry: more water evaporates than falls as rain, so the land is arid. Deserts and dry grasslands.
  • C, temperate: mild winters (the coldest month stays above 0 C) and warm summers. Much of the southeastern United States and western Europe.
  • D, continental: cold winters (the coldest month drops below 0 C) but warm summers (the warmest month tops 10 C). The interior of North America and Asia.
  • E, polar: cold all year (even the warmest month stays below 10 C). Tundra and ice caps.

Roughly, these zones stack up by latitude, hottest and wettest near the equator and coldest at the poles, because latitude sets how directly the Sun's energy strikes the ground (see Chapter 2). The band map below shows the simple picture.

The full Koppen system goes further, adding a second and third letter for the seasonal rain pattern and the summer heat (for example Cfa for a humid subtropical climate, or BWh for a hot desert), which sorts the world into about thirty types. You do not need those subtypes here. Just know the five main letters and that a finer detailed Koppen classification exists as the next step.

The controls that set a place's climate

Latitude alone does not finish the job. Two cities on the same latitude line can have very different climates. Six main controls decide what a place actually gets:

  • Latitude: how directly sunlight hits, which sets how much energy arrives.
  • Altitude (elevation): higher up is colder, because the air thins and cools with height. A mountaintop on the equator can be freezing.
  • Distance from water: places near a big ocean or lake have milder, steadier temperatures. Places deep inland swing hard between hot summers and cold winters.
  • Ocean currents: rivers of seawater carry heat around the globe and warm or cool the coasts they pass.
  • Prevailing winds: the steady winds from Chapter 13 carry ocean air, or dry inland air, over the land.
  • Mountains: a mountain range forces air up and wrings out rain on the windward side, leaving a dry "rain shadow" on the far side.

Oceans: the planet's giant heat reservoir

Water is slow to heat up and slow to cool down. It can soak up an enormous amount of energy while barely changing temperature. That makes the oceans a huge heat reservoir: they store the Sun's energy and release it gradually. Two big effects follow.

First, the ocean moderates the climate of nearby land. A coast warms less in summer and cools less in winter than an inland spot at the same latitude, so its temperature range is smaller. That is the whole story behind Case 1: San Francisco's small range comes from the Pacific next door. Second, ocean currents move heat across the whole globe. The Gulf Stream, for example, is a warm current that carries tropical water up the eastern coast of the United States and then across the Atlantic toward Europe. It is a big reason that western Europe stays milder in winter than other places at the same far-north latitude.

Those surface currents are the top of a larger loop. Differences in water temperature and saltiness drive a slow, deep, planet-wide circulation that links all the oceans and takes centuries to complete. It is named the thermohaline circulation (sometimes called the global ocean conveyor belt), from "thermo" for heat and "haline" for salt. You only need the name here: it is the deep engine that keeps redistributing heat under the surface currents.

ENSO: the tropical Pacific seesaw

The ocean and the air are one connected machine, and nowhere is that clearer than in the tropical Pacific. Its back-and-forth swing is called ENSO, short for the El Nino-Southern Oscillation. Here is the story in plain terms.

Normally the trade winds blow from east to west across the tropical Pacific. They pile warm surface water in the western Pacific, near Indonesia and Australia, and let cold deep water rise to the surface off South America. That is the neutral, ordinary state.

  • El Nino: the trade winds weaken. The pile of warm water slides back east toward South America. The zone of heavy tropical rain shifts east with it. This nudges the whole planet's weather, often bringing drought to Australia and Indonesia and extra rain to parts of the Americas.
  • La Nina: the trade winds strengthen. Even more warm water heaps up in the west and the cold upwelling in the east grows stronger. The pattern is roughly the opposite of El Nino.

ENSO is a seesaw, tipping every few years between El Nino, neutral, and La Nina. Because the tropical Pacific is so large, that one seesaw shifts rainfall, droughts, and storm tracks in far-off places, an example of how the ocean and atmosphere steer weather worldwide.

The greenhouse effect and climate change

Why is Earth warm enough to live on at all? The answer is the greenhouse effect, and it is worth getting exactly right because it is easy to describe backward.

Sunlight arrives mostly as shortwave radiation. It passes almost straight through the atmosphere and warms the ground. The warmed ground then glows, but not with visible light. It sends energy back upward as longwave radiation, which is infrared, the same invisible heat you feel from a warm road at night.

Here is the key step. Certain gases, called greenhouse gases, absorb this OUTGOING infrared and re-emit it in all directions, including back down toward the surface. They do not block the incoming sunlight. They trap the outgoing heat on its way out. The main greenhouse gases are water vapor (the most abundant), carbon dioxide (CO2), and methane. This trapping keeps the surface far warmer than bare sunlight alone would.

The greenhouse effect is natural and necessary. Without it, calculations put Earth's average surface temperature at about 18 C below zero, written \(-18\ ^\circ\text{C}\), a frozen planet. The real average is about \(+15\ ^\circ\text{C}\). So the natural greenhouse effect is worth roughly \(15-(-18)=33\) degrees Celsius of warming. The trouble is that burning coal, oil, and gas adds extra CO2 to the air. More CO2 traps more outgoing infrared, which strengthens the effect and warms the planet further. That extra warming is what people mean by climate change. Most of the extra heat so far has gone into the oceans, that giant reservoir again.

This is where Case 3 becomes the evidence. That rising curve, from about 280 ppm before the industrial era to about 420 ppm today, is the extra CO2 from burning fuels, measured directly. The mechanism predicts that adding it should trap more outgoing infrared and warm the surface, and the temperature record shows about 1.2 C of warming over the same time. The measured cause and the measured effect line up, which is why the greenhouse mechanism is not just a theory but a well-checked explanation of the record.

Keep these two separate. The greenhouse effect (warming from CO2 and other gases trapping outgoing infrared) is a DIFFERENT problem from the ozone hole. The ozone hole is about a thin ozone layer high in the atmosphere being damaged by other chemicals, which lets in more harmful ultraviolet from the Sun. One is about trapping heat going out; the other is about a shield for radiation coming in. They are often mixed up, but they are not the same thing.

Worked examples

Example 1. Classify these two places with the Koppen letters. City X: every month averages between 25 and 28 C, with heavy rain all year. City Y: warmest month 26 C, coldest month 6 C below zero, rain spread through the year.

City X. Start with temperature. Every month is 18 C or warmer, so it is hot all year. It also has plenty of rain. That is the tropical box.

Answer for X: A, tropical.

City Y. Its coldest month is 6 C below zero, which is under 0 C, so the winter is truly cold, not just mild. That rules out C. But its warmest month, 26 C, is well above 10 C, so it still has a real summer. Cold winter plus warm summer is the continental box.

Answer for Y: D, continental. (This is close to Kansas City's own climate. Kansas City sits right near the line between C and D, because its coldest month hovers around the 0 C mark.)

Aha: read the temperature first, coldest month then warmest month, to pick the letter, then check that the precipitation fits. A above 18 all year, C mild winter (above 0), D cold winter but warm summer, E cold even in summer (below 10).

Example 2. San Francisco sits on the Pacific coast near 38 degrees north. Its summers are cool and its winters are mild, so the temperature barely changes across the year. Kansas City sits near 39 degrees north, almost the same latitude, but its summers are hot and its winters are cold. Why is the coastal city so much milder even though the Sun angle is nearly the same?

The two cities get almost the same sunlight, so latitude is not the difference. The difference is the control called distance from water, helped by the prevailing winds. San Francisco sits right next to the Pacific Ocean. Water is a giant heat reservoir. It warms slowly in summer and cools slowly in winter, so it never lets the nearby air get very hot or very cold. The steady west winds off the Pacific carry that mild ocean air onshore over the city all year.

Kansas City sits far from any ocean, deep in the continent. The land heats up fast under the summer Sun and cools off fast in winter, with no ocean to steady it. So its temperature swings hard between the seasons.

Answer: the ocean moderates San Francisco's climate, giving it a small yearly temperature range. Kansas City, far inland, has a large range. Same latitude, very different climates, because distance from water and the prevailing winds differ.

Aha: latitude sets the Sun angle, but water sets the swing. Near a big ocean the climate is steady and mild. Far inland it is extreme. This inland extremeness even has a name, continentality.

Example 3. A student says, "Greenhouse gases warm the Earth by blocking sunlight from escaping, like a blanket over the Sun." What is wrong with this, and what is the correct mechanism?

What is wrong. Greenhouse gases do not block incoming sunlight, and the warming is not about sunlight being unable to escape. Sunlight comes IN as shortwave radiation and mostly passes straight through the air to the ground.

The correct mechanism, step by step.

  • Sunlight (shortwave) passes through the atmosphere and warms the surface.
  • The warmed surface sends energy back upward as infrared (longwave) radiation.
  • Greenhouse gases (CO2, water vapor, methane) absorb some of this OUTGOING infrared.
  • They re-emit it in all directions, including back down, which keeps the surface warmer.

Answer: the effect works on the OUTGOING infrared, not the incoming sunlight. Without it Earth's average surface would sit near \(-18\ ^\circ\text{C}\); with it, near \(+15\ ^\circ\text{C}\). Adding CO2 from burning fuels traps more outgoing infrared and warms the planet more.

Aha: the gas is a one-way toll gate on heat going OUT, not a curtain over the Sun. Get the direction right, infrared outbound, and the whole idea falls into place. And remember it is a separate issue from the ozone hole.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: mixing up weather and climate. One cold snowy week does not undo a warming climate, and one hot day does not prove it. Weather is a single day or week. Climate is the long-run average and range over about 30 years. Judge climate from the long record, not from today's sky.
Pitfall 2: describing the greenhouse effect backward. Greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit the OUTGOING infrared (longwave) that the surface gives off. They do NOT block the incoming sunlight. "Rising heat, trapped on the way out" is the right picture.
Pitfall 3: confusing the greenhouse effect with the ozone hole. Extra CO2 trapping outgoing heat (warming) is a different problem from ozone being damaged high up (which lets in more ultraviolet). Different gases, different layers, different problems.
Pitfall 4: thinking latitude alone sets climate. Two cities on the same latitude can differ hugely because of altitude, distance from water, ocean currents, winds, and mountains. Latitude is only the first control.

All course you followed a representative three-day cold-front episode over Kansas City: a warm, humid Day 1 under a departing high (about 1013 hPa), a cold front crashing through on Day 2 (pressure bottoming near 1002 hPa), and a cool, clear Day 3 behind the front under a new high (about 1020 hPa). Case 1: decode a station model

Below is the plot for Kansas City on Day 1 of the episode, the warm, humid day under the departing high. It is called a station model. It packs six measurements into one wordless cluster around a circle. Read the temperature, dew point, sea-level pressure, sky cover, and wind off it.

Temperature (upper left): 75 F. A warm afternoon.

Dew point (lower left): 68 F. The spread between temperature and dew point is \(75-68 = 7\) degrees, which is small. A small spread means the air is close to saturation, so it is humid. That matches Day 1.

Sea-level pressure (upper right): the code is 132. It is shorthand, so decode it in two moves. First put a decimal point before the last digit: 132 becomes 13.2. Then add a 9 or a 10 in front, whichever gives a normal sea-level value near 1000 hPa. Adding 10 gives \(1013.2\) hPa. Adding 9 would give 913.2 hPa, which is far too low, so 10 is right. A quick rule: if the code is under 500 add a 10, and if it is 500 or more add a 9. So the pressure is 1013 hPa, which is about 29.92 inHg. That is the Day 1 value.

Sky cover (the circle): the circle is open (empty), so the sky is mostly clear.

Wind (the staff and barbs): the staff sticks out toward the bottom of the map, which is south, and a wind is named for where it comes from, so this is a south wind. The staff carries one full barb, and a full barb counts 10 knots (a half barb would be 5 knots, a filled pennant 50 knots), so the wind is 10 knots.

Present weather: there is no weather symbol next to the circle, so nothing is falling. Clear and quiet.

Answer: Kansas City on Day 1 is 75 F with a 68 F dew point (humid), pressure 1013 hPa, mostly clear sky, a south wind at 10 knots, and no precipitation. That is exactly the warm-sector calm the episode describes.

Aha: a station model is a tiny weather report with no words. Every number sits in a fixed spot, so you always know which is which: temperature upper left, dew point lower left, pressure upper right, the circle in the middle for the sky, and the staff for the wind.

Case 2: read a surface map and forecast

Below is the surface map for Day 2, when the cold front reaches Kansas City. Find the wind at Kansas City, say where the wind is strongest, read the front, then make a forecast for Day 3.

The wind at Kansas City. Use the rule from Chapter 6. The push points from high pressure toward low pressure, so it points toward the upper left. As the air starts moving, the Coriolis effect turns it to the right (Northern Hemisphere) until it coasts parallel to the isobars. A quick shortcut: stand with your back to the wind and the low is on your left. For Kansas City on the warm side, that leaves the wind blowing from the south, a warm, moist south wind feeding the front. The map shows exactly that.

Where the wind is strongest. The isobars are packed tightly on the left near the low and spread wide on the right near the high. Packed isobars mean a steep pressure change and a strong wind, so the wind is strongest on the left side, near the low. Near the high on the right the wind is light.

Reading the front. The blue line with triangles is a cold front, and the triangles point southeast, so that is the way it is moving. It is sweeping toward Kansas City. When it arrives, the cold air behind it shoves the warm air up, the pressure bottoms out near 1002 hPa, and Parcel P's thunderstorm fires along the line.

Now forecast Day 3. Do not guess, watch the map move. Mid-latitude highs, lows, and fronts are carried along by the jet stream (Chapter 13), which mostly steers them from west to east across the United States. Slide the whole picture eastward by one day: the front and its storms move off to the east, and the sinking, drying air of the new high (bottom right) settles over Kansas City. So Day 3 clears and cools, the pressure rises toward about 1020 hPa, the wind swings around to the northwest, and the air dries out.

Answer: Kansas City has a strong south wind ahead of an advancing cold front, storms to its west and northwest, and a clear, cooler, drier Day 3 coming as the front slides east and the high builds in. That is exactly the episode's Day 3.

Aha: a surface map is a snapshot you can read like a story, then run forward like a movie. The L tells you where the storms are, the isobar spacing tells you how hard the wind blows, the front symbol tells you what is coming and from which direction, and sliding the whole map east gives you tomorrow.

The pattern

Both cases follow the same three beats, and that is the whole job of forecasting.

  1. Observations. Instruments measure the air at many spots and heights. Each spot's numbers get packed into one wordless station model, so a single symbol carries temperature, dew point, pressure, sky, and wind at once.
  2. Maps and models. Those observations are plotted on a map (isobars, highs, lows, fronts) and fed to computer models, turning a pile of numbers into a picture of where the systems are.
  3. A forecast. You read the map and project the systems forward in time, steered west to east by the jet stream, to say what happens next.

Observations, then maps and models, then a forecast. The rest of this chapter names the tools that do each beat, then shows how a forecast is actually made and why it can only reach so far ahead.

The tools

The instruments

You already met most of these earlier in the course. Here they are together, each one paired with the one thing it measures.

  • Thermometer measures temperature (how hot or cold the air is), in degrees Fahrenheit or Celsius. Met in Chapter 4.
  • Barometer measures air pressure, in hectopascals (hPa, the same as millibars) or inches of mercury (inHg). Met in Chapter 5.
  • Anemometer measures wind speed, usually with little cups that spin faster in stronger wind. Met in Chapter 6.
  • Wind vane measures wind direction, swinging so its arrow points into the wind. A wind is named for the direction it comes FROM, so a south wind blows from the south. Met in Chapter 6.
  • Hygrometer measures humidity (how much water vapor is in the air), which gives you relative humidity and the dew point. Met in Chapter 7.
  • Rain gauge measures precipitation, the depth of rain that has fallen, in millimeters or inches. Met in Chapter 11.
  • Weather balloon and radiosonde. A radiosonde is a small instrument package carried up on a weather balloon. As it rises it radios back a profile, called a sounding, of temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind all the way up through the atmosphere. This is how we see the vertical picture that decides stability (Chapter 9) and precipitation type (Chapter 11).
  • Radar sends out radio pulses and listens for the echo bouncing off raindrops and snowflakes. It shows where precipitation is falling and how heavy it is. Doppler radar also measures how fast the drops are moving toward or away from it, which reveals rotation inside a storm, the warning sign for a tornado (Chapter 15).
  • Satellite looks down from space and photographs clouds over whole continents and oceans, and it tracks large systems like hurricanes long before they reach land. It sees in two main ways. A visible image is reflected sunlight, so it works only in daytime and shows how bright and thick the clouds are. An infrared image reads the heat the cloud tops give off, so it works day and night, and because higher clouds are colder it also tells you how tall the clouds are.

The first six sit at one spot and measure the air right there. The radiosonde reaches up in the vertical, while radar and satellite reach out across the map, so together they fill in the picture between the ground stations.

Reading a weather map

A surface weather map is covered in curved lines and symbols. Here is what each one means.

  • Isobars are lines that connect places with the same air pressure. They are the pressure contours of the map. The key rule from Chapter 6: where isobars are packed close together the pressure changes fast over a short distance, so the wind is strong. Where they are spread far apart the wind is light.
  • Isotherms are lines that connect places with the same temperature. They show you where the warm air and the cold air are, so a front (a boundary between two air masses) usually runs where isotherms bunch up.
  • Highs and lows are the peaks and valleys of the pressure field, marked H and L. A low has air spiraling in and rising, which cools it and makes clouds, so a LOW means clouds and storms. A high has air sinking and spreading out, which dries it, so a HIGH means clear, calm weather. Do not swap these.
  • Fronts are drawn as bold lines with symbols. A cold front is a blue line with filled triangles, and the triangles point the way the front is moving. A warm front is a red line with filled half circles that point the way it moves. A front is always named for the advancing air mass, so a cold front is cold air pushing forward and shoving the warm air out of the way.
  • The station model is the compact plot at each reporting spot. It squeezes six or more measurements into a small cluster around a circle, with no words: temperature upper left, dew point lower left, sea-level pressure (coded) upper right, sky cover as the fraction of the central circle filled, wind as a staff with barbs, and a present-weather symbol just left of the circle. You decoded one in Case 1.

How a forecast is made

There are three simple, named ways to forecast, and a fourth that beats them when weather is on the move.

  • Persistence: guess that tomorrow will be like today. Easy, and often right when the weather is not changing, but it fails badly right at a front, when the weather is changing fast.
  • Analog: find a past day that looked like this one, then predict that tomorrow will do what that past case did next.
  • Climatology: use the long-run average for this place and date. A safe but blurry guess that ignores today's systems entirely.
  • Read the map and project it forward (the strongest): find the highs, lows, and fronts, learn which way the jet stream is steering them (mostly west to east, Chapter 13), and slide them forward in time. This is what you did in Case 2. The professional version of this is run on supercomputers (see the landmarks).

Why forecasts have limits

A forecast for tomorrow is sharp, but a forecast for three weeks out is nearly useless, and no faster computer will fix that. The atmosphere is chaotic: a tiny error in today's measurements grows quickly into a huge error a week or two later. So forecast skill drops as the lead time (how far ahead you are looking) grows, and past about a week even the best forecast fades. This is a hard mathematical limit, not a weak-computer problem. It is named below.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: swapping highs and lows. A LOW is rising air, so it brings clouds and storms. A HIGH is sinking air, so it brings clear, calm skies. Students often say the opposite. On the Day 2 map, the storms are at the L, not the H.
Pitfall 2: misreading the pressure code on a station model. The three digits are tenths of a hectopascal with the leading 9 or 10 dropped. Add a 10 when the code is under 500 and a 9 when it is 500 or more. So 132 is 1013.2 hPa and 020 is 1002.0 hPa, while 850 would be 985.0 hPa, not 850 hPa.
Pitfall 3: reading the wind backward. The wind staff points toward where the wind comes FROM, and a wind is named for that direction. A staff pointing south is a south wind, blowing from the south toward the north. Do not read it as blowing toward the south.
Pitfall 4: trusting persistence through a front. Persistence (tomorrow equals today) works on quiet days but breaks exactly when it matters, at a front, because that is when the weather changes the most. Always check whether a system is about to move through.

★ Landmarks it points toward

The limit is not weak computers, it is the math of chaos itself.

Glossary

Quick definitions, grouped by layer of the concept map.

Foundation

Troposphere
The lowest layer of the atmosphere (about 0 to 12 km) where almost all weather happens; temperature falls with height.
Atmosphere layers
Troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, from the ground up, each defined by how temperature changes with height.
Insolation
Incoming solar radiation reaching Earth; it is greatest where the Sun is high overhead.
Energy budget
The balance between solar energy absorbed and infrared energy radiated back to space; it drives the whole climate system.
Albedo
The fraction of sunlight a surface reflects; ice and clouds are high, oceans and forests low.
Radiation, conduction, convection
The three ways heat moves: electromagnetic waves, direct contact, and fluid motion carrying heat.
Latent heat
Energy stored or released when water changes phase; evaporation stores it, condensation releases it. The atmosphere's hidden fuel.
Axial tilt
Earth's 23.5 degree tilt, which causes the seasons by changing sun angle and day length (not distance from the Sun).

Core weather

Air pressure
The weight of the air column above a point; measured in hPa (mb) or inHg. It falls with altitude.
Pressure-gradient force
The push on air from high toward low pressure; the primary cause of wind.
Coriolis effect
The apparent deflection of moving air due to Earth's rotation: right in the Northern Hemisphere, left in the Southern.
Geostrophic wind
The balanced, isobar-parallel wind found aloft where friction is negligible.
Relative humidity
The water vapor present as a percentage of the maximum the air can hold at that temperature.
Dew point
The temperature to which air must cool to reach saturation; a direct measure of how much moisture it holds.
Saturation
The state of air holding the maximum water vapor possible at its temperature (relative humidity 100%).
Adiabatic process
A temperature change from expansion or compression alone, with no heat added or removed.
Lapse rate
The rate temperature changes with altitude: dry adiabatic about 10 C/km, moist about 6 C/km, environmental is the observed profile.
Stability
Whether a lifted parcel of air keeps rising (unstable) or sinks back (stable), found by comparing it to its surroundings.
Temperature inversion
A layer where temperature rises with height, capping vertical motion and trapping pollution.
Condensation nuclei
Tiny particles on which water vapor condenses to form cloud droplets.
Cloud types
Cumulus (puffy), stratus (layered), cirrus (high and icy); the prefix nimbo- or suffix -nimbus means it rains.
Collision-coalescence / Bergeron process
The two ways cloud droplets grow big enough to fall: warm-rain droplet collisions, and the cold ice-crystal process.

Systems, severe & climate

Air mass
A large body of air with fairly uniform temperature and humidity, labeled by its source region.
Front
The boundary between two air masses (cold, warm, stationary, or occluded), named for the advancing air mass.
Hadley / Ferrel / Polar cells
The three latitude convection loops that organize the planet's surface winds.
Jet stream
A fast, narrow ribbon of upper-level wind that steers mid-latitude storms.
Trade winds / Westerlies
The prevailing surface winds: easterly in the tropics, westerly in the mid-latitudes.
Anticyclone / Cyclone
A high-pressure system (sinking air, clear) and a low-pressure system (rising air, stormy).
Mid-latitude cyclone
A traveling low-pressure storm along the polar front, with warm and cold sectors; it brings much temperate-zone weather.
Supercell
A long-lived thunderstorm with a rotating updraft; the main producer of strong tornadoes.
Tropical cyclone (hurricane)
A warm-core storm powered by warm ocean water, with an eye, an eyewall, and spiral rain bands.
Storm surge
The abnormal rise of seawater pushed ashore by a hurricane's winds; a major flood and casualty source.
Koppen climate zones
A classification (A tropical, B dry, C temperate, D continental, E polar) based on temperature and precipitation.
Ocean currents
Large-scale flows of seawater (such as the Gulf Stream) that move heat and moderate coastal climates.
ENSO (El Nino / La Nina)
The tropical-Pacific ocean and atmosphere oscillation that shifts weather patterns worldwide.
Greenhouse effect
Warming caused by gases such as CO2, water vapor, and methane absorbing and re-emitting Earth's outgoing infrared radiation.
Station model
The compact cluster of symbols on a weather map that encodes temperature, dew point, pressure, wind, sky, and weather at one site.
Isobar / Isotherm
A line connecting points of equal pressure / equal temperature on a map.

★ Landmarks (name-only)

Numerical weather prediction
Forecasting by solving the atmosphere's physics equations on supercomputers.
Chaos / butterfly effect
Extreme sensitivity to starting conditions that limits useful forecasts to roughly one to two weeks.
Ozone layer
The stratospheric ozone that shields Earth from ultraviolet light; thinned by CFCs (the ozone hole), a separate issue from CO2 warming.
Paleoclimatology
Reconstructing past climates from ice cores, tree rings, and sediment records.
